\title[Concerted evolution and unorthodox recombination of human subtelomeres]{Concerted evolution and unorthodox recombination of human subtelomeres}

\author*[1,2]{\fnm{Andrea} \sur{Guarracino}\orcidid{0000-0001-9744-131X}}\email{aguarracino@tgen.org}

\abstract{
Human subtelomeres contain duplicated sequence that is shared among the ends of
non-homologous chromosomes and provides a substrate for ectopic exchange
\cite{MeffordTrask2002,Flint1997,Linardopoulou2005,Brown1990,Wilkie1991,Trask1991}.
However, incomplete reference assemblies and chromosome-by-chromosome analyses
have prevented a population-scale view of the extent and organization of
subtelomeric exchange \cite{Nurk2022,Liao2023,hprc_hprcv2_2025}.
Here we apply a reference-free pangenome approach to 465 near-complete human
assemblies, comparing every chromosome end against every other, and find that
high-identity pseudo-homolog regions~\cite{Guarracino2023} occur on 41 of 48 chromosome arms.
These regions form structured sequence communities containing recurrent,
copy-number-enriched functional gene modules associated with chromosome dynamics,
intracellular organization and germ-cell development.
Human chromosome-contact maps show preferential proximity between subtelomeres
with similar sequences, persisting even in adjacent flanks that lack the shared
sequence used to define each pair. Mouse contact maps show the same preference,
strongest in meiosis at the zygotene bouquet, when telomeres cluster at the
nuclear envelope. In a three-generation telomere-to-telomere
pedigree, whole-genome comparison identifies putative recombination between
subtelomeric regions on non-homologous chromosomes from the same sequence
communities, while recovering the obligate Xp/Yp PAR1 recombination in the male
germline.
These results generalize known subtelomeric exchange systems into a
near-ubiquitous architecture and support recurrent ectopic exchange as a
genome-wide force in the concerted evolution of human chromosome ends.
}

\maketitle

Human subtelomeres are among the most rearrangement-prone and fast-evolving
regions of the human genome \cite{MeffordTrask2002}. Cytogenetic and clone-based studies established that each chromosome end is a
mosaic of duplicated segments also found at the ends of several other,
non-homologous chromosomes, arranged in two domains: proximal,
chromosome-specific sequence and a distal zone of repeats shared across many
ends \cite{Flint1997,Linardopoulou2005}.
These duplicated blocks harbor subtelomeric copies of gene families and
repeat-associated loci (olfactory receptors, tubulins and the D4Z4/DUX4
macrosatellite), with copy number and sequence varying within and between human
populations
\cite{MeffordTrask2002,segdup_bailey_eichler2006,segdup_jeong_yoo2025}.
These chromosome ends also exchange sequence ectopically, by translocation and gene
conversion \cite{Brown1990,Wilkie1991,Trask1991}, and that exchange has
continued into recent human evolution, with a large fraction of subtelomeric
sequence having arisen from duplications specific to the human lineage
\cite{Linardopoulou2005}.

This ectopic exchange has well-known consequences at particular chromosome ends.
The pseudoautosomal regions PAR1 and PAR2 are shared by the sex chromosomes,
and PAR1 carries the obligate male sex-chromosome crossover
\cite{Rouyer1986,sexchrompars_bellott2024}.
The ribosomal-DNA-bearing acrocentric short arms are organized around the
nucleolus, where they recombine and underlie recurrent Robertsonian translocations
\cite{acrocentric_Altemose2022,Guarracino2023,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025,acrocentric_Guarracino2025ape}.
The 4q35 D4Z4 macrosatellite, homologous to the 10q26 array, is the causal
locus in facioscapulohumeral dystrophy, and the DUX4 gene it contains, normally
silenced, is reactivated in diverse cancers
\cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide,dux4_cancer_Chew2019}.
Unbalanced subtelomeric rearrangements are also a recurrent cause of
intellectual disability
\cite{MeffordTrask2002}.
Despite these consequences, the broader picture remained assembled from heterogeneous and often
locus-focused evidence, including fluorescence in situ hybridization, BAC and
half-YAC clones, monochromosomal hybrids, and later complete-assembly analyses
of particular chromosome classes or genomes
\cite{Mefford2001,Riethman2004,Ambrosini2007,Guarracino2023,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025}.
The unresolved question was not whether inter-chromosomal subtelomeric exchange
occurs, but whether it could be mapped uniformly across complete chromosome
ends in a large haplotype-resolved cohort.

Two advances now make this view possible.
The telomere-to-telomere CHM13 assembly resolved one human genome end to end
\cite{Nurk2022}.
The second release of the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium (HPRC v2)
provides near-complete, haplotype-resolved assemblies for 232 individuals from
five superpopulations \cite{hprc_hprcv2_2025}.
HPRC analyses of heterologous acrocentric chromosomes and complete assemblies
of human Robertsonian chromosomes established that their short arms recombine
through shared pseudo-homolog regions (PHRs)
\cite{Guarracino2023,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025}. Building on those
pangenome graph methods, we extend reference-free homology analysis from these
individual exchange systems to all chromosome ends across the cohort.
Here we ask whether the acrocentric result is one instance of a genome-wide
pattern.
Revisiting subtelomeric organization without chromosomal partitioning, we pose
three questions the cytogenetic era could consider but not resolve.
How much sequence do all chromosome ends share with one another?
Is this sharing organized into distinct sequence communities?
Do sequence-similar subtelomeres lie close together in the nucleus and exchange
between members of the same communities in pedigrees?

\subsection*{A whole-genome survey reveals subtelomeric sharing}

We first asked where inter-chromosomal sequence sharing occurs without
preselecting chromosome ends. We aligned 25,272 sampled
ordered pairs (11.6\% of all possible) from a 466-sequence whole-genome set
comprising 464 HPRC v2 haplotypes plus CHM13v2.0 and GRCh38.
The sample size was selected well above the modeled
connectivity threshold for the assembly-pair network (Methods). We indexed these
alignments as an implicit pangenome graph and queried non-overlapping 100 kb
CHM13 windows through transitive alignment paths
\cite{pangenome_graphs_impg_IMPG2023}.
The resulting genome-wide map reveals high-identity inter-chromosomal sharing
concentrated at chromosome ends (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1}A). The acrocentric short
arms and pseudoautosomal regions appear as expected positive controls, but the
same pattern recurs at most other chromosome ends.

To delimit these regions, we identified terminal
telomeric repeat tracts and extracted the 500 kb flank immediately inward from
each telomere in 465 near-complete assemblies (excluding GRCh38, which lacks the
complete telomere ends needed for flank extraction), giving 18,827 flanks across
the 48 chromosome arms. We then performed a separate, complete all-against-all flank
alignment at 95\% minimum identity, without restricting comparisons
to homologous chromosomes. We indexed this flank-specific alignment set as an
implicit pangenome graph~\cite{pangenome_graphs_impg_IMPG2023}
and queried consecutive windows from each telomere inward. We define a PHR here as a telomere-anchored interval containing
windows
with at least five directly aligned segments from each of at least two
chromosomes, including its own, at $\geq 95\%$ identity.
This yields 15,668 PHRs on 41 of the 48 arms; the remaining 7 arms (chr2p,
chr3p, chr5p, chr8q, chr11q, chr14q, chr18q) carry no detectable
inter-chromosomal sharing.
Walking inward from each telomere until inter-chromosomal similarity disappears,
the called PHRs span tens to hundreds of kilobases (median 105 kb, mean 144 kb;
Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1}B), the upper bound set by the 500 kb flank window rather
than by biology.
Excluding the acrocentric short arms and the pseudoautosomal regions, these PHRs
total 3.51 Mb of subtelomeric sequence, with individual PHRs within the same
order of magnitude as PAR2 (the median PHR is 31\% of PAR2's length)
\cite{sexchrompars_bellott2024}.

\subsection*{Communities of subtelomeric PHRs}

The explicit pangenome graph~\cite{Garrison2024pggb,pangenome_graphs_unbiased_GarrisonGuarracino2023}
built from all 15,668 PHR sequences forms a large main
connected component (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}A) that contains 15,654 of them (99.9\%)
and is too tangled to interpret node by node.
We therefore measured pangenome similarity between chromosome ends as the
Jaccard overlap of the graph nodes they traverse, and reduced the result to an
arm-level similarity matrix.
The matrix is not uniform: locally dense blocks of high similarity are
embedded in a broader continuum of lower inter-community similarity
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}B, left).
Leiden community detection~\cite{leiden_traag2019} on this arm-level matrix partitions the 41
PHR-bearing arms into 15 arm-level communities (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}B,
right).
The communities recover every previously described case of inter-chromosomal
subtelomere homology: the two pseudoautosomal pairs Xp/Yp and Xq/Yq
\cite{sexchrompars_acquaviva2020}, the five acrocentric short arms
\cite{acrocentric_Altemose2022}, the 10p/18p pair first reported by Linardopoulou
and colleagues \cite{Linardopoulou2005}, and the 4q/10q D4Z4 pair
\cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide}.
Beyond these discrete communities, the same matrix shows a weaker q-arm
enrichment that does not resolve into a separate community (22q, 21q, 19q, 1q,
13q and 17q).
Average-linkage UPGMA provides an alternative display and method comparison
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}B, left), but we do not interpret the ordering as an
evolutionary tree.

\subsection*{Architecture of representative communities}

Examined at the sequence level, each community carries a characteristic duplicon
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}).
Community C1 (4q/10q) carries the D4Z4 macrosatellite and its DUX4 retrogene, with
homologous DUX4L arrays and FRG1/FRG2 paralogues on both ends and copy number
ranging from 0 to 22 across haplotypes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}A)
\cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide,Cabianca2012}.
Community C2 (10p/18p) is anchored by a TUBB8-family duplicon, with TUBB8 on
10p and its TUBB8B paralogue on 18p amid tandem tubulin-related copies
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}B) \cite{Linardopoulou2005}.
Community C11 (5q/6q) is built on the OR4F olfactory-receptor family and its
pseudogenes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}C).
More broadly, subtelomeric gene content is dominated by pseudogenes and
non-coding RNAs. What defines the communities is characteristic subtelomeric
duplicons, not community-specific protein-coding genes
\cite{Ambrosini2007}. Within-community arm pairs are overwhelmingly
assortative by arm type: 58 of 75 pairs (77\%) join two p-arms or
two q-arms, compared with 400 of 820 (49\%) among all possible pairs of the
41 PHR-bearing arms. PHR sharing is therefore constrained by chromosome-arm
context rather than distributed indiscriminately among chromosome ends.

\subsection*{Functional systems enriched within subtelomeric PHRs}

To investigate the functional significance of the PHR compartment, we
performed a copy-number-aware enrichment analysis of Gene Ontology (GO)
\cite{GeneOntologyConsortium2026} and Reactome \cite{Reactome2024} annotations
in CHM13 (Methods). Gene copies within PHRs were compared with all annotated gene copies
elsewhere in the genome, with each duplicated copy counted separately rather
than collapsed by gene name or family. The analysis
identified 209 significantly enriched terms spanning biological processes,
molecular functions, cellular components and Reactome pathways.

The enrichments fall into several functional systems whose distributions differ
markedly across the PHR communities (Extended Data Fig.~\ref{fig:ed2}). The most
community-restricted system is associated with DUX4: functions related to
zygotic genome activation, transcriptional regulation, the nuclear envelope and
the cell cycle are concentrated in the community C1 exchange network linking 4q and 10q.
By contrast, functions involved in intracellular organization and chromosome
dynamics are distributed across multiple communities. A recurrent WASH/DDX11
module spans six communities, combining WASH-associated endosomal actin and
exocyst activity with DDX11-associated helicase activity and sister-chromatid
cohesion. A second distributed system is associated with male germ-cell
development and sexual reproduction. The direct GO term spermatid development
is enriched 25-fold relative to the rest of the genome and is carried by 19
SEPTIN14-associated PHR copies;
broader enriched terms include spermatogenesis, germ-cell development, gamete
generation and sexual reproduction. These copies also contribute septin and
cytokinesis functions and are concentrated in community C3, with additional copies in
communities C5, C11 and C12. Signaling functions associated with WBP1L/CXCL12 and ribosomal
and nucleolar functions associated with RPL23A are also shared among several
otherwise distinct communities.

Extended Data Fig.~\ref{fig:ed2} maps these systems across chromosome ends and
shows that PHRs do not carry a uniform functional repertoire. Instead, each
community contains a different combination of partially overlapping modules:
some are highly amplified within a single exchange network, whereas others
recur across multiple communities. This functional mosaic parallels the
underlying sequence-sharing communities and suggests that inter-chromosomal exchange
redistributes discrete blocks of gene content rather than homogenizing entire
chromosome ends.

\subsection*{Three-dimensional proximity of sequence-similar subtelomeres}

Why should pseudo-homolog regions persist at 41 of the 48 arms?
If persistence involves spatial proximity, subtelomeres that share more sequence
should lie closer together in the nucleus. In human contact maps, we find that
aggregate contact at PHR coordinates increases with PHR sequence similarity.
We measured this per inter-chromosomal PHR pair within a single genome in
HG002 Pore-C (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}A; pointwise
Spearman $\rho = 0.376$, $n = 2{,}830$), with the same direction in
CHM13 Hi-C, HG002 Hi-C and HG002 CiFi (Extended Data Fig.~\ref{fig:ed1}).
Because near-identical PHRs on non-homologous chromosomes cannot be told apart by
read mapping, we interpret contact at a PHR window as an aggregate over its
near-identical copies rather than reads uniquely assigned to a single PHR.
The 100 kb of unique sequence immediately centromere-ward of each PHR, the
non-PHR flanks, map unambiguously and so provide the key control for this
multi-mapping concern. In HG002 Hi-C, their mean between- over within-community
contact ratio ($B/W$; lower means stronger) is 0.0013 versus 0.028 in the PHR
windows, a 21-fold stronger within-community enrichment, although the flanks do
not contain the shared sequence used to define PHR pairs.
Ordering a Pore-C contact matrix by sequence community shows the same community
block structure after observed/expected inter-chromosomal normalization
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}B) \cite{Ulahannan2019}.
The same within-community enrichment holds in the HG002 CiFi contact map
\cite{hic3d_cifi2025}.
Mouse meiotic Hi-C from a B6 $\times$ CAST F1 hybrid, mapped to the B6 and CAST
T2T assemblies, provides a stage-resolved comparison across prophase I. The
same assemblies, PHR sequence identities and read-placement
procedure are used at all four stages (leptotene, zygotene, pachytene and
diplotene), so only the meiotic stage differs \cite{Francis2025,Zuo2021}.
Subtelomeric sequence similarity is positively correlated with contact across
all four stages (pointwise Spearman $\rho = 0.419$, 0.614, 0.576 and 0.245 at
leptotene, zygotene, pachytene and diplotene; all $n = 1{,}135$;
Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}C), strongest at the zygotene bouquet, when telomeres cluster
at the nuclear envelope, and lowest at diplotene.

\subsection*{Whole-genome pedigree windows recover putative subtelomeric exchange}

To look for recent exchange within a single family, we traced transmission
through the WashU pedigree \cite{Cechova2025}, to our knowledge the only one with
complete telomere-to-telomere assemblies in all three generations
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}A), which makes
transmission-resolved subtelomeric comparison possible at chromosome ends where
incomplete assemblies usually fail. We compared the chromosomes a
daughter inherited from her father against both of his haplotypes by
whole-genome alignment (Methods, Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}).
This father-to-daughter transmission is a useful positive-control setting because
recombination is biased toward chromosome ends in the male germline
\cite{Bherer2017}, and the same procedure recovers PAR1 sharing between Xp and
Yp, the obligate site of male sex-chromosome recombination.
Almost everywhere, each of the daughter's chromosomes matches the same
chromosome in her father. However, the whole-genome scan (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}C)
highlights two putative autosomal PHR exchanges, chr5q/chr1p and
chr9q/chr3q, where a subtelomere's best match is instead a non-homologous father
chromosome. For chr9q/chr3q, the best match shifts from the father's chr9 to his
chr3q across the 20 kb tract (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}B).
The two autosomal segments are short, 20--28 kb, compared with 138 kb for the
PAR1 control. The exchanges were called without using the community structure,
yet each pair falls in the community expected from the survey (Xp/Yp in C15,
chr5q/chr1p in C11, chr9q/chr3q in C3).
We also ran the same scan on two further transmissions, the chromosomes the
daughter inherited from her mother and the chromosomes the granddaughter
inherited from that same daughter. The
daughter's maternal side shows only the expected acrocentric-acrocentric sharing
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}D), while the granddaughter's is again dominated by
acrocentric-acrocentric sharing, with two further highlighted candidates,
the subtelomeric chr5q/chr1p and a chr21p/chr4p run whose chr4 partner is
pericentromeric rather than subtelomeric (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}E).

The two autosomal candidates (chr9q/chr3q and chr5q/chr1p) are inter-chromosomal
exchanges, each a subtelomere of the daughter that matches a non-homologous
chromosome in her father more closely than the homologous one. Of the two,
chr9q/chr3q is the better-supported call, its chr3q match beating the homologous
chr9q by a wider identity margin than the chr1p match beats chr5q (Methods). In the WashU crossover map this paternal transmission has no
crossover on chromosome 3 (M. Cechova and T. Marschall, personal communication),
so ordinary chr3 recombination cannot account for the chr9q/chr3q signal. We therefore treat these as candidate PHR
exchanges consistent with recombination, not as confirmed events with resolved
breakpoints.

\subsection*{Discussion}

Together, these results define a subtelomeric exchange compartment, a distributed
set of chromosome ends that share high-identity sequence, sit in preferential
three-dimensional proximity, and recurrently exchange material. Individual pieces
of this compartment have long been recognized through cytogenetics and locus-specific studies,
but had not been assembled into a systematic, population-scale view across nearly
complete human genomes. The HPRC v2 assemblies let us build one, turning scattered
observations into a sequence-resolved map across 41 chromosome arms, in which the
pseudoautosomal, acrocentric, 10p/18p and 4q/10q systems stand out as local peaks
within a broader continuum. The 3.51 Mb of subtelomeric sequence mapped here
traces the routes along which chromosome-end sequence has repeatedly been copied,
exchanged and homogenized \cite{Dover1982,concerted_evolution_nahr_Hastings2009}.

This compartment is also internally organized. Communities assort by arm type,
giving pronounced p--p and q--q structure whose cause we cannot yet determine.
One possibility, because arm type is defined by centromere position, is that
colocalization of centromeres in the nucleus brings same-arm-type ends into
repeated contact and biases opportunities for ectopic exchange, with telomere
clustering at the meiotic nuclear envelope acting in the same direction.
Alternatively, the arm-type structure may reflect history rather than current
geometry. It could record which ends have accumulated the same duplicated
sequence over time, or follow a dedicated structure that bundles particular arms,
as shared rDNA bundles the acrocentric p-arms at the nucleolus
\cite{Guarracino2023}. In either
case, inter-chromosomal sequence sharing coincides with preferential
three-dimensional proximity and with putative recombination between subtelomeric
regions on non-homologous chromosomes. Together these observations point to a
self-reinforcing process: proximity at the meiotic bouquet creates opportunities
for ectopic recombination, and exchange generates new shared sequence that enters
the population and can reinforce proximity at subsequent meioses.

Copy-number-aware ontology enrichment shows that PHRs carry a nonrandom
functional repertoire whose distribution mirrors their mosaic community
structure. The enriched systems connect germ-cell development and sexual
reproduction with sister-chromatid cohesion, cytokinesis, intracellular
organization and zygotic genome activation, collectively spanning chromosome
organization and transmission through the germline and genome reactivation
after fertilization. Their subtelomeric location places these gene copies in the
nuclear-envelope-associated compartment where telomeres cluster during meiotic
prophase, a physical configuration that also promotes opportunities for ectopic
exchange. This architecture may couple two complementary properties:
distributed copy number can provide robustness for processes on which
successful germline transmission depends, while recurrent exchange can
homogenize, reshuffle and diversify individual copies
\cite{Linardopoulou2005,Ambrosini2007}. PHRs may therefore provide both a stable
foundation for chromosome transmission and the flexibility needed for germline
systems to accommodate continuing change in chromosome-end sequence and
organization. This interpretation remains a hypothesis: our
single-genome CHM13 analysis measures the number and functional annotations of
gene copies, not their expression, protein activity, dosage effects, retained
pseudogene function, fitness consequences, selective history or prevalence in
the population. The 209 enriched GO terms are also nested and overlapping, and
so represent far fewer independent biological systems.

The repetitive and inter-chromosomal nature of PHRs has made them difficult to
represent using conventional genomic analyses. Pipelines built around unique
mapping, stable chromosome assignment and predominantly biallelic variation may
collapse or exclude precisely the variation that defines this compartment. Some
of the same variation may therefore remain undercounted in population-genetic
summaries and poorly tagged in SNP-based genome-wide association studies.
Which way the sequence--proximity relationship runs remains open, and our human
three-dimensional data are somatic rather than germline, with the meiotic-stage
measurement supplied only by mouse. Resolving these questions will require
germline-stage contact maps and larger multigenerational pedigrees with complete,
haplotype-resolved telomere-to-telomere assemblies \cite{Cechova2025}, together
with orthogonal assays such as FISH \cite{Trask1991}. Even so,
the map presented here already converts relationships inferred from cytogenetics
decades ago into a population-scale, complete-genome view of an active system of
chromosome-end evolution.

\section*{Methods}
\label{secMethods}

\setcounter{figure}{0}

\subsection*{Whole-genome implicit pangenome survey}

The whole-genome survey used 464 HPRC v2 haplotype assemblies together with
CHM13v2.0 and GRCh38, for 466 input sequences. We used wfmash v0.23.0~\cite{wfmash}
(\texttt{-p 95 -P inf}) to align 25,272 sampled ordered target--query
pairs, representing 11.6\% of the $466^{2}$ possible ordered pairs.
The sample size was selected using an Erd\H{o}s--R\'{e}nyi
connectivity model and lay well above the modeled threshold for a connected
assembly-pair network. The resulting pairwise alignments were indexed with IMPG
v0.4.1. For Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1}A, non-overlapping 100 kb windows spanning CHM13
were queried through at most five transitive alignment hops at a minimum
estimated identity of 98\%; for each window we summarized the distinct
chromosome origins and mean identity of the sequences reached
\cite{pangenome_graphs_impg_IMPG2023}.

\subsection*{Samples, exclusions and flank extraction}

The dataset comprised 464 haplotype-resolved HPRC v2 assemblies from 232
individuals plus CHM13v2.0, for 465 near-complete assemblies, with
superpopulation labels assigned at the HPRC individual level
\cite{hprc_hprcv2_2025,Nurk2022,logsdon2025hgsvc}. Two exclusions were applied.
First, GRCh38 contigs and the CHM13v2.0 Y chromosome (masked PAR1) were excluded
from flank extraction because they lack telomere ends suitable for the 500 kb
flank definition. Second, the chr18q flank of NA18982 haplotype 1
(contig JBKABS010000018.1) was excluded as a scaffolding chimera: in the
whole-genome wfmash alignments its first 83.37 Mb map to chr18 and a terminal
966 kb (83.38--84.35 Mb) map to chrX PAR1, joined across a 100 bp gap of Ns. No
further haplotype-level exclusions were applied. We identified terminal repeat
tracts with \texttt{seqtk telo}~\cite{seqtk} and, from each retained haplotype assembly,
extracted the 500 kb immediately internal to each tract on every contig at least
1 Mb long that was classified as p- or q-arm telomere-bearing. The telomeric
repeat tract itself was removed, giving 18,827 flanks across the 48 arms.

\subsection*{Alignment, implicit pangenome graph and PHR detection}

We aligned the complete flank collection all-against-all with wfmash
v0.23.0~\cite{wfmash} at 95\% minimum identity, each of the 18,827 flanks serving
in turn as the target against the full collection. Every flank was therefore
compared with every other without restricting comparisons to homologous
chromosomes. We indexed this pairwise alignment set with IMPG v0.4.1 for direct
interval queries \cite{pangenome_graphs_impg_IMPG2023}. Starting at each
telomere, we queried 5 kb windows in 5 kb steps, stopping after four consecutive
windows failed the detection criteria. A window passed when the direct IMPG
query returned at least five alignments from each of at least two
chromosomes, including its own (so pseudoautosomal Xp/Yp and Xq/Yq, with a
single partner, are also detected), at a minimum identity of 95\% and minimum
output length of 3 kb.
The resulting telomere-anchored intervals defined 15,668 PHRs (83.2\% of
flanks) on 41 of the 48 arms.

\subsection*{Pangenome graph, similarity and community detection}

We extracted the 15,668 called PHR intervals and supplied those sequences as the
input to pggb at 95\% identity. We computed all-pairs Jaccard similarity from
the resulting explicit graph with odgi, giving a $15{,}668 \times
15{,}668$ matrix
\cite{Garrison2024pggb,pangenome_graphs_impg_GuarracinoHeumos2022,andreace2023pangenome,heumos2024nfcore}.
The main connected component was laid out with odgi and rendered in R
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}A), each node colored by the dominant arm-level Leiden
community of the PHR paths crossing it. The arm-level distance matrix is the mean
pairwise Jaccard distance between PHR sequences within each arm pair. From it, arm-level
communities were inferred after converting distances to graph weights
$w_{ij} = \exp(-d_{ij} / \mathrm{median}(d))$ and scanning Leiden resolution from
0.1 to 3.0; we selected the maximum-silhouette partition, 15 communities at
resolution 1.16, and the same community count held across resolutions 1.13--1.18.
We classified every pair of arms assigned to the same community as either
same-type (p--p or q--q) or mixed (p--q) and compared the
within-community counts with the corresponding counts across all
$\binom{41}{2}=820$ possible pairs of PHR-bearing arms.

\subsection*{Copy-number-aware gene ontology enrichment}

We performed the copy-number-aware GO and Reactome enrichment analysis using
61,312 gene records from the CHM13v2.0 RefSeq/Liftoff v5.2 GFF3. Each record was
counted as one gene copy. Records at the same coordinates remained separate, as
did copies sharing a gene name, family, locus family or duplicated segment.
Thus, a gene represented at 20 genomic positions contributed 20 copies rather
than one collapsed gene-family entry. Before using the PHR coordinates, we
assigned a functional source and recorded the supporting evidence for every
gene copy. An exact stable identifier could use only its own functional
annotation; a non-self record could inherit a source only through a unique,
explicitly directed human
NCBI Gene ``Related functional gene'' relation. Ambiguous exact identifiers,
type-only records, unresolved records and unsupported records failed closed and
contributed no ontology terms. This kept the number of genomic gene copies
separate from the identity of the functional source used to annotate them.

NCBI Gene registry and directed relationship snapshots were dated 16 July
2026. Direct GO assertions came from the NCBI human gene2go snapshot dated 13
July 2026 and were propagated through the go-basic release dated 15 June 2026;
Reactome human direct assertions and recorded pathway ancestors came from
release 96 (1 April 2026)
\cite{GeneOntologyAshburner2000,GeneOntologyConsortium2026,Reactome2024}.
Direct and ancestral annotations were tested separately. The functional
mappings and 31,235 prespecified combinations of database, annotation level and
ontology term were defined and checksum-recorded before gene copies were
classified as PHR or non-PHR.

A gene copy entered the enrichment analysis if it had at least one direct or
ancestral GO or Reactome assignment, defining a fixed universe of $N=31{,}966$
copies. A gene was assigned to a PHR when its GFF3 midpoint intersected the
CHM13 PHR BED. Every eligible gene copy occurred exactly once, either within a
PHR or in the complete non-PHR remainder of the genome. The comparison therefore
contained $K=187$ PHR copies and $N-K=31{,}779$ non-PHR copies.
Another 215 of the 402 gene copies with midpoints in PHRs could not be assigned
confidently to an ontology term and were retained as a coverage limitation. We
used the complete genome-wide non-PHR complement as the background, without
sampling, permutation, spatial placement or matching to other subtelomeres.

For every tested term $T$, $a_T$ was the number of eligible PHR copies carrying
$T$, $b_T=K-a_T$ the eligible PHR copies not carrying it, $c_T$ the non-PHR
copies carrying it, and $d_T=(N-K)-c_T$ the non-PHR copies not carrying it;
$M_T=a_T+c_T$ was the genome-wide eligible burden. We calculated the exact
one-sided upper tail
$P[X\geq a_T]$ for $X\sim\mathrm{Hypergeometric}(N,M_T,K)$ over this fully
enumerated fixed population. The hypergeometric distribution is the exact
finite-population calculation here and does not treat copies as independently
sampled biological replicates. Effect summaries comprised PHR and non-PHR copy
fractions, their unsmoothed fold enrichment and odds ratio with explicit zero
states, Haldane--Anscombe-smoothed fold and odds ratio after adding 0.5 to all
four cells, and observed-minus-expected PHR copy burden.

We applied Benjamini--Hochberg correction within each of GO biological process,
GO molecular function, GO cellular component and Reactome across both direct
and ancestral annotations, and Holm correction globally across all 31,235
tests. We considered a term enriched in the primary analysis when its
within-database BH $q\leq0.05$ and its global Holm-adjusted $P\leq0.05$. The
overlap analysis was used only to assess sensitivity to PHR boundaries. After
completing the enrichment tests, we mapped the contributing gene copies back to
the 15 arm communities and grouped related terms into six readable functional
classes for Extended Data Fig.~\ref{fig:ed2}. These classes added no new
hypotheses or statistical tests.

Reproducibility checks validated the inherited source-map and hypothesis
checksums, complete PHR/non-PHR partitions, all $2\times2$ tables, contributor
recounts, multiplicity families, coordinate-copy semantics, deterministic
ordering and the absence of stochastic or placement inference. The retained V7
engine, validation JSON, community-attribution workflow and unit tests are in
the analysis repository. The two compact ED2 tables are regenerated without
new inference by \texttt{submission/scripts/make\_ed2\_supporting\_material.py}.

\subsection*{Hierarchical clustering and resampling controls}

For display and method comparison we also ordered the arm-level matrix by
average-linkage hierarchical clustering (UPGMA); cutting it into 14 clusters gave
a mean silhouette of 0.342 and exact agreement with 12 of the 15 Leiden
communities, and we use this dendrogram as a heatmap ordering, not as a
phylogenetic model. As a sensitivity analysis, we evaluated a PHR-resampling
control that resamples rows of a
collapsed per-PHR cross-chromosome involvement table, rebuilds an arm-level
surrogate distance matrix, and recomputes neighbor-joining and UPGMA trees.
Because it does not resample the full 15,668-sequence Jaccard matrix, it is not
used as evidence for the Leiden communities or for a discrete q-arm partition;
under this chromosome-level surrogate, the six-arm q-neighborhood has low
support, so we treat the q-arm pattern as an enriched neighborhood in the
similarity matrix rather than a bootstrap-supported grouping.

\subsection*{Hi-C, Pore-C and CiFi pipeline}

Hi-C, Pore-C and CiFi were reprocessed from raw reads
against the corresponding T2T reference (CHM13v2.0 or the matched HPRC v2
haplotype) with multi-mappers retained, because the default mapping-quality
filter ($\geq 30$) of deposited maps masks the high-identity, signal-bearing
subtelomeric sequence at the mapping stage. Pore-C (NlaIII) and CiFi (DpnII) were
processed with pore-c-py, minimap2 and pairtools, and Hi-C with HiC-Pro and
Bowtie2, binning contacts with cooler. Because no mapping-quality filter is
applied, a molecule mapping equally well to several paralogous ends contributes
to each of them, so PHR-internal contacts are aggregate coordinate-level
measurements rather than uniquely assigned read-level contacts. Contact matrices
were generated at 5, 10, 20, 50 and 100 kb, with maternal and paternal contigs
kept as separate arms.

Pointwise Spearman correlations are reported as effect sizes, not significance
tests, because PHR-pair observations share chromosome arms, PHRs, matrix rows and
columns, and read-placement histories. Each correlation takes one
inter-chromosomal PHR sequence pair as a point and relates its pangenome-graph
Jaccard similarity to its length-normalized contact at the pair's exact
coordinates within a single genome, at 20 kb, independently of the community
partition, so the correlation cannot be an artifact of the community grouping. The primary comparison is HG002
Pore-C (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}A; $\rho = 0.376$, $n = 2{,}830$); the replicates in
Extended Data Fig.~\ref{fig:ed1} are CHM13 Hi-C ($\rho = 0.742$, $n = 688$), HG002
Hi-C ($\rho = 0.616$, $n = 2{,}413$) and HG002 CiFi ($\rho = 0.196$,
$n = 2{,}757$; the sparsest assay, consistent with the weakest effect size).

The adjacent 100 kb centromere-ward non-PHR flanks are the principal control
against mapping-quality-zero inflation: in HG002 Hi-C they give $B/W = 0.0013$
against $0.028$ in the PHR windows. Community-ordered contact matrices used
observed/expected inter-chromosomal normalization
\cite{hic3d_dixon2012,hic3d_imakaev2012,Ulahannan2019,hic3d_cifi2025}; the
community-ordered Pore-C matrix (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}B; $B/W = 0.054$) is at 20 kb.
Matrix-level inference used row-and-column Mantel and permutation tests, with
finite permutation floors reported when no permutation exceeded the observed
statistic.

\subsection*{Mouse pipeline}

We repeated the entire pipeline on a non-human mammal, using the B6 and CAST T2T
assemblies \cite{Francis2025}. Telomere-anchored flanks were extracted and PHRs
called de novo, with a 1, 2 and 4 Mb window sweep (30 of 49 mouse PHRs saturate
the 1 Mb window) and a two-community arm-level partition. The stage-resolved
meiotic Hi-C is from a B6 $\times$ CAST F1 hybrid \cite{Zuo2021}, with reads
phased into paternal (B6) and maternal (CAST) haplotypes, mapped to the diploid
B6+CAST reference and treated as separate physical chromosomes. For each meiotic
prophase stage we computed the per-PHR-pair Spearman correlation between
length-normalized contact and PHR Jaccard similarity at 20 kb over 1 Mb flanks.
Subtelomeric similarity was positively correlated with inter-chromosomal contact
at all four stages, strongest at zygotene and lowest at diplotene
($\rho = 0.419$, 0.614, 0.576 and 0.245 at leptotene, zygotene, pachytene and
diplotene; all $n = 1{,}135$; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}C). These are point estimates
without a formal stage-contrast test; a Mantel cross-check agreed to within
$\pm 0.02$.

\subsection*{Pedigree direct-alignment windows}

For Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}C we used the PAN027 paternal haplotype as query against a
joint two-haplotype target from the transmitting father, PAN011
\cite{Cechova2025}. Starting from a whole-genome SweepGA/FastGA alignment
(frequency threshold 32, many-to-many mappings, no scaffold jumps), we filtered
to 10:10 mapping classes and summarized the query in non-overlapping 2 kb
windows, excluding centromeric windows, windows without alignment support, and
windows with inter-chromosomal alignment depth above 100. For each retained
window we computed IMPG similarity (partial-order alignment, no merge,
many-to-many mapping, no scaffold jumps) and kept the best same-chromosome or
homologous father match and the best non-homologous father match. A window was
assigned to the non-homologous class only when the non-homologous match was the
most similar. Adjacent windows were joined into a run only when the query and
donor intervals abutted end to end on the same donor chromosome, haplotype and
sequence, with no gap-tolerant or transitive merge. Colored runs are
non-homologous runs of at least 10 kb with mean inter-chromosomal identity at
least 0.95. The gray inheritance background was drawn from the same table,
joining same-chromosome father-child rows of at least 0.95 identity by the same
rule and drawing chains of at least 10 kb. PHR and community labels were assigned
after calling and were not used to select the windows. This many-to-many,
graph-projected comparison is used because short, highly similar subtelomeric
segments are difficult targets for whole-genome aligners. It keeps the
near-identical competing matches between non-homologous chromosome ends that a
single pairwise or reference-guided alignment would assign to one location and
discard, and that marker-based recombination maps do not resolve.

For Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}D,E we applied the same workflow, thresholds and display
rules to two maternal comparisons: the PAN027 maternal haplotype against the
joint PAN010 mother target, and the PAN028 maternal haplotype against the joint
PAN027 mother target. The GitHub supporting-material table reports, for all
displayed calls, assembly-native query and donor intervals, 2 kb window counts,
mean identities, same-chromosome competitor scores, and post hoc PHR and
community labels; its CHM13 columns are arm-level PHR annotations, not exact
coordinate liftovers of the pedigree-specific haplotype assemblies.
