\title[Concerted evolution and unorthodox recombination of human subtelomeres]{Concerted evolution and unorthodox recombination of human subtelomeres}

\abstract{
Human subtelomeres contain duplicated sequence that is shared among the ends of
non-homologous chromosomes and provides a substrate for ectopic exchange
\cite{MeffordTrask2002,Flint1997,Linardopoulou2005,Brown1990,Wilkie1991,Trask1991}.
However, incomplete reference assemblies and chromosome-by-chromosome analyses
have prevented a population-scale view of the extent and organization of
subtelomeric exchange \cite{Nurk2022,Liao2023,hprc_hprcv2_2025}.
Here we apply a reference-free pangenome approach to 465 near-complete human
assemblies, comparing every chromosome end against every other, and find that
high-identity pseudo-homolog regions occur on 41 of 48 chromosome arms.
These regions form structured sequence communities in which previously described
exchange systems appear as local peaks within a broader continuum.
Human and mouse chromosome-contact maps show preferential proximity between
subtelomeres with similar sequences. In mouse meiosis this proximity is
strongest at the zygotene bouquet, when telomeres cluster at the nuclear
envelope; in human data it persists even in adjacent flanks that lack the shared
sequence used to define each pair. In a three-generation telomere-to-telomere
pedigree, whole-genome comparison identifies putative recombination between
subtelomeric regions on non-homologous chromosomes that matches this community
organization, while recovering the obligate Xp/Yp PAR1 recombination in the male
germline.
These results generalize known subtelomeric exchange systems into a
near-ubiquitous architecture and support recurrent ectopic exchange as a
genome-wide force in the concerted evolution of human chromosome ends.
}

\maketitle

Human subtelomeres are among the most rearrangement-prone and fast-evolving
regions of the human genome. Cytogenetic and clone-based studies established that each chromosome end is a
mosaic of duplicated segments also found at the ends of several other,
non-homologous chromosomes, arranged in two domains: proximal,
chromosome-specific sequence and a distal zone of repeats shared across many
ends \cite{MeffordTrask2002,Flint1997,Linardopoulou2005}.
These duplicated blocks harbor subtelomeric copies of gene families and
repeat-associated loci (olfactory receptors, tubulins and the D4Z4/DUX4
macrosatellite), with copy number and sequence varying within and between human
populations
\cite{MeffordTrask2002,segdup_bailey_eichler2006,segdup_jeong_yoo2025}.
These ends also exchange sequence ectopically, by translocation and gene
conversion \cite{Brown1990,Wilkie1991,Trask1991}, and that exchange has
continued into recent human evolution, with a large fraction of subtelomeric
sequence having arisen from duplications specific to the human lineage
\cite{Linardopoulou2005}.

This ectopic exchange has well-known consequences at particular chromosome ends.
The pseudoautosomal regions PAR1 and PAR2 are shared by the sex chromosomes,
and PAR1 carries the obligate male sex-chromosome crossover
\cite{Rouyer1986,sexchrompars_bellott2024}; the ribosomal-DNA-bearing
acrocentric short arms are organized around the nucleolus, recombine, and
underlie recurrent
Robertsonian translocations
\cite{acrocentric_Altemose2022,Guarracino2023,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025,acrocentric_Guarracino2025ape}; the
4q35 D4Z4 macrosatellite, homologous to the 10q26 array, is the causal
locus in facioscapulohumeral dystrophy and is reactivated in
diverse cancers
\cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide,dux4_cancer_Chew2019}; and unbalanced
subtelomeric rearrangements are a recurrent cause of intellectual disability
\cite{MeffordTrask2002}.
Yet this picture rested on locus-specific and reference-limited evidence:
fluorescence in situ
hybridization, BAC and half-YAC clones, and monochromosomal hybrids in a handful
of genomes \cite{Mefford2001,Riethman2004,Ambrosini2007}, because no reference
assembly reached the telomeres \cite{Nurk2022} and analyzing each chromosome
separately hid the sharing between them \cite{Liao2023}.
The unresolved question was therefore not whether inter-chromosomal
subtelomeric exchange occurs, but its population-scale extent, variation and
organization across complete chromosome ends.

Two advances now make this view possible: the
telomere-to-telomere CHM13 assembly resolved one human genome end to end
\cite{Nurk2022}, and the second release of the Human Pangenome Reference
Consortium (HPRC v2) provides near-complete, haplotype-resolved assemblies for
232 individuals from five superpopulations \cite{hprc_hprcv2_2025}.
At the same time, pangenome analysis of heterologous acrocentric chromosomes
and complete assemblies of human Robertsonian chromosomes showed that the
best-characterized subtelomeric exchange system could be resolved sequence by sequence
\cite{Guarracino2023,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025}; here we ask
whether that acrocentric result is one instance of a genome-wide pattern.
Revisiting subtelomeric organization without chromosomal partitioning, we pose
three questions the cytogenetic era could consider but not resolve: how
extensive is
inter-chromosomal subtelomeric sharing across all 48 chromosome arms; how is it
organized into sequence communities; and does sequence similarity predict both
three-dimensional proximity in the nucleus and compatible pedigree-resolved
patch patterns within the same communities.

\subsection*{Implicit pangenome graphs of subtelomeres}

We treat every haplotype as its own reference.
From 232 HPRC v2 individuals we used both haplotype assemblies together
with CHM13v2.0, for 465 near-complete assemblies; we
extracted 18,827 telomere-anchored 500 kb flanks across the 48
chromosome arms, and aligned them all-against-all with wfmash at 95\% minimum
identity \cite{Guarracino2023}, without restricting alignment to homologous
chromosomes.
A pangenome graph and the set of alignments that define it are equivalent
representations of the same object \cite{Garrison2024pggb}, so we treat the
resulting alignments as an implicit pangenome graph and query them through their
transitive closure, without ever building the graph
\cite{pangenome_graphs_impg_GarrisonGuarracino2023,pangenome_graphs_impg_IMPG2023}.
An all-base comparison across the full 465-assembly dataset would be
intractable, of order $10^{24}$ base-pair comparisons; restricting the
analysis to telomere-anchored flanks reduced the problem to the
18,827-flank all-against-all, which we queried through transitive closure to
recover essentially every subtelomere.
As a quality control, querying any reference window recovers nearly all other
haplotypes across the genome, with the expected exceptions of ribosomal DNA, the
centromeres and the sex chromosomes.
We define a pseudo-homolog region (PHR) as a flank window whose transitive
closure reaches at least five segments on each of at least two other chromosomes at
$\geq 95\%$ identity.
This yields 15,668 PHRs on 41 of the 48 arms; the remaining 7 arms (chr2\_p,
chr3\_p, chr5\_p, chr8\_q, chr11\_q, chr14\_q, chr18\_q) carry no detectable
inter-chromosomal sharing.

\subsection*{Genome-wide subtelomeric sharing}

A genome-wide survey reveals telomere-anchored, high-identity blocks at nearly
every chromosome end (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1}A), each window colored by the number
of other chromosomes it aligns to elsewhere in the pangenome.
The acrocentric short arms and the pseudoautosomal regions appear as expected
positive controls, but the same signal recurs at almost every other chromosome
end.
Walking inward from each telomere until inter-chromosomal similarity disappears,
the PHRs span tens to hundreds of kilobases (median 105 kb, mean 144 kb;
Fig.~\ref{fig:fig1}B), the upper bound set by the 500 kb flank window rather
than by biology.
Excluding the acrocentric short arms and the pseudoautosomal regions, these PHRs
total 3.51 Mb of subtelomeric sequence, with individual PHRs within the same
order of magnitude as PAR2 (the median PHR is 31\% of PAR2's length)
\cite{sexchrompars_bellott2024}.

\subsection*{Communities of subtelomeric PHRs}

Read directly, the implicit graph of all 15,668 PHRs forms a single connected
component (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}A) that is too tangled to interpret node by node.
We therefore measured pangenome similarity between chromosome ends as the
Jaccard overlap of the graph nodes they traverse, and reduced the result to an
arm-level similarity matrix.
The matrix is structured rather than uniform: it contains locally dense blocks
embedded in a broader continuum of lower inter-community similarity
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}B, left).
Leiden community detection on this arm-level matrix partitions the 41
signal-bearing arms into 15 arm-level communities (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}B,
right).
The communities recover every previously described case of inter-chromosomal
subtelomere homology: the two pseudoautosomal pairs Xp/Yp and Xq/Yq
\cite{sexchrompars_acquaviva2020}, the five acrocentric short arms
\cite{acrocentric_Altemose2022}, the 10p/18p pair first reported by Linardopoulou
and colleagues \cite{Linardopoulou2005}, and the 4q/10q D4Z4 pair
\cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide}.
Beyond these discrete communities, the same matrix shows a weaker q-arm
enrichment that does not resolve into a separate community (22q, 21q, 19q, 1q,
13q and 17q).
Average-linkage UPGMA provides a secondary display and method comparison, but we
do not interpret the ordering as an evolutionary tree.

\subsection*{Architecture of representative communities}

Examined at the sequence level, each community carries a characteristic duplicon
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}).
Community C1 (4q/10q) carries the D4Z4 macrosatellite and its DUX4 retrogene, with
homologous DUX4L arrays and FRG1/FRG2 paralogues on both ends and copy number
ranging from 0 to 22 across haplotypes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}A)
\cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide,Cabianca2012}.
Community C2 (10p/18p) is anchored by a TUBB8-family duplicon, with TUBB8 on
10p and its TUBB8B paralogue on 18p amid tandem tubulin-related copies
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}B) \cite{Linardopoulou2005}.
Community C11 (5q/6q) is built on the OR4F olfactory-receptor family and its
pseudogenes (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3}C).
More broadly, subtelomeric gene content is dominated by pseudogenes and
non-coding RNAs. What defines the communities is characteristic subtelomeric
duplicons, not community-specific protein-coding genes
\cite{Ambrosini2007}. Within-community arm pairs are overwhelmingly
of the same orientation (only 17 of 75 pair a p-arm with a q-arm).

\subsection*{Three-dimensional proximity of sequence-similar subtelomeres}

Why should pseudo-homolog regions persist at 41 of the 48 arms?
In human contact-map data, aggregate contact at PHR coordinates increases with
PHR sequence similarity.
We measured this per inter-chromosomal PHR pair within a single genome and
without community labels in HG002 Pore-C (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}A; pointwise
Spearman $\rho = 0.376$, $n = 2{,}830$), with the same direction in
CHM13 Hi-C, HG002 Hi-C and HG002 CiFi (Extended Data Fig.~\ref{fig:ed1}).
Because highly similar non-homologous PHR tracts cannot be assigned uniquely by
a mapping-quality threshold, we interpret PHR-window contacts as aggregate
coordinate-level measurements.
The adjacent 100 kb centromere-ward non-PHR flanks provide the key mapping
control: in HG002 Hi-C they show stronger within-community enrichment than the
PHR windows themselves ($B/W = 0.0013$ versus $0.028$, a 21-fold lower B/W ratio),
although the flanks do not contain the shared sequence used to define PHR
pairs.
Ordering a Pore-C contact matrix by sequence community shows the same community
block structure after observed/expected inter-chromosomal normalization
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}B) \cite{Ulahannan2019}.
Here and below $B/W$ denotes mean between-community contact divided by mean
within-community contact, so lower values indicate stronger within-community
proximity.
The same within-community enrichment holds in the HG002 CiFi contact map
\cite{hic3d_cifi2025}.
Mouse meiotic Hi-C provides a stage-resolved prophase-I comparison: the same B6
and CAST T2T assemblies, PHR sequence identities and read-placement procedure
are used at leptotene, zygotene, pachytene and diplotene
\cite{Francis2025,Zuo2021}.
Subtelomeric sequence similarity is positively correlated with contact across
all four stages, and the per-PHR-pair correlation is strongest at the zygotene
bouquet, when telomeres cluster at the nuclear envelope, and lowest at diplotene
(pointwise Spearman $\rho = 0.419$, 0.614, 0.576 and 0.245 at
leptotene, zygotene, pachytene and diplotene, all $n = 1{,}135$;
Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}C).

\subsection*{Whole-genome pedigree windows recover putative subtelomeric exchange}

To ask whether recent exchange can leave pedigree-resolved patterns compatible
with PHR sharing, we compared the PAN027 paternal haplotype with a joint
two-haplotype target from the transmitting father, PAN011
\cite{Cechova2025}, using whole-genome alignment followed by 2 kb
IMPG-similarity windows (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}).
This pedigree is uniquely suited to this test because it is, to our knowledge,
the only multigenerational human pedigree in which all three generations have
complete telomere-to-telomere assemblies, making transmission-resolved
subtelomeric comparison possible at chromosome ends where incomplete assemblies
usually fail.
This male transmission is a useful positive-control setting because
recombination is biased toward chromosome ends in the male germline
\cite{Bherer2017}, and the same procedure recovers PAR1 sharing between Xp and
Yp, the obligate site of male sex-chromosome recombination.
Against a background of same-chromosome father-child inheritance, the
whole-genome scan also highlights two short putative autosomal PHR exchanges,
chr5q/chr1p and chr9q/chr3q, in which the best non-homologous father match
beats the same-chromosome or homologous father match across adjacent windows.
These segments are modest in length (20--28 kb for the autosomal calls, and
138 kb total for PAR1) and lie within independently defined PHR/PAR intervals.
PHR and community labels were assigned only after calling; the highlighted pairs
fall in the expected arm-level sequence-similarity communities and show
post hoc Jaccard support (Xp/Yp C15, chr5q/chr1p C11, chr9q/chr3q C3).
Exact assembly-native intervals, identities, same-chromosome competitor scores,
PHR/community annotations and corresponding arm-level CHM13 PHR BED intervals
are provided in the GitHub supporting-material table.
Applying the same whole-genome homologous-competition scan to the maternal
PAN027 and PAN028 transmissions provides matched context rather than a second
primary claim: PAN027 maternal windows resolve only the expected
acrocentric-acrocentric class, while PAN028 maternal windows are again dominated
by acrocentric-acrocentric sharing, with one displayed chr21p/chr4p context run
and one additional chr5/chr1 candidate visible in the same display framework
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}C,D).

The two autosomal candidates differ in support, but their primary evidence is
the implicit-graph homologous-competition scan rather than concordance with a
single pairwise aligner. This distinction matters because short, highly similar
subtelomeric patches are difficult targets for whole-genome aligners, and
standard recombination maps usually infer crossovers from reference-positioned
markers rather than from sequence-resolved graph neighborhoods. As an
independent sensitivity check, wfmash reproduces the chr9q/chr3q donor at the
same chr3q subtelomeric locus at $>$99.8\% identity, while the chr5q/chr1p
signal has a narrower identity margin and is not recovered by wfmash above the
same threshold. Manual comparison with the WashU pedigree recombination
annotation indicates that this paternal transmission has no ordinary chr3
crossover (M. Cechova and T. Marschall, personal communication), so the
chr9q/chr3q signal is not explained by a standard homologous chr3 recombination
event. We therefore interpret these patterns as putative PHR-exchange
candidates compatible with recombination, not breakpoint-resolved event calls
or aligner-defined truth sets. Follow-up work should test transmission of the
implicated chromosome ends in descendant haplotypes and apply orthogonal assays
such as FISH or long-read single-gamete recombination maps
\cite{Halldorsson2019,palsson2025recomb}.

\subsection*{Discussion}

Inter-chromosomal subtelomeric sequence sharing coincides with preferential
three-dimensional proximity and with putative recombination between subtelomeric
regions on non-homologous chromosomes. Together, these point to a self-reinforcing
loop. Shared sequence tracks three-dimensional proximity. Proximity at the meiotic bouquet creates the
opportunity for ectopic recombination between non-homologous chromosomes, and
that recombination in turn generates new shared sequence that enters the
population and reinforces proximity at the next meiosis.
The 3.5 Mb of subtelomeric sequence described here therefore behaves as a
population-scale extension of the cytogenetically defined subtelomeric exchange systems
that anchor it, shaped and maintained by recurrent ectopic exchange
\cite{Dover1982,concerted_evolution_nahr_Hastings2009}.
What lives at these ends may be as consequential as the sharing itself.
Although the three duplicons illustrated in Fig.~\ref{fig:fig3} do not establish
functional enrichment across all PHRs, they intersect distinct biological programs in cleavage-stage
regulation, oocyte meiosis and sensory identity, making them hypothesis-generating
examples of what recurrent chromosome-end exchange could move. PHR communities
may therefore be distributed chromosome-end superloci that exchange not only
sequence but gene dosage, cis-regulatory DNA and nuclear-positioning information.
Interlocus gene conversion can rewrite coding exons across high-identity
segmental duplications \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Vollger2023}. C1 provides
an extreme example. D4Z4 copy number controls a long-noncoding-RNA-mediated
Polycomb/trithorax switch; an 80-bp D4Z4 element is sufficient to reposition an
adjacent telomere to the nuclear periphery; and the 4q array makes cis and trans
contacts whose regulatory network is rewired after pathogenic contraction
\cite{Cabianca2012,D4Z4_Ottaviani2009,D4Z4_Cortesi2019}. The embedded DUX4
factor activates a normal cleavage-stage program but becomes pathogenic when
derepressed in muscle and cancer
\cite{DUX4_Hendrickson2017,DUX4_Snider2010,dux4_cancer_Chew2019}. FRG2A/B
transcripts add a second trans-acting layer: they associate with centromeres
genome-wide and rDNA arrays on every acrocentric chromosome and reorganize
perinucleolar heterochromatin, functionally connecting C1 cargo to the nucleolus
\cite{FRG2_Salsi2025}. Exchange of the D4Z4--DUX4--FRG block could thus move a
developmentally gated regulatory cassette, not simply a repeat. C2 illustrates
the creative side of this process: subtelomeric tubulin duplications shifted the
functional copy between loci during ape evolution, and TUBB8 is now the
predominant beta-tubulin in human oocytes, where pathogenic variants disrupt the
meiotic spindle \cite{TUBB8_vanGeel2002,TUBB8_Feng2016}; the homologous 18p
TUBB8B block is therefore a plausible substrate through which conversion or
copy-number change could tune this germline system. C11 supplies a suggestive
analogy rather than direct functional evidence: human subtelomeric
olfactory-receptor copies can be transcribed, while in mouse olfactory neurons
dispersed receptor loci assemble multi-chromosomal hubs that control singular
receptor choice \cite{OR_Linardopoulou2001,OR_Monahan2019}. Subtelomeric RNAs
offer one possible route from sequence to proximity: PAR-TERRA can induce ectopic
chromosome pairing, while TERRA forms RAD51-dependent R-loops at telomeres in
trans \cite{TERRA_Chu2017,TERRA_Feretzaki2020}. The zygotene maximum in mouse
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}C) suggests that the bouquet is not an indiscriminate
reaction vessel but a recurrent encounter zone in which long, high-identity
PHRs may occasionally evade mechanisms that normally enforce homolog choice;
bouquet formation
restricts ectopic recombination in fission yeast
\cite{bouquet_DavisSmith2006}. In this reading, PHRs may generalize the
homology--proximity--exchange logic of nucleolus-associated acrocentrics to other
chromosome ends \cite{Guarracino2023}: shared sequence could serve both as a
repair substrate and as a nuclear address. Under this model, recurrent exchange
has the potential to accelerate sensory and germline innovation while renewing
susceptibility to infertility, muscular dystrophy, developmental disorders and
cancer.
Which way the sequence--proximity link runs is still open: whether shared
sequence drives co-localization, or enforced proximity generates it. The mechanism is likewise unresolved, split among conversion,
crossover-associated repair and larger exchange patterns.
Our human three-dimensional data are somatic rather than germline, with the
meiotic-stage measurement supplied only by mouse.
Resolving both will require germline-stage contact maps, long-read
recombination maps, and complete trio assemblies
\cite{palsson2025recomb,Cechova2025}; even so, the
pattern already provides a population-scale counterpart, at complete-genome
resolution, to relationships first inferred from cytogenetics decades ago.

\section*{Methods}
\label{secMethods}

\setcounter{figure}{0}

\subsection*{Samples, exclusions and flank extraction}

The dataset comprised 464 haplotype-resolved HPRC v2 assemblies from 232
individuals plus CHM13v2.0, for 465 near-complete assemblies, with
superpopulation labels assigned at the HPRC individual level
\cite{hprc_hprcv2_2025,Nurk2022,logsdon2025hgsvc}. Two exclusions were applied.
First, GRCh38 contigs and the CHM13v2.0 Y chromosome (masked PAR1) were excluded
from flank extraction because they lack telomere ends suitable for the 500 kb
flank definition. Second, the chr18q flank of NA18982 haplotype 1
(contig JBKABS010000018.1) was excluded as a scaffolding chimera: in the
all-against-all wfmash alignments its first 83.37 Mb map to chr18 and a terminal
966 kb (83.38--84.35 Mb) map to chrX PAR1, joined across a 100 bp gap of Ns. No
further haplotype-level exclusions were applied. From each retained haplotype
assembly we then extracted the terminal 500 kb of every contig at least 1 Mb long
that was classified as p- or q-arm telomere-bearing, giving 18,827 flanks across
the 48 arms.

\subsection*{Alignment, implicit pangenome graph and PHR detection}

We aligned all flanks all-against-all with wfmash v0.23.0 at 95\% minimum
identity, each flank serving in turn as the target \cite{Guarracino2023}, and
treated the resulting alignment set as an implicit pangenome graph whose
reachability we computed by transitive closure with IMPG
\cite{pangenome_graphs_impg_IMPG2023}. Every flank was compared against every
other without restricting to homologous chromosomes,
so the transitive closure recovers subtelomere sharing genome-wide. PHRs were
called from a sliding-window scan of the transitive closure, requiring at least
95\% identity, at least five alignments to each of at least two other
chromosomes, and a minimum aligned length of 3 kb per called region; this gave
15,668 PHRs (83.2\% of flanks) on 41 of the 48 arms.

\subsection*{Pangenome graph, similarity and community detection}

We built a pangenome graph of the 15,668 PHR sequences with pggb at 95\% identity
and computed all-pairs Jaccard similarity with odgi, giving a $15{,}668 \times
15{,}668$ matrix
\cite{Garrison2024pggb,pangenome_graphs_impg_GuarracinoHeumos2022,andreace2023pangenome,heumos2024nfcore}.
The main connected component was laid out with odgi and rendered in R
(Fig.~\ref{fig:fig2}A), each node colored by the dominant arm-level Leiden
community of the PHR paths crossing it. The arm-level distance matrix is the mean
pairwise Jaccard distance between PHR sequences within each arm pair. From it, arm-level
communities were inferred after converting distances to graph weights
$w_{ij} = \exp(-d_{ij} / \mathrm{median}(d))$ and scanning Leiden resolution from
0.1 to 3.0; we selected the maximum-silhouette partition, 15 communities at
resolution 1.16, and the same community count held across resolutions 1.13--1.18.

\subsection*{Hierarchical clustering and resampling controls}

For display and method comparison we also ordered the arm-level matrix by
average-linkage hierarchical clustering (UPGMA); cutting it into 14 clusters gave
a mean silhouette of 0.342 and exact agreement with 12 of the 15 Leiden
communities, and we use this dendrogram as a heatmap ordering, not as a
phylogenetic model. As a sensitivity analysis, we evaluated a PHR-resampling
control that resamples rows of a
collapsed per-PHR cross-chromosome involvement table, rebuilds an arm-level
surrogate distance matrix, and recomputes neighbor-joining and UPGMA trees.
Because it does not resample the full 15,668-sequence Jaccard matrix, it is not
used as evidence for the Leiden communities or for a discrete q-arm partition;
under this chromosome-level surrogate, the six-arm q-neighborhood has low
support, so we treat the q-arm pattern as an enriched neighborhood in the
similarity matrix rather than a bootstrap-supported grouping.

\subsection*{Hi-C, Pore-C and CiFi pipeline}

Hi-C, Pore-C and CiFi were reprocessed from raw reads
against the corresponding T2T reference (CHM13v2.0 or the matched HPRC v2
haplotype) with multi-mappers retained, because the default mapping-quality
filter ($\geq 30$) of deposited maps masks the high-identity, signal-bearing
subtelomeric sequence at the mapping stage. Pore-C (NlaIII) and CiFi (DpnII) were
processed with pore-c-py, minimap2 and pairtools, and Hi-C with HiC-Pro and
Bowtie2, binning contacts with cooler. Because no mapping-quality filter is
applied, a molecule mapping equally well to several paralogous ends contributes
to each of them, so PHR-internal contacts are aggregate coordinate-level
measurements rather than uniquely assigned read-level contacts. Contact matrices
were generated at 5, 10, 20, 50 and 100 kb, with maternal and paternal contigs
kept as separate arms.

Pointwise Spearman correlations are reported as effect sizes, not significance
tests, because PHR-pair observations share chromosome arms, PHRs, matrix rows and
columns, and read-placement histories. Each correlation takes one
inter-chromosomal PHR sequence pair as a point and relates its pangenome-graph
Jaccard similarity to its length-normalized contact at the pair's exact
coordinates within a single genome, at 20 kb. The primary comparison is HG002
Pore-C (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}A; $\rho = 0.376$, $n = 2{,}830$); the replicates in
Extended Data Fig.~\ref{fig:ed1} are CHM13 Hi-C ($\rho = 0.742$, $n = 688$), HG002
Hi-C ($\rho = 0.616$, $n = 2{,}413$) and HG002 CiFi ($\rho = 0.196$,
$n = 2{,}757$; the sparsest assay, consistent with the weakest effect size).

The adjacent 100 kb centromere-ward non-PHR flanks are the principal control
against mapping-quality-zero inflation: in HG002 Hi-C they give $B/W = 0.0013$
against $0.028$ in the PHR windows. Community-ordered contact matrices used
observed/expected inter-chromosomal normalization
\cite{hic3d_dixon2012,hic3d_imakaev2012,Ulahannan2019,hic3d_cifi2025}; the
community-ordered Pore-C matrix (Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}B; $B/W = 0.054$) is at 20 kb.
Matrix-level inference used row-and-column Mantel and permutation tests, with
finite permutation floors reported when no permutation exceeded the observed
statistic.

\subsection*{Mouse pipeline}

We repeated the entire pipeline on a non-human mammal, using the B6 and CAST T2T
assemblies \cite{Francis2025}. Telomere-anchored flanks were extracted and PHRs
called de novo, with a 1, 2 and 4 Mb window sweep (30 of 49 mouse PHRs saturate
the 1 Mb window) and a two-community arm-level partition. For each meiotic
prophase stage we computed the per-PHR-pair Spearman correlation between
length-normalized contact and PHR Jaccard similarity at 20 kb over 1 Mb flanks.
Subtelomeric similarity was positively correlated with inter-chromosomal contact
at all four stages, strongest at zygotene and lowest at diplotene
($\rho = 0.419$, 0.614, 0.576 and 0.245 at leptotene, zygotene, pachytene and
diplotene; all $n = 1{,}135$; Fig.~\ref{fig:fig4}C). These are point estimates
without a formal stage-contrast test; a Mantel cross-check agreed to within
$\pm 0.02$ \cite{Zuo2021}.

\subsection*{Pedigree direct-alignment windows}

For Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}B we used the PAN027 paternal haplotype as query against a
joint two-haplotype target from the transmitting father, PAN011
\cite{Cechova2025}. Starting from a whole-genome SweepGA/FastGA alignment
(frequency threshold 32, many-to-many mappings, no scaffold jumps), we filtered
to 10:10 mapping classes and summarized the query in non-overlapping 2 kb
windows, excluding centromeric windows, windows without alignment support, and
windows with inter-chromosomal alignment depth above 100. For each retained
window we computed IMPG similarity (partial-order alignment, no merge,
many-to-many mapping, no scaffold jumps) and kept the best same-chromosome or
homologous father match and the best non-homologous father match. A window was
assigned to the non-homologous class only when the non-homologous match was the
most similar. Adjacent windows were joined into a run only when the query and
donor intervals abutted end to end on the same donor chromosome, haplotype and
sequence, with no gap-tolerant or transitive merge. Colored runs are
non-homologous runs of at least 10 kb with mean inter-chromosomal identity at
least 0.95. The gray inheritance background was drawn from the same table,
joining same-chromosome father-child rows of at least 0.95 identity by the same
rule and drawing chains of at least 10 kb. PHR and community labels were assigned
after calling and were not used to select the windows.

As an independent sensitivity check, the two autosomal candidates were
re-examined in a whole-genome wfmash v0.23.0 alignment of the same PAN027 versus
PAN011 haplotypes at 95\% identity. This check was not used as a calling
criterion. wfmash reproduced the chr9q/chr3q donor on chr3q at $>$99.8\%
identity over the same coordinates but placed the chr5q window on chr5 with no
chr1 donor above threshold.

For Fig.~\ref{fig:fig5}C,D we applied the same workflow, thresholds and display
rules to two maternal comparisons: the PAN027 maternal haplotype against the
joint PAN010 mother target, and the PAN028 maternal haplotype against the joint
PAN027 mother target. The GitHub supporting-material table reports, for all
displayed calls, assembly-native query and donor intervals, 2 kb window counts,
mean identities, same-chromosome competitor scores, and post hoc PHR and
community labels; its CHM13 columns are arm-level PHR annotations, not exact
coordinate liftovers of the pedigree-specific haplotype assemblies.

\subsection*{Software versions}

Pangenome graph construction used PGGB v0.7.4, commit 498c5d7, with wfmash
v0.23.0, seqwish v0.7.11, smoothxg v0.8.2, odgi v0.9.2, GFAffix
v0.2.1 and vg v1.62.0. Flank all-against-all and pedigree whole-genome alignment
used wfmash v0.23.0, with sweepga 0.1.1 and impg 0.4.1 for whole-genome mapping
and interval projection. Arm-level communities were called with the Leiden
algorithm in R (igraph). Chromosome-contact processing ran under Python
3.12.12 (numpy 1.26.4, pandas 3.0.0, scipy 1.17.0): Pore-C with pore-c-py 2.1.5
(NlaIII), minimap2 2.30, pairtools 1.1.3, cooler 0.10.4 and samtools 1.23; CiFi
with the same pore-c-py toolchain (DpnII); Hi-C with HiC-Pro 3.0.0 and Bowtie2
2.5.3. General sequence utilities were samtools and bgzip 1.23,
bedtools 2.31.1, seqkit 2.5.0 and gffread 0.12.7. Statistical and figure analysis
used R with the packages ape, vegan and cluster.
