# Topic 06: D4Z4 Macrosatellite, DUX4 Retrogene, and FSHD Biology

**Slug:** dux4_d4z4_fshd  
**Anchor claims:** C5 (4q–10q DUX4 clade), C7 (lamin/nuclear envelope), C8 (ongoing exchange)  
**Time range:** 1990s–present

## Era Overview

Facioscapulohumeral muscular dystrophy (FSHD) is the third most common hereditary myopathy worldwide, affecting roughly 1 in 8,000 individuals. Its unusual genetic architecture — anchored in a subtelomeric macrosatellite array rather than a conventional exon — made it one of the most intellectually challenging disease loci in human genetics for nearly two decades.

The defining feature of FSHD is a polymorphic array of 3.3-kilobase tandem repeats called **D4Z4**, situated in the subtelomeric region of chromosome 4q35. In healthy individuals this array harbors 11–100 copies; FSHD patients typically carry 1–10 copies on one allele, with repeat contractions arising de novo or transmitted in autosomal dominant fashion. The earliest diagnostic work in the 1990s identified that co-segregating EcoRI restriction fragments shorter than 28 kb at 4q35 distinguished affected individuals from controls \cite{vanDeutekom1996}, but the causal gene remained unknown because the region appeared gene-poor and repetitive.

A crucial complication emerged immediately: a nearly identical repeat array exists at chromosome 10q26. The two loci share more than 98% sequence identity over the D4Z4 unit and approximately 95% homology over a flanking 42-kilobase region \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_vanGeel2002}. This homology is deep enough that inter-chromosomal exchanges — detectable by FISH as hybrid restriction fragments — occur in roughly 20% of the population \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_wijmenga1992}. Yet contraction of the 10q26 array does not cause disease, nor does contraction of the non-permissive 4qB haplotype variant. The disease-causing mechanism is therefore exquisitely haplotype-specific.

The resolution of this paradox came in stages. First came the recognition that the two major 4q variants — 4qA and 4qB — differ by the presence of a 6.2-kb beta-satellite sequence distal to D4Z4, generating different downstream polyadenylation contexts \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2002}. Second, fine mapping of sequence polymorphisms within the 4q subtelomere revealed that only specific haplotypes in the 4qA class are disease-permissive \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2007}. Third, and definitively, Lemmers et al. \citeyear{Lemmers2010} showed that each 3.3 kb D4Z4 unit encodes **DUX4**, a double homeodomain transcription factor whose mRNA is stabilized by a polyadenylation signal (pLAM) present immediately distal to D4Z4 on pathogenic 4qA chromosomes but absent on 4qB and most 10q chromosomes. This unified genetic framework explained why copy number at 10q26 is clinically irrelevant while that at 4q35(4qA) is pathogenic.

The era from the early 1990s through approximately 2012 thus constitutes the discovery arc of FSHD genetics: from chromosomal mapping, through structural characterization of D4Z4, to identification of the DUX4 transcription factor as the disease effector. Subsequent years have focused on DUX4's normal biology (roles in early embryo genome activation), the epigenetic mechanisms that silence D4Z4 arrays, and the clinical and population-genetic heterogeneity of both the 4q35 and 10q26 repeat tracts.

## Key Papers in Chronological Order

**Wijmenga et al. 1992** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_wijmenga1992} performed the foundational chromosomal mapping study. Using cosmid p13E-11, they showed that FSHD co-segregates with shortened EcoRI fragments (14–28 kb, compared to the normal >28 kb) at the 4q35 locus. Six of six new FSHD cases carried de novo rearrangements. This established the molecular diagnostic test used clinically for more than a decade and focused attention firmly on the 4q35 subtelomere.

**Hewitt et al. 1994** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_hewitt1994} sequenced the D4Z4 repeat unit and revealed its internal complexity: each 3.3 kb copy contains two homeodomain sequences (later named DUX4), GC-rich repetitive elements (hhspm3, LSau), and open reading frames. The presence of two homeoboxes hinted immediately at transcriptional regulatory potential. The study also noted D4Z4-like repeats at multiple genomic loci, with dramatic copy-number expansion within the primate lineage over the past 25 million years.

**van Deutekom et al. 1996** \cite{vanDeutekom1996} identified the first genes in the 4q35 region, including FRG1 (FSHD region gene 1), stimulating the hypothesis — now largely abandoned — that FSHD pathogenesis involves position-effect variegation of flanking genes.

**Stout et al. 1999** \cite{Stout1999} used dual-color FISH with chromosome 4- and chromosome 10-specific probes flanking D4Z4 to demonstrate that the two loci can be reliably distinguished in the context of hybrid chromosomes, clarifying the diagnostic framework for hybrid 4q/10q arrangements.

**van Geel et al. 2002** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_vanGeel2002} performed comparative genomic analysis of the 4q35 and 10q26 subtelomeres, establishing that the 4q homology on chromosome 10 extends well beyond D4Z4 — at least 42 kb proximally and ~15–25 kb distally. The finding that both arms encode D4Z4-like arrays with >99% internal unit identity, yet have different functional consequences, set the stage for haplotype-based explanations of FSHD specificity.

**Lemmers et al. 2002** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2002} demonstrated, using a large Dutch cohort, that FSHD is uniquely associated with the 4qA variant of the 4q subtelomere and not with 4qB (which differs by presence of a 6.2 kb beta-satellite). Because 4qA and 4qB alleles are equally common in the population but FSHD is essentially never linked to 4qB contractions, the distal sequence context — not simply repeat number — was established as the pathogenic determinant.

**Masny et al. 2004** \cite{Masny2004} showed that the 4q35 locus is specifically anchored at the nuclear periphery via interactions with lamin A/C, establishing that this subtelomeric domain has unusual three-dimensional genome organization. The 4q35 anchoring depends on the D4Z4 array and the surrounding chromatin structure, linking lamin biology directly to FSHD pathomechanism.

**Ottaviani et al. 2009** \cite{Ottaviani2009} demonstrated that D4Z4 acts as a CTCF- and A-type lamin-dependent insulator in normal cells: the repeat array binds CTCF and blocks enhancer-promoter communication. In FSHD patients with contracted D4Z4 arrays, this insulator activity is compromised, permitting aberrant transcription of flanking loci. This result provided a mechanistic link between D4Z4 copy number, chromatin architecture, and gene expression.

**Snider et al. 2009** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_snider2009} profiled the full repertoire of transcripts arising from D4Z4 units, identifying sense and antisense RNAs, miRNA-sized fragments, and multiple DUX4 protein isoforms (including DUX4c from the more proximal units). This work established that D4Z4 is transcriptionally active to a degree not previously appreciated and that the disease-relevant transcript — stabilized full-length DUX4 mRNA from the most distal unit — is one of several molecular outputs of the repeat array.

**Lemmers et al. 2010 (Science)** \cite{Lemmers2010} provided the definitive unifying genetic model. Full-length DUX4 mRNA from the last (most telomeric) D4Z4 unit is specifically stabilized and polyadenylated via a pLAM polyadenylation signal that exists only on disease-permissive 4qA chromosomes. D4Z4 contraction in FSHD1 increases DUX4 expression by relaxing epigenetic silencing of the contracted array. This model elegantly explained why contractions at 10q26 are benign (10q lacks the pLAM signal in most haplotypes) and why 4qB contractions do not cause disease.

**Lemmers et al. 2010 (AJHG)** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide} surveyed 4q/10q subtelomere haplotype distributions across global human populations (HGDP-CEPH panel). By exhaustive haplotype analysis they inferred that all observed 4q/10q hybrid chromosomes can be explained by just **four discrete interchromosomal sequence transfer events** in human evolutionary history. This remarkably low rate of exchange — compared to the high sequence similarity — implies that these subtelomeres are fundamentally less recombinogenic than their mutual identity would predict. All four hybrid haplotypes predate the human out-of-Africa migration.

**de Greef et al. 2010** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_degreef2010} established that D4Z4 hypomethylation is a shared epigenetic signature of both FSHD1 (contraction-dependent) and FSHD2 (contraction-independent), but with important differences: FSHD1 shows hypomethylation restricted to 4q D4Z4, while FSHD2 shows symmetric hypomethylation at both 4q and 10q, indicating a trans-acting epigenetic defect. Controls carrying D4Z4 contractions on 4qB or 10q also show local hypomethylation without disease, confirming that hypomethylation alone is insufficient for FSHD.

**Cabianca et al. 2012** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_cabianca2012} discovered DBE-T, a long noncoding RNA produced exclusively in FSHD muscle cells from the contracted D4Z4 array. DBE-T recruits the Trithorax-group protein Ash1L to the 4q35 locus, driving H3K36me2 histone modification, displacement of Polycomb complexes, and transcriptional de-repression of flanking 4q35 genes. This work revealed an ncRNA-based epigenetic switch as an additional layer of FSHD pathomechanism.

**Geng et al. 2012** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_geng2012} provided the first genome-wide characterization of DUX4 transcriptional targets in muscle cells, identifying germline genes (ZSCAN4, PRAMEF-family), endogenous retroelements (MaLR class), and immune mediators (DEFB103). DUX4 target genes are detected in FSHD muscle but not controls, directly validating the DUX4 misexpression model. The ability of DUX4 to simultaneously activate germline programs and suppress innate immunity suggested mechanisms for muscle toxicity beyond simple apoptosis.

**Lemmers et al. 2012** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2012} identified SMCHD1 mutations as the cause of FSHD2, establishing a digenic model: patients require both an SMCHD1 loss-of-function mutation (causing genome-wide D4Z4 hypomethylation) and a permissive 4qA D4Z4 allele. SMCHD1 is thus a trans-acting epigenetic maintainer of D4Z4 chromatin; its loss phenocopies the cis-acting effect of D4Z4 contraction. This work expanded the FSHD genetic framework and identified the chromatin machinery that normally silences DUX4 in somatic cells.

**Hendrickson et al. 2017** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_hendrickson2017} demonstrated that DUX4 (and its mouse ortholog Dux) are normally expressed in 4-cell (human) and 2-cell (mouse) embryos, where they activate hundreds of MERVL/HERVL retroelement-derived loci and the cleavage-specific transcriptional program. This discovery reframed DUX4's biology: rather than being a silent pseudogene in normal somatic tissues, DUX4 serves as a master regulator of zygotic genome activation, with expression in adult FSHD muscle representing pathological ectopic reactivation of an embryonic program.

**Ma et al. 2024** \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_ma2024} demonstrated proof-of-concept for CRISPR-Cas9-mediated correction of FSHD by engineering a translocation that exchanges the pathogenic 4q35 subtelomere (containing contracted D4Z4 on 4qA) with the corresponding 10q26 subtelomere (lacking the pLAM signal). The induced t(4;10) translocation generated cells with reduced DUX4 target gene expression, restored PAX7 activity, improved differentiation, and reduced oxidative stress sensitivity — establishing that subtelomeric exchange itself can revert the disease phenotype.

## Findings Most Relevant to Abstract Claims C5, C7, and C8

**Claim C5 — the 4q–10q DUX4 clade with wide copy-number diversity.** The abstract states that neighbor-joining analysis of subtelomeric similarity recovers "4q–10q DUX4 homology with wide copy-number diversity." This claim is anchored in the biology described above:

The 4q35 and 10q26 D4Z4 arrays share >99% internal unit identity \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_vanGeel2002}, making them by far the most mutually similar subtelomere pair in the human genome outside the sex chromosomes. This extreme homology drives their tight clustering in any similarity-based analysis. In the pangenome community analysis, the 4q–10q pair forms community C1, anchored by DUX4L (the D4Z4-encoded homeobox locus), with a gene-replacement score of 0.49–0.50 — reflecting partial but not complete homogenization compared to acrocentric arms.

Copy-number diversity is the most clinically salient feature of this locus: healthy individuals carry 11–100 D4Z4 repeats, FSHD patients carry 1–10, and the distribution is highly variable. At the pangenome scale, the chromatography of D4Z4 copy-number across 465 near-complete assemblies reveals extreme heterogeneity at both 4q35 and 10q26, with outlier haplotypes carrying 0–2 DUX4L copies on their own arm while the community median is ~22 — a nearly ten-fold range that exceeds most other subtelomeric loci \cite{Lemmers2010}\cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide}. This diversity is the direct product of the unequal inter- and intrachromosomal recombination events that continuously reshape repeat arrays in this extreme-homology zone.

The population genetic finding that only four discrete interchromosomal transfer events have occurred in human evolutionary history \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide}, despite the high ongoing sequence similarity, underscores a key tension: the two arms are sufficiently similar to enable frequent gene-conversion-scale exchanges but are apparently insulated from large-scale chromosomal fusions or translocations. This is consistent with the pangenome model in which PHR-scale exchanges are common while arm-level exchanges are rare \cite{MeffordTrask2002}.

**Claim C7 — lamin/nuclear envelope proximity.** The meiotic bouquet mechanism requires that recombining chromosomes co-localize at the nuclear envelope during prophase I. Two lines of evidence tie D4Z4/4q35 to lamin biology:

First, Masny et al. 2004 \cite{Masny2004} showed that 4q35.2 is specifically sequestered at the nuclear periphery through lamin A/C interactions, and that this peripheral localization is disrupted in FSHD patients in a D4Z4-copy-number-dependent manner. The peripheral anchoring is not a general subtelomeric property — other chromosome termini do not show the same degree of lamin association — making 4q35 a model for subtelomere-specific nuclear envelope tethering.

Second, Ottaviani et al. 2009 \cite{Ottaviani2009} demonstrated that D4Z4's insulator activity requires both CTCF binding and intact A-type lamin. In cells with lamin A/C mutations or D4Z4 contractions, the insulator fails, and the 4q35 domain is released from peripheral heterochromatin. This mechanistic link between CTCF, lamin, and D4Z4 chromatin organization places the 4q35 locus at the heart of nuclear-envelope-linked chromatin regulation.

The Dip-C radial positioning analysis in the pangenome project assigns Community 1 (C1, the 4q-10q D4Z4 community) a radial position of 0.732 — strongly peripheral — consistent with the lamin tethering established by Masny and Ottaviani. No other community reaches this degree of peripheral localization except PAR2 (C14, 0.840), which also has documented lamin-proximal organization.

**Claim C8 — ongoing interchromosomal exchange.** The strongest direct evidence that D4Z4-bearing subtelomeres undergo present-day exchange comes from the pedigree analysis in the companion pangenome study: a D4Z4 chr10q←chr4q gene-conversion event is directly observed at score 0.957 in the PAN028 maternal haplotype, representing transmission of a 4q-derived sequence to the 10q arm within a living pedigree. This individual-level exchange validates the population-level inference that 4q–10q transfers continue in the present.

At the molecular scale, the subtelomeric 4q/10q exchange described by Ma et al. 2024 \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_ma2024} demonstrates that CRISPR-induced exchange between the two arms generates functional hybrid chromosomes with altered disease phenotype — proving that the sequence homology is sufficient to support productive inter-arm recombination even when initiated artificially. In nature, the same homology drives spontaneous gene conversion and segmental duplication events that are the mechanistic basis of the FSHD copy-number spectrum.

## How This Topic Intersects Adjacent Topics

**Topic 03 (pseudohomologous regions concept) and Topic 05 (acrocentric short arms).** The D4Z4 locus is the canonical example of a subtelomeric PHR: a region of extended inter-chromosomal homology arising from past exchange events and sustained by ongoing gene conversion. The pangenome analysis formalizes this: C1 (chr4q–chr10q) is one of 15 arm-level communities in the Leiden partition, distinguished by the DUX4L homeobox marker and by the high D4Z4 copy-number variance. The community model is directly supported by FSHD genetics, which independently established that these two arms exchange sequences at detectable rates. Acrocentric p-arms (Topic 05) represent the extreme end of homogenization (gene-replacement scores 0.91–1.0; allele-vs-paralog distance reversed), while the D4Z4 C1 community occupies an intermediate position (score 0.49–0.50), reflecting partial rather than complete inter-arm identity.

**Topic 11 (pedigree-based recombination detection).** The chr10q←chr4q gene conversion observed in the PAN028 maternal haplotype (score 0.957 in the pedigree analysis) directly connects Topic 06 to Topic 11. This is not merely a statistical inference but a molecularly resolved recent exchange event: a stretch of chr10q sequence in PAN028 carries the 4q-specific flanking signature, consistent with a gene-conversion event occurring within the last two generations. The FSHD literature independently documented 4q/10q hybrid chromosomes by Southern blot in roughly 20% of the population \cite{Stout1999}, but the pangenome pedigree evidence provides haplotype-phased, sequence-resolved confirmation at a much finer scale.

**Topic 07 (Hi-C and nuclear architecture) / C7.** The nuclear-envelope tethering of 4q35 established by Masny and Ottaviani is the molecular mechanism underlying the Hi-C signal that defines C7. When the pangenome Hi-C analysis recovers elevated inter-community contact between chr4q and chr10q (relative to randomly chosen pairs), this reflects the same proximity that Masny 2004 documented by immunofluorescence: both arms are lamin-proximal, and their shared peripheral localization is the geometrical prerequisite for the ectopic exchange events that maintain their sequence similarity. The meiotic-bouquet data in the pangenome project (Mantel rho = 0.715 at zygotene for mouse) provides cross-species confirmation that lamin-proximal arms are preferentially co-localized during the precise window when meiotic recombination occurs.

**Topic 12 (DUX4 functional biology / zygotic genome activation).** The discovery that DUX4 is a master regulator of zygotic genome activation \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_hendrickson2017} reframes the subtelomeric D4Z4 array as the locus of an ancestral totipotency program rather than an irrelevant repeat. The evolutionary conservation of the DUX4 homeodomain — including the near-identical D4Z4-encoded DUX4 at 4q35 and 10q26 — becomes interpretable as purifying selection on this embryonic function. The fact that DUX4 copies also exist at other genomic loci (encoded by D4Z4-like arrays; Hewitt 1994), and that 10q26 D4Z4 units are transcribed but fail to produce stable mRNA (due to absent pLAM), provides a natural experiment showing how polyadenylation context — a few kilobases of distal sequence — determines whether a retrogene is expressed as disease effector or silenced.

## Open Questions and Recent Developments

Several fundamental questions remain unresolved even after three decades of FSHD research, and the field has been substantially reshaped by long-read sequencing technologies since 2017.

**D4Z4 array architecture at nucleotide resolution.** Until recently, the size and methylation state of D4Z4 arrays were assessed by Southern blot — a technology that provides only fragment-length information and requires substantial DNA. Long-read sequencing changed this: Snider et al. had already noted the complexity of transcripts from individual D4Z4 units, and recent nanopore-based approaches (D4Z4End2End, Genome Research 2026) have achieved full-span sequencing of arrays up to 40 repeat units (~132 kb), revealing allele-specific methylation gradients, in-cis duplications, and complex rearrangements invisible to Southern blot. The 2024 complex rearrangement paper identified 147 atypical 4q35/10q26 configurations in a cohort of 2,363 individuals — including mosaicism and cis-duplications — emphasizing that the binary "contracted vs. normal" view underestimates the diversity of this locus.

**Mechanism of DUX4 toxicity in muscle.** DUX4 activates a stereotyped germline/cleavage-stage program that is highly toxic to differentiated cells. Geng et al. 2012 \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_geng2012} identified DEFB103 (inhibits muscle regeneration) and retroelement activation as toxicity pathways; Hendrickson et al. 2017 \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_hendrickson2017} placed DUX4 in the context of normal embryonic genome activation. Whether FSHD muscle damage reflects primarily apoptosis, cell-cycle re-entry, immune activation, or failed regeneration remains unresolved. The feedback loop between DUX4 and NMD (nonsense-mediated decay) — in which DUX4 protein degrades UPF1, stabilizing DUX4 mRNA — adds another regulatory layer that may explain why DUX4 expression is sporadic (a few muscle nuclei per biopsy) yet clinically penetrant.

**The 4q–10q exchange paradox.** The worldwide population analysis \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_lemmers2010worldwide} found only four interchromosomal transfer events in human evolutionary history, despite ongoing similarity. This is paradoxical: if the homology is high enough to support gene conversion at the nucleotide scale (as seen in the pedigree analysis), why is arm-level transfer so rare? One possibility is that the functional consequence of a 10q→4qA transfer is lethal or severely fitness-reducing (because it converts a nonpathogenic 10q into a potentially pathogenic contracted 4qA). Alternatively, the size of the homology region (42 kb of flanking homology) may be insufficient to drive reciprocal translocation at appreciable frequency while still supporting short-track gene conversion. Distinguishing these models requires population-scale haplotype-resolved sequencing across diverse cohorts.

**Therapeutic implications of subtelomeric exchange.** The proof-of-concept CRISPR study by Ma et al. 2024 \cite{dux4_d4z4_fshd_ma2024} demonstrated that forced 4q/10q translocation reverts the FSHD phenotype in patient-derived myoblasts, validating the mechanistic model and opening a therapeutic avenue. However, off-target translocations and the challenge of delivering CRISPR to muscle fibers at scale remain formidable obstacles. An alternative strategy — epigenetic silencing of DUX4 by restoration of D4Z4 methylation (e.g., via SMCHD1 overexpression or DNMT3B recruitment) — is also under active investigation.

**D4Z4 copy-number and population diversity.** The pangenome analysis of 465 near-complete assemblies reveals that D4Z4 copy-number distribution at both 4q35 and 10q26 spans a range far broader than previously appreciated from Southern blot surveys. The copy-number at 10q26, in particular, has been historically underreported because it is clinically irrelevant — but in the context of subtelomeric evolution, 10q26 D4Z4 copy-number diversity is a direct readout of the rate and direction of 4q↔10q exchange. Long-read assemblies from global cohorts will eventually allow inference of the mutation rate of D4Z4 contraction/expansion as a function of haplotype background, linking the FSHD mutation rate to the broader model of subtelomeric recombination dynamics.
