# Topic 07: Concerted Evolution Mechanism and Non-Allelic Homologous Recombination, 1979–present

## Claim map

This review anchors two abstract claims. **C8** is the title-thesis claim: "ongoing recombination shapes human subtelomeres → concerted evolution." **C5** is the mechanistic claim: named interchromosomal similarity communities (10p–18p, acrocentric p-arms, D4Z4 at 4q–10q, etc.) whose boundaries require an explanation in terms of which chromosomal substrates actually recombine and why. A secondary connection runs to **C4** (extended interchromosomal homology at nearly all subtelomeres), because the very existence of that homology implies a history of NAHR-mediated duplication followed by ongoing gene-conversion maintenance. The literature covered here provides the mechanistic vocabulary needed to interpret every one of these claims: concerted evolution in its strict sense (Dover's molecular drive, fixation through genomic turnover), in its loose sense (ongoing recombination exchange maintaining similarity communities), and the molecular machinery—NAHR, gene conversion, BIR, and GC-biased gene conversion—that drives both.

## Era overview

The intellectual history of concerted evolution as a formal concept runs from the late 1970s through the mid-1990s, overlapping with but largely preceding the sequencing-scale discovery of segmental duplications that now anchors it to subtelomeric biology.

The earliest clear empirical signal came from ribosomal DNA. By the early 1980s it was apparent that the hundreds of tandemly repeated rRNA gene copies within a species were far more similar to one another than to rDNA copies in related species—a pattern that natural selection and drift alone could not explain. Arnheim and colleagues demonstrated, from restriction-enzyme analysis of human and ape rDNA, that genetic exchanges among nucleolus organizers on nonhomologous chromosomes must have repeatedly shuffled variants across multiple chromosomal loci \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Arnheim1980}. This established the key phenomenological facts: intraspecific homogeneity coexists with interspecific divergence, and the homogeneity is maintained by recurrent inter-locus exchange rather than purifying selection.

Dover formalized the mechanism in 1982, coining "molecular drive" and placing "concerted evolution" at its center \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Dover1982}. His Nature paper argued that fixation of mutations could proceed through genomic mechanisms—gene conversion, unequal crossing-over, transposition, slippage replication, and RNA-mediated sequence transfer—independently of selection and drift. This is the strict sense of concerted evolution that the present manuscript's lead author has explicitly distinguished from the loose usage in the current abstract. In Dover's strict sense, a mutation must spread through the entire family and then fix in the population; the rDNA repeat system is canonical because both stages can be tracked. In the loose sense, ongoing inter-locus exchange maintains sequence similarity among communities without necessarily driving any single variant to population fixation.

Theoretical population genetics began formalizing gene conversion's contribution in parallel. Ohta's 1984 models examined how identity coefficients within multigene families reach equilibrium under the balance of diversification by mutation and homogenization by unequal crossing-over and gene conversion \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Ohta1984}. These models showed that gene conversion could be a more powerful homogenizing agent than unequal crossover under realistic assumptions, and they predicted the observed excess of intraspecific over interspecific similarity. Charlesworth and colleagues extended the population-genetics treatment by analyzing how population size, family size, and the ratio of gene-conversion to mutation rates jointly determine steady-state divergence among paralogs \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Charlesworth1994}. These theoretical contributions are the foundation for interpreting quantitative measures—like the within-community versus between-community Jaccard distances in the current manuscript—as reflecting the dynamics of exchange rather than the outcomes of natural selection.

By the early 1990s, evidence that gene conversion was GC-biased had appeared in rDNA systems, foreshadowing a broader mechanism. Hillis et al. demonstrated that concerted evolution in salamander rDNA proceeded primarily through biased gene conversion rather than unequal crossing-over, and that the bias favored GC alleles \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Hillis1991}. This finding linked the homogenization mechanism to the isochore structure of vertebrate genomes and began a line of work culminating in the modern gBGC framework.

Liao's 1999 review synthesized the pre-genomics era \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Liao1999}, bringing together the rDNA canonical case, multigene families in general, and the molecular machinery. It is notable for the clarity with which it distinguishes gene conversion (short-tract, non-reciprocal replacement) from unequal crossing-over (reciprocal, changes copy number) and argues that both contribute to concerted evolution, with gene conversion dominant in dispersed families and unequal crossing-over dominant in tandem arrays. The review laid the conceptual groundwork that the human genomics community drew on when segmental duplications emerged as the next major arena for concerted evolution.

## Key papers in chronological order

**Arnheim et al. 1980 (PNAS)** used restriction mapping across human, chimpanzee, gorilla, and orangutan to show that rDNA variants spread between nonhomologous nucleolus organizer chromosomes within each species lineage \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Arnheim1980}. This was the first genomic-context evidence for inter-chromosomal gene conversion in primates, directly relevant because subtelomeric exchange follows the same logic: nonhomologous chromosomes sharing sequence due to recurrent inter-locus exchange.

**Dover 1982 (Nature)** coined the field's organizing vocabulary \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Dover1982}. The molecular-drive framework united several previously separate observations (rDNA homogeneity, satellite DNA concurrence, transposon spreading) into a single theory with speciation implications. For the present manuscript, the critical contribution is the distinction between spread-within-genome and fixation-within-population; the pedigree analysis (C8) provides direct evidence for the within-generation spread step in human subtelomeres.

**Ohta 1984 (Genetics)** and **Charlesworth et al. 1994 (Genetics)** provided quantitative population-genetics frameworks for gene conversion in multigene families \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Ohta1984, concerted_evolution_nahr_Charlesworth1994}. These papers allow formal comparison between observed inter-community divergence and within-community homogeneity in the HPRC data.

**Hillis et al. 1991 (Science)** demonstrated GC-biased gene conversion as the dominant mechanism of rDNA concerted evolution in vertebrates \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Hillis1991}. Their result foreshadowed the broader gBGC field and specifically predicts that gene conversion at subtelomeric hotspots should be GC-biased—a prediction testable with the HPRC pangenome.

**Liao 1999 (Am J Hum Genet)** synthesized the pre-genomics literature and placed the rDNA model in a multigene-family context \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Liao1999}. It remains a standard reference for distinguishing strict from loose concerted evolution and for tract-length distinctions (short-tract gene conversion versus long-tract events driven by BIR).

**Lupski 1998 (Trends Genet)** introduced the "genomic disorders" framework and established the conceptual link between low-copy repeats (LCRs) and recurrent NAHR-mediated rearrangements \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Lupski1998}. The CMT1A duplication flanked by CMT1A-REP elements was the founding case: LCRs of ≥97% identity flanking a dosage-sensitive interval predispose to meiotic misalignment and deletion or duplication via NAHR.

**Stankiewicz & Lupski 2002 (Trends Genet)** systematized NAHR as a mechanism responsible for more than 25 recurrent genomic disorders, with LCR length, sequence identity, orientation, and inter-LCR distance as the key determinants of NAHR frequency \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_StankiewiczLupski2002}. This paper is the standard NAHR reference for subtelomeric biology: the same principles that produce CMT1A duplications produce inter-chromosomal subtelomeric transfers.

**Eichler 2001 (Nat Rev Genet)** reviewed the rapidly expanding evidence that segmental duplications were organizing principles of human genome instability, linking pericentromeric and subtelomeric SD enrichment to NAHR hotspots at the population scale \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Eichler2001}. **Samonte & Eichler 2002 (Nat Rev Genet)** then placed primate segmental duplications in evolutionary context, showing that the burst of interchromosomal duplications in the great-ape lineage was bimodal: a pericentromeric wave and a subtelomeric wave, each feeding subsequent NAHR \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_SamonteEichler2002}.

**Rozen et al. 2003 (Nature)** demonstrated abundant gene conversion between the inverted arms of Y chromosome palindromes \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Rozen2003}. This is the closest single-chromosome analogue to the subtelomeric exchange detected in the pedigree: the palindrome arms share 99.97% identity across human–chimpanzee comparisons, maintained by frequent within-haplotype arm-to-arm conversion. The Y palindrome system is effectively a single-chromosome laboratory for the concerted evolution mechanism that the current paper documents across 41 subtelomeric arms.

**Duret & Galtier 2009 (Annu Rev Genomics Hum Genet)** synthesized the evidence that gene conversion is GC-biased in mammals and that this bias shapes the isochore structure and apparent positive selection signatures in the human genome \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_DuretGaltier2009}. The gBGC framework predicts that subtelomeric gene-conversion hotspots—where meiotic recombination rates and heteroduplex formation are elevated—should show systematic AT→GC substitution biases, a prediction testable with the HPRC haplotype data.

**Gu et al. 2011 (Am J Hum Genet)** provided the quantitative model for NAHR frequency as a function of LCR length, arguing that ectopic synapsis must precede ectopic crossing-over, and that the probability of ectopic synapsis scales with homology length \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Gu2011}. This model directly predicts the community structure observed in the current manuscript: longer and more similar subtelomeric segments (PAR1, acrocentric p-arms, D4Z4) should show the highest exchange rates, which they do.

**Chen et al. 2013 (PLoS One)** systematically mapped interlocus gene conversion signals in human segmental duplications and found that one-quarter of paralog families carry clear IGC footprints, with 1477 conversion tracts totaling 1.54 Mb \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Chen2013}. Subtelomeric sequences contributed to the interchromosomal fraction, confirming that gene conversion is the ongoing mechanism of subtelomeric similarity maintenance.

**Benovoy & Drouin 2009 (Genomics)** provided genome-wide estimates of ectopic gene conversion rates in segmental duplications, finding that IGC outpaces point mutation by an order of magnitude in duplicated regions \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Benovoy2009}. This rate differential is the quantitative basis for treating gene conversion as the dominant evolutionary force in subtelomeric paralogs.

## Findings most relevant to claims C8 and C5 of the abstract

**Claim C8** — "These data establish that human subtelomeres are unified by ongoing recombination and concerted evolution" — rests on the pedigree analysis as its direct proof (538 inter-chromosomal patches in the WashU T2T pedigree, 92% within Leiden communities, including 133 gene-conversion-like events at perfect alignment scores; chapter 14 of the end-to-end report). The concerted-evolution literature provides the interpretive framework for those events.

The critical conceptual link is the distinction between strict and loose concerted evolution. In Dover's strict sense, concerted evolution requires that a mutation spread through the entire gene family *and* fix in the population; rDNA homogeneity is the model case because both the spread (intra-chromosomal exchange) and fixation (population-level) can be verified \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Dover1982, concerted_evolution_nahr_Liao1999}. The loose sense—used by the current manuscript's lead author—requires only that inter-locus exchange maintains sequence communities across chromosomes without necessarily fixing any particular variant. The 133 pedigree gene-conversion-like events are unambiguously in the loose-sense category: patches of recent sequence transfer, confined within similarity communities, transmitted across one to three generations. They are the observed exchange events that the rDNA literature predicted in principle but could never directly observe in humans at subtelomeres.

The NAHR framework (Lupski 1998; Stankiewicz & Lupski 2002) is necessary to explain how the similarity communities formed in the first place \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Lupski1998, concerted_evolution_nahr_StankiewiczLupski2002}. Subtelomeric segmental duplications, with lengths of 50–500 kb and sequence identities often exceeding 95%, are canonical NAHR substrates: exactly the LCR size and identity range associated with CMT1A and other well-characterized genomic disorders. The community structure detected by Leiden clustering maps directly onto the expected NAHR substrates: community C7 (acrocentric p-arms) has nearly complete interchangeability (98%+ cross-arm affinity) because the rDNA-flanking sequences provide very long, very similar repeats; community C1 (4q–10q D4Z4) has partial interchangeability because the D4Z4 macrosatellite provides the NAHR substrate but the flanking sequence identity is lower. The model predicts the community structure.

**Claim C5** — named clades in the cladistic analysis — is supported by the NAHR and gene-conversion literature in a more specific way. The 10p–18p community (C2 in the Leiden partition) was already identified by Linardopoulou et al. as a pair with particularly strong paralogy and evidence for repeated interchromosomal transfer \cite{Linardopoulou2005}. The frequency of non-allelic homologous recombination scales with the length and identity of the flanking LCRs (Gu et al. 2011), so the most tightly bound communities should be those with the longest shared PHR intervals at highest identity—which matches the observation that C2 (chr10p, chr18p) shows the highest per-pair cross-arm affinity among non-PAR, non-acrocentric communities. The gene-conversion hotspot dynamics at Y palindromes (Rozen et al. 2003) provide a mechanistic precedent for how such tight community identity is maintained: the same arm-to-arm conversion that keeps Y palindrome arms at 99.97% identity can maintain interchromosomal subtelomeric pairs at >95% once an initial NAHR duplication establishes the substrate.

What is contested in the literature is whether these exchange events are meiotic or somatic, and whether they are predominantly crossover or gene conversion. The pedigree analysis in the current manuscript partially resolves this: 16 crossover-like events versus 133 gene-conversion-like events, with the conversion events at near-perfect (1.000/1.000) alignment scores, argues strongly for gene conversion as the dominant ongoing mechanism. This aligns with the NAHR literature (gene conversion via SDSA is more frequent than crossover-forming NAHR) and with the rDNA concerted-evolution literature (Liao 1999; Hillis et al. 1991: gene conversion dominates in the absence of tandem-repeat copy-number change). The 16 crossover-like events are consistent with a subset of subtelomeric PHRs having elevated crossover rates—particularly those in communities with the longest homology stretches.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

**Topic 03 (PHR concept)** is the structural substrate topic: PHRs are the actual DNA sequences on which the molecular mechanisms covered here operate. The two-domain Flint/Mefford model (Flint et al. 1997) predicts that inter-chromosomal sharing should decrease with distance from the telomere because only the most distal, longest PHRs maintain the homology necessary for efficient NAHR and gene conversion (Gu et al. 2011). The HPRC pangenome confirms this: 39/48 arms show monotonically decreasing sharing with distance, and the two-domain breakpoints correspond to the transition from NAHR-competent to NAHR-incompetent sequence lengths. The PHR concept provides the "what" (sequence structure); the concerted evolution and NAHR literature provides the "how" (mechanism) and "why" (exchange dynamics).

**Topic 11 (pedigree-based recombination detection)** is where the mechanism becomes directly observable. The 133 gene-conversion-like events in the WashU T2T pedigree and the 11 cross-assembler-validated CEPH1463 events are the modern empirical instances of the gene conversion process that rDNA biologists documented in the 1980s and 1990s. The key methodological point is that the "sandwich pattern" used to classify gene-conversion-like events (a patch of foreign chromosome sequence embedded between blocks of native sequence) is exactly what Liao's 1999 review described as the molecular signature of a short-tract gene conversion event: non-reciprocal, leaving the donor locus unchanged while overwriting a stretch of the recipient \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Liao1999}. The pedigree analysis is, in this sense, a direct empirical test of the theoretical framework built around rDNA 40 years earlier.

**Topic 04 (sex chromosome PARs)** represents the extreme end of the NAHR spectrum. PAR1 and PAR2 are defined by an obligate meiotic crossover in PAR1 (Rouyer et al. 1986) and by the sequence homology maintained by repeated crossover and gene conversion in PAR2. The PAR communities (C14, C15 in the Leiden partition) show 99.7% cross-arm affinity—essentially complete sequence interchangeability—which is the expected outcome when NAHR crossover is obligate and gene conversion is frequent. This is concerted evolution in the strict sense for PAR1: the obligate crossover ensures spread through every meiosis, and the resulting Y-to-X (or X-to-Y) gene conversion is fixed in essentially every generation. The PAR system is the limiting case of what the rest of the subtelomeric exchange communities approach at varying rates.

## Open questions and recent developments

Several questions remain actively contested or newly opened by the T2T/pangenome era.

**BIR versus SDSA in subtelomeric gene conversion.** The rDNA literature (Liao 1999; Hastings et al. 2009) distinguishes short-tract gene conversion (SDSA: synthesis-dependent strand annealing, typically <1 kb) from long-tract events driven by BIR (break-induced replication, extending hundreds of kilobases) \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Liao1999, concerted_evolution_nahr_Hastings2009}. The pedigree analysis reports gene-conversion-like tracts with a "sandwich" classification (score 0.81–1.00), but does not yet directly measure tract lengths. The 96 events at perfect score suggest that at least some tracts are long enough to convert an entire PHR region (median 105 kb), which would require BIR rather than SDSA. Resolving this distinction matters for understanding the mechanism: BIR is error-prone and Pol32/POLD3-dependent, while SDSA is high-fidelity. The current data are consistent with both, and future analysis of allele-specific changes within conversion tracts could distinguish them.

**GC-biased gene conversion at subtelomeric hotspots.** The gBGC framework (Duret & Galtier 2009) predicts that recombination hotspots in the germ line should show systematic AT→GC substitution biases \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_DuretGaltier2009}. Subtelomeric regions have elevated GC content generally, and PHR sequences with recent exchange signals show GC content slightly above the arm average. Population-scale phased haplotype data from HPRC would allow a formal test: do positions within community-specific PHRs show signatures of gBGC (excess derived GC alleles, correlated with recombination rate)? This has not been done and represents an accessible but high-value follow-up analysis.

**PRDM9 and the boundary of allelic versus non-allelic crossover.** PRDM9 determines the positions of meiotic recombination hotspots via its zinc-finger domain \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Myers2010}. NAHR crossover hotspots share features with allelic recombination hotspots (small, DSB-initiated, PRDM9-binding-motif enriched). The question for subtelomeres is whether the inter-chromosomal crossover events (16 crossover-like events in the pedigree) occur at PRDM9-determined hotspots within the PHR sequences, or whether they occur preferentially at subtelomere-specific structural features (D4Z4 junctions, CTCF binding sites, internal telomeric repeats). If they are PRDM9-dependent, the crossover hotspot landscape within subtelomeric PHRs should be mappable from the HPRC variation data. The rapid evolution of PRDM9 binding motifs means that cross-population differences in hotspot positions could contribute to the population-stratified subtelomeric exchange frequencies observed in the HPRC analysis.

**Distinguishing strict and loose concerted evolution operationally.** The current manuscript uses "concerted evolution" in the loose sense (lead author clarification: "ongoing recombination exchange; see pedigree analysis for proof"). The risk is conflation with the strict sense in peer review. The cleanest operational distinction is: strict concerted evolution requires demonstrating that an exchange event has spread to approach fixation in the population at a particular locus; loose concerted evolution requires only that inter-locus similarity is maintained by ongoing exchange at a rate exceeding mutation. The HPRC data provide both: the chr3_q community (C3 in the f7501 cluster) shows nearly complete population-level homogeneity (within-community variance 2.8× lower than chr15_q), which approaches the strict-sense outcome; while the D4Z4 community (C1) shows 43.4% type discordance and incomplete homogenization—more consistent with the loose sense. Future pangenome analyses tracking haplotype-specific exchange signatures across multiple generations should be able to formally test which communities are approaching population-level fixation and which are in a dynamic exchange equilibrium.

**T2T and the newly resolved subtelomeric repeat catalog.** Recent T2T assemblies have resolved many previously inaccessible subtelomeric regions, particularly the acrocentric p-arm rDNA arrays, the D4Z4 macrosatellite, and the PAR2 boundary \cite{concerted_evolution_nahr_Nurk2022T2T, concerted_evolution_nahr_Gao2023}. This resolution enables direct measurement of gene-conversion tract lengths at individual loci, comparison of allelic versus paralogous distances at sequence resolution, and detection of NAHR breakpoints that were previously in assembly gaps. Gao et al. (2023) demonstrated that mutation rates and interlocus gene conversion are both elevated within human segmental duplications compared to unique regions, confirming that the processes described in the concerted evolution literature are measurably elevated in precisely the genomic compartment where subtelomeric PHRs reside.

The key open challenge is bridging the mechanistic literature (which is built primarily from yeast genetic experiments and specific locus studies like CMT1A and Y palindromes) to the population-scale statistical patterns in the HPRC data. The pedigree analysis is the crucial bridge: it provides human in vivo event-by-event observations of the same molecular events—gene conversion tracts, crossover patches, NAHR-like exchanges—that the mechanistic literature has characterized at single loci. The combined framework argues that human subtelomeres are not merely fossils of ancient duplication but are actively maintained—in the strict biological sense of "maintained"—by the same molecular machinery that rDNA biologists described as concerted evolution four decades ago.
