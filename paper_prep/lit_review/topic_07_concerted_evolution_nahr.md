# Topic 07: Concerted evolution, NAHR, and gene conversion, 1976-present

## Claim map

This review supports two abstract claims directly. **C8** ("ongoing recombination shapes these dynamic regions" and the title term "concerted evolution") is the title-thesis: the loose-sense usage adopted by the lead author (Erik Garrison clarification, 2026-05-06) treats subtelomeres as substrates of ongoing inter-arm exchange, and the literature in this topic supplies the mechanistic vocabulary — molecular drive, NAHR, gene conversion, BIR — that justifies that usage. **C5** (mechanism / community structure inferred from sequence) is supported insofar as the named clades (10p–18p, 4q–10q, acrocentric short arms, the {22q,21q,19q,1q,13q,17q} group) are exactly the kind of paralogous community that NAHR + gene conversion are predicted to maintain at high pairwise identity. The literature does not directly anchor C2-C4 (those are HPRC-scale + pipeline claims) and only indirectly anchors C6-C7 (via the prediction that ectopic exchange requires physical proximity).

A precise scope note matters: the abstract uses "concerted evolution" in the **loose** sense — *ongoing recombination exchange among paralogs that keeps them more similar than neutral expectation* — not the strict Dover sense (a homogenization mechanism that operates faster than allelic divergence). This review distinguishes the two usages explicitly because the strict sense was coined for tandem rDNA arrays and the loose sense for any dispersed, recombination-coupled paralog system. Both usages are present in the literature; only the loose sense is load-bearing for the abstract.

## Era overview

Five eras structure the topic. **(i) 1972–1982: discovery and naming.** Brown's Xenopus rDNA work [@Brown1972] and Smith's unequal-crossover model [@Smith1976] showed that tandem repeats are homogenized within species but diverge between species, and Dover named this pattern *molecular drive* and gave it a process-level identity distinct from selection and drift [@Dover1982; @Dover1986]. **(ii) 1985–1999: mechanistic synthesis.** Liao consolidated the field into a single review and articulated unequal crossing-over and gene conversion as the two principal molecular mechanisms [@Liao1999]. The Dover/Liao framework treats concerted evolution as a population-genetic phenomenon: paralogs do not evolve independently because intra- and inter-array exchange couples their fates. **(iii) 1998–2007: NAHR and the genomic-disorders frame.** Lupski showed that recurrent disease-causing rearrangements have predictable breakpoints at large segmental duplications (SDs) and named the underlying process Non-Allelic Homologous Recombination [@Lupski1998; @StankiewiczLupski2002; @LupskiStankiewicz2005]. Bailey and Eichler formalised the architectural argument: recent SDs are crucibles of evolution and disease because homology between them feeds NAHR [@BaileyEichler2006]. Chen et al. consolidated gene conversion specifically [@ChenCooper2007]. **(iv) 2008–2015: quantitative gene-conversion biology.** Llorente et al. and Anand et al. characterised break-induced replication (BIR) as a third mechanism distinct from synthesis-dependent strand annealing [@Llorente2008; @Anand2013]. Galtier and Duret quantified GC-biased gene conversion (gBGC) and tied it to mammalian isochore structure [@Galtier2001; @DuretGaltier2009]. Jeffreys and May measured tract lengths in human crossover hotspots [@JeffreysMay2004]. **(v) 2019–present: pedigree-resolution measurement.** deCODE and population pedigrees produced sequence-level genetic maps and direct gene-conversion calls [@Halldorsson2019; @Williams2015]. Nei and Rooney's birth-and-death model offered an alternative to concerted evolution for some multigene families [@NeiRooney2005], a cautionary note this manuscript adopts in its loose-sense framing.

## Key papers in chronological order

The canonical lineage begins with **Smith 1976** [@Smith1976], who derived from population-genetics first principles that unequal crossing-over among repeats produces simultaneous fixation across the array and divergence between species — the formal substrate of concerted evolution. **Brown 1972** had already shown the empirical pattern in Xenopus rDNA [@Brown1972]. **Dover 1982** named the process molecular drive and argued it should be added to the modern synthesis as a third mode alongside selection and drift [@Dover1982; @Dover1986]. **Liao 1999** is the field's compact synthesis, used here to anchor mechanism: unequal crossover and gene conversion as the two recombination-coupled engines [@Liao1999]. **Stankiewicz and Lupski 2002** is the founding NAHR paper, drawing the connection from yeast ectopic recombination to human disease loci with flanking SD architecture [@StankiewiczLupski2002], with the broader genomic-disorders framework built up across [@Lupski1998; @LupskiStankiewicz2005; @Lupski2009]. **Bailey and Eichler 2006** [@BaileyEichler2006] argued that primate SDs are evolutionarily young, structurally biased toward pericentromeric and subtelomeric locations, and recombinationally active — directly relevant to subtelomeres as a high-NAHR class. **Chen et al. 2007** [@ChenCooper2007] is the gene-conversion review of record, treating mechanisms (synthesis-dependent strand annealing, BIR), disease examples and tract-length distributions. **Hastings et al. 2009** [@Hastings2009] is the canonical synthesis of mechanisms of change in gene copy number, integrating NAHR, NHEJ, FoSTeS/MMBIR. **Eickbush and Eickbush 2007** [@EickbushEickbush2007] provides the rDNA case study: total repeat homogenization in real time, with the mechanism mapped onto recombination at the ribosomal locus. **Ganley and Kobayashi 2007** [@GanleyKobayashi2007] offered the first whole-genome quantification of intra-array homogenization rates. **Nei and Rooney 2005** [@NeiRooney2005] introduced the birth-and-death alternative — important because the abstract's loose-sense usage explicitly avoids the strict Dover/Liao claim that homogenization is faster than divergence. **Galtier et al. 2001** [@Galtier2001] and **Duret and Galtier 2009** [@DuretGaltier2009] established that gene conversion is GC-biased, with population-genetic and isochore consequences. **Jeffreys and May 2004** [@JeffreysMay2004] measured human gene-conversion tract length at ~50–500 bp from sperm crossover hotspots. **Williams et al. 2015** [@Williams2015] and **Halldorsson et al. 2019** [@Halldorsson2019] produced direct pedigree-resolved gene conversion calls at sequence resolution. The two BIR papers [@Llorente2008; @Anand2013] complete the mechanism inventory: long-tract synthesis from one homologous template can copy hundreds of kilobases without crossover — the operationally relevant mechanism for kilobase-scale gene conversion patches that the present manuscript reports in pedigrees (Andrea's chapter 14: 133 gene-conversion-like patches).

## Findings most relevant to claims C5 and C8

For **C8**, the literature contributes three pieces. First, the Dover/Liao framework legitimizes the loose-sense usage of "concerted evolution" in the title: ongoing inter-arm recombination among paralogs is exactly the kind of process Dover called *molecular drive*, even when the paralogs are dispersed (subtelomeres) rather than tandem (rDNA) [@Dover1982; @Liao1999]. Second, NAHR-with-segmental-duplication architecture predicts subtelomeres specifically: Bailey and Eichler showed that human SDs are pericentromeric and subtelomeric in their preferred location [@BaileyEichler2006], and Stankiewicz and Lupski showed that flanking SD architecture is the structural substrate of NAHR [@StankiewiczLupski2002]. Third, the pedigree-resolved gene-conversion measurements at ~50–500 bp tracts [@JeffreysMay2004] and the kilobase-scale BIR tracts [@Anand2013] together cover the size range observed in Andrea's chapter 14 pedigree analysis, where 538 patches range from a few kb up to hundreds of kb in 41 chromosome arms. The manuscript's 92% within-community concordance is the population-scale signature predicted by the NAHR + gene-conversion framework: exchange happens preferentially among already-similar sequences. Halldorsson et al.'s 2019 sequence-level genetic map [@Halldorsson2019] is the closest pedigree-resolution external comparator, although it does not focus on subtelomeres.

For **C5**, the named clades fit NAHR predictions in detail. The 4q–10q D4Z4 community has a documented ~20% inter-arm exchange rate [@vanDeutekom1996], directly characteristic of NAHR. The acrocentric short-arm community (chr13_p, 14_p, 15_p, 21_p, 22_p) carries the rDNA arrays whose homogenization is the canonical strict-sense concerted-evolution case [@Brown1972; @EickbushEickbush2007; @GanleyKobayashi2007]. The 10p–18p clade matches Linardopoulou's hot-spot pair [@Linardopoulou2005]. The {22q, 21q, 19q, 1q, 13q, 17q} community is consistent with multiple inter-q-arm SD blocks of the Bailey/Eichler type, although a published mechanism for this specific clade is not on record. The chr16q polymorphism with f7501 distribution [@MeffordTrask2002] is the kind of low-frequency NAHR product Stankiewicz and Lupski 2002 predicted at SD-flanked loci. Gene-conversion GC bias [@DuretGaltier2009] also explains why subtelomeric communities tend to retain GC-rich block structure. None of these mechanistic predictions were tested at HPRC scale before; the present manuscript provides that test.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

This topic interlocks tightly with topics 02 (subtelomere structure), 03 (PHR concept), 04 (PARs), 06 (DUX4/D4Z4 FSHD), and 14 (OR4F). Topic 02 supplies the cytogenetic substrate; topic 03 supplies the architectural concept; topic 04 supplies the obligate-recombination boundary case; topics 06 and 14 supply the most-studied paralog-clade examples (D4Z4 and OR). The present topic is the *mechanistic engine* — NAHR, gene conversion, BIR, gBGC — that the other topics invoke without naming. Topic 03's Mefford and Trask 2002 [@MeffordTrask2002] and Linardopoulou 2005 [@Linardopoulou2005] explicitly cite Liao 1999 [@Liao1999] and the NAHR framework but do not redo the mechanistic synthesis; that synthesis is in this topic. The boundary with topic 11 (pedigree-based recombination detection) is the empirical edge: pedigree topics measure the rate of present-day events, while this topic provides the theoretical machinery for interpreting them. The boundary with topic 12 (HPRC pangenomes) is methodological: gene conversion at sequence resolution requires pangenome-scale comparison because reference assemblies hide paralog exchange. The Hi-C boundary (topic 09) is mechanistic: NAHR requires physical proximity of donor and acceptor, which the meiotic bouquet plus subtelomere clustering provides (topic 08). Finally, the gBGC boundary [@Galtier2001; @DuretGaltier2009] connects this topic to base-composition genomics, which is mostly outside the abstract's scope but matters for any future GC-content analyses on the HPRC dataset.

## Open questions and recent developments

Three open questions remain. First, the relative contribution of **NAHR (crossover) vs gene conversion (no crossover) vs BIR (long copying tracts)** to subtelomeric concerted evolution has not been resolved at HPRC scale. The 133 gene-conversion-like + 16 crossover-like patches in Andrea's pedigree chapter 14 imply gene conversion dominates by an order of magnitude, but BIR-mediated tracts of 100+ kb may be miscategorised as gene conversion under the current sandwich-pattern heuristic. Anand et al.'s BIR review [@Anand2013] flags exactly this miscategorisation risk in yeast. Second, whether **gBGC** [@DuretGaltier2009] leaves a measurable fingerprint on subtelomeric base composition is testable from the existing pangenome graph — a useful extension. Third, the **birth-and-death alternative** [@NeiRooney2005] needs explicit consideration for OR4F (where the gene family does turn over rather than being homogenized). For recent developments, the deCODE pedigree map at sequence resolution [@Halldorsson2019] is the closest external comparator. The acrocentric short-arm work by Guarracino, Buonaiuto, de Lima, Potapova et al. [@Guarracino2023] is the most direct recent precedent for the abstract's loose-sense thesis: it documented heterologous recombination among the human rDNA-bearing acrocentric p-arms in T2T-CHM13 and HPRC year-1 data and explicitly invoked concerted evolution in the loose sense. The present manuscript extends that result genome-wide. Logsdon et al.'s recent T2T-resolved subtelomere completion [@Logsdon2024] gives the architectural complement: sequence-resolution maps make it possible to revisit Liao 1999's mechanistic predictions with population-scale data for the first time.
