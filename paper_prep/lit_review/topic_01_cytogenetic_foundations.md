# Topic 01: Cytogenetic foundations of subtelomere study

## Claim map

This review anchors the cytogenetic and early molecular foundations of subtelomere research to the claims in the current abstract. The literature most directly supports **C2** (the logic motivating a reference-free, all-to-all implicit pangenome approach) and **C4** (the discovery that human subtelomeres harbor extended interchromosomal homology that is variable in position and copy number). It also underpins **C3** (the dataset requirement for near-complete haplotype assemblies) and **C8** (the long-standing inference that subtelomeres undergo ongoing recombinogenic exchange), by providing the historical demonstrations of subtelomeric polymorphism and exchange that the HPRC v2 analysis now quantifies at population scale. The cytogenetic era is foundational context for C5 (named clades such as the pseudoautosomal regions and the acrocentric short-arm community), because early FISH probe sets and cytogenetic studies first identified the recurring chromosome-end pairings that later showed up as Leiden communities.

## Era overview

The systematic study of chromosome ends has passed through three methodological eras, each of which reshaped the questions that could be asked about subtelomeric regions. The pre-molecular era (roughly 1916–1969) is defined by the light-microscope cytology that first identified satellite DNA, heterochromatin blocks, and the unusual behaviour of acrocentric chromosome tips. The molecular cytogenetics era (1970–1995) began with the banding revolution of the early 1970s, expanded through the introduction of fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) in the mid-1980s, and culminated in the first probe sets specific to individual human chromosome ends. The transitional or mapping era (1995–2005) bridged cytogenetics and genomics by combining STS-anchored physical maps, half-YAC clone libraries, and sequence tag site scaffolds to assemble subtelomeric contigs from the Human Genome Project finishing phase, while FISH-based population surveys documented the striking interindividual and interpopulation variation that made subtelomeres both a clinical target and a population genetics puzzle.

The thread connecting all three eras is the recognition that chromosome ends are not inert caps. Already in the pre-molecular era, cytogeneticists noted that acrocentric short arms behaved oddly: they showed nucleolar organizer activity, satellite stalks visible in Giemsa preparations, and a propensity to associate in meiotic nuclei. Satellite DNA banding in the late 1960s and early 1970s revealed that C-band heterochromatin at chromosome tips was more variable between individuals than interstitial heterochromatin. Once FISH probes specific to individual subtelomeric regions became available, the scale of that variability became clear: a single block of subtelomeric duplicated sequence could be present at different combinations of chromosome ends in different individuals, inherited as a stable polymorphism, and shared at high frequency in some human populations but rare in others. By the late 1990s, the term pseudohomologous region (PHR) had been coined and the concept of a dynamic, exchange-mediated patchwork had replaced the earlier assumption of fixed chromosome-end sequences. The HPRC v2 analysis described in the current paper is the genome-wide population-scale realization of that patchwork: not inferred from single-chromosome cloning experiments or cytogenetic probes, but computed from 465 near-complete assemblies aligned in a reference-free graph.

Understanding this history matters for interpreting C2 and C4. The reason the analysis must be reference-free and all-to-all is precisely that the pre-molecular and early molecular literature demonstrated that subtelomeric sequences cannot be treated as fixed attributes of individual chromosomes. The reason the analysis finds extended interchromosomal homology at nearly all subtelomeres (C4) is that cytogenetics, FISH, and molecular cloning had already sketched the outlines of this homology at a handful of chromosome ends; the current work simply extends the finding to the full chromosome complement across a diverse pangenome.

## Key papers in chronological order

### Robertson (1916) — First description of acrocentric fusions

William Rees Brebner Robertson's 1916 study of grasshopper karyotypes introduced the translocation type now named in his honour: the fusion of two acrocentric chromosomes through centric breakage, producing a single metacentric chromosome with a fused centromere \cite{Robertson1916}. While Robertson's objects were insect chromosomes, his observations established that acrocentric chromosome tips were sites of preferential breakage and reunion. This observation resurfaced in human cytogenetics when the five human acrocentric chromosomes (13, 14, 15, 21, 22) were found to be disproportionately involved in Robertsonian translocations. That bias is now understood in terms of the shared satellite DNA arrays at acrocentric short arms, the same sequences that make acrocentric p-arms a single Leiden community (C7) in the current analysis.

### Caspersson et al. (1970–1971) — Q-banding and the banding revolution

Torbjörn Caspersson and colleagues introduced quinacrine mustard fluorescence staining in 1970, producing the first chromosome-specific banding patterns in human metaphase spreads \cite{Caspersson1970, Caspersson1971}. Q-banding, and the G-banding and C-banding variants that followed within two years, made it possible to identify every human chromosome unambiguously and to locate heterochromatin blocks at chromosome tips and centromeres. C-banding in particular revealed that constitutive heterochromatin at chromosome tips was polymorphic between individuals in size, an early cytogenetic indication of subtelomeric variability. The banding revolution also made Robertsonian translocations involving acrocentric chromosomes easier to detect, reinforcing the observation that these particular chromosome ends were unusually recombinogenic.

### Blackburn & Gall (1978) — Telomere sequence structure in Tetrahymena

Elizabeth Blackburn and Joseph Gall determined that the telomeres of Tetrahymena ribosomal DNA minichromosomes consist of 20–70 tandem copies of the hexanucleotide TTGGGG \cite{BlackburnGall1978}. This was the first molecular characterization of a telomere sequence and established the principle that chromosome ends are composed of simple tandem repeats. The conservation of this structure across eukaryotes was confirmed when Moyzis et al. identified (TTAGGG)n as the human telomeric repeat in 1988 \cite{Moyzis1988}, showing that human chromosomes terminate in the same type of G-rich simple-repeat array. These sequence discoveries provided the molecular framework for defining where the telomere ends and where the subtelomeric region begins — a boundary that remains critical for interpreting what the HPRC v2 analysis counts as a pseudohomologous region.

### Pinkel et al. (1986) — FISH as a cytogenetic tool

Daniel Pinkel and colleagues published two papers in 1986 establishing fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) as a practical cytogenetic method, including one in the Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences demonstrating quantitative, high-sensitivity detection of specific sequences on metaphase chromosomes using fluorescently labeled nucleic acid probes \cite{Pinkel1986a, Pinkel1986b}. FISH made it possible to localize specific DNA sequences to individual chromosome bands without radioactive detection, and to perform multicolor detection of several probes simultaneously. For subtelomere research, FISH was transformative: it allowed investigators to ask whether a particular sequence was present at one chromosome end or many, in a single cell, in a normal or rearranged karyotype. Within a few years of Pinkel's publications, FISH probes for the telomeric repeat itself and for subtelomere-specific sequences were being used to demonstrate that individual telomere-associated sequences could be physically mapped to multiple chromosome ends in the same genome.

### Rouyer et al. (1986) — The pseudoautosomal region as a model for subtelomeric exchange

Françoise Rouyer, Marc Simmler, and colleagues published the molecular characterization of the human pseudoautosomal region (PAR1) at the tip of the X and Y short arms in 1986 \cite{Rouyer1986}. PAR1 is the prototype subtelomeric interchromosomal homology: a region so similar between the X and Y chromosomes that it behaves as autosomal in genetic terms, with an obligate crossover in male meiosis. The PAR1 paper established several principles that were later generalized to non-PAR subtelomeric regions: that two non-homologous chromosomes can share substantial telomere-adjacent sequence; that the shared region can be maintained by recombination; and that recombination frequency within the shared region correlates with physical distance from the telomere tip. In the current paper's community structure, PAR1 (chrXp/chrYp) and PAR2 (chrXq/chrYq) appear as the two most extreme communities in terms of cross-arm identity, with PAR2 used as the benchmark for scale when assessing the extent of interchromosomal homology at non-PAR subtelomeres (C4).

### Moyzis et al. (1988) — The human telomeric repeat (TTAGGG)n

Robert Moyzis and colleagues identified (TTAGGG)n as the human telomeric sequence from a repetitive DNA library \cite{Moyzis1988}. Their demonstration that this hexanucleotide is present at the telomeres of all 46 human chromosomes and is conserved across vertebrates provided the physical anchor for defining chromosome ends at the molecular level. Critically, the paper showed that the terminal telomere array in human cells is not fixed in length: the number of TTAGGG repeats varies among chromosomes and between individuals. This length variation was an early indication that chromosome ends are dynamic structures subject to ongoing change, foreshadowing the far more extensive sequence polymorphism discovered at the subtelomeric level.

### Brown et al. (1990) — Polymorphic telomere-associated sequences as a class

W. R. A. Brown and colleagues described the structure and polymorphism of human telomere-associated DNA, reporting that one of the telomere-associated repeated sequences they characterized had a polymorphic distribution among chromosome ends — present at a different combination of chromosome tips in different individuals \cite{Brown1990}. This paper identified a new source of human genetic variation and made the conceptual leap from terminal repeat polymorphism (variable TTAGGG repeat number) to subtelomeric block polymorphism (variable chromosomal location of a duplicated sequence). It is one of the earliest demonstrations of what would later be called the PHR patchwork.

### Greider & Blackburn (1985, 1989) — Telomerase

Carol Greider and Elizabeth Blackburn discovered telomerase in Tetrahymena in 1985 and showed it was a ribonucleoprotein that used its own RNA as a template to extend G-rich telomere repeats \cite{Greider1985}. Gregg Morin then demonstrated that a similar ribonucleoprotein enzyme in human cell extracts synthesized (TTAGGG)n repeats onto primer ends \cite{Morin1989}. These discoveries established the enzymatic basis for telomere maintenance and showed that chromosomal ends are not passively copied but are actively extended by a dedicated reverse transcriptase. Telomerase acts strictly on the terminal repeat array and does not synthesize subtelomeric sequence, which means subtelomeric dynamics — the formation of PHRs, the cross-chromosomal duplications, the population-scale variation — must arise from recombinogenic and duplicative mechanisms rather than from template extension. The telomerase discoveries thus defined the boundary between the enzymatically maintained terminal end and the recombination-shaped subtelomeric zone.

### Ijdo et al. (1991) — Chromosome 2 telomere fusion as a window into ancient subtelomeric exchange

Joseph Ijdo and colleagues demonstrated that human chromosome 2q13 contains a relic of an ancestral telomere-telomere fusion: two inverted arrays of TTAGGG repeats in head-to-head orientation, flanked by sequences hybridizing to multiple present-day chromosome ends \cite{Ijdo1991}. This paper showed that telomere-adjacent sequences that once resided at the tips of two ancestral ape chromosomes have been interspersed in a new genomic context since the fusion event, and that the flanking sequences were shared among modern chromosome ends — an ancient PHR preserved in interstitial position. For the current paper, the chromosome 2 fusion locus is background for understanding why some "subtelomeric" sequences appear at interstitial positions in rearranged chromosomes and why interstitial telomere sequences co-localize with PHR domain boundaries in the two-domain model tested in Andrea's HPRC analysis.

### Wilkie et al. (1991) — Stable polymorphism at 16p as evidence for interchromosomal exchange

Andrew Wilkie and colleagues long-range restriction-mapped the terminal 430 kb of chromosome 16p13.3, finding three common alleles that differed in which terminal segment was present beyond a fixed boundary 145 kb distal to the alpha-globin locus \cite{Wilkie1991}. The two main alleles were non-homologous to each other but each was homologous to sequences at other (different) chromosome ends. The authors concluded that this polymorphism most likely arose through occasional exchanges between subtelomeric regions of nonhomologous chromosomes — the first explicit argument for current-generation interchromosomal subtelomeric exchange from DNA sequence evidence. This paper is a direct intellectual ancestor of the HPRC v2 analysis, which counts analogous cross-chromosomal sequence affinity events at population scale.

### NIH and Institute of Molecular Medicine Collaboration (1996) — Complete set of human subtelomeric FISH probes

A collaboration coordinated through the NIH and the Institute of Molecular Medicine produced and characterized a complete set of FISH probes, one for each of the 41 genetically distinct human chromosome ends, positioned within 100–300 kb of the terminal TTAGGG repeat \cite{NIH1996}. Published in Nature Genetics in 1996, this paper was the practical foundation for clinical subtelomeric diagnostics: investigators could now systematically screen all chromosome ends in a single patient for cryptic rearrangements. It was also a research tool that enabled the first genome-wide FISH surveys of subtelomeric variation in normal populations, which showed that polymorphism was far more common than previously appreciated. The clinical applications of this probe set (screening for idiopathic intellectual disability) are described in subsequent work by Knight, de Vries, and others and are discussed further in the adjacent topics (topic 12 and topic 14).

### Flint et al. (1997) — The two-domain model of subtelomeric structure

Julian Flint and colleagues sequenced and compared the terminal regions of chromosomes 4p, 16p, and 22q, identifying a shared structural principle: each chromosome end is divided by degenerate (TTAGGG)n repeats into a distal sub-domain and a proximal sub-domain, with the distal domain showing short-range homology to many chromosome ends and the proximal domain showing longer-range homology to a smaller number of ends \cite{Flint1997}. This two-domain model became the reference architecture for subtelomeric organization. It directly predicts the gradient of interchromosomal similarity with distance from the telomere that the HPRC analysis confirms on 39/48 chromosome arms and recovers as a discrete two-segment breakpoint structure on 39/41 testable arms. The current paper's confirmation of the Flint/Mefford model at pangenome scale is one of the most direct literature confirmations reported in Andrea's chapter 12.

### Trask et al. (1998) — Cross-population variation in subtelomeric olfactory receptor blocks

Barbara Trask and colleagues used FISH to characterize the genomic distribution of a cosmid-sized block of duplicated sequence (containing olfactory receptor genes) across human individuals from diverse populations and in nonhuman primates \cite{Trask1998}. The block was consistently present at the 3q, 15q, and 19p telomeres in all humans but variably distributed at additional chromosome ends, with both the chromosome location and copy number varying between individuals and between human populations. The primate comparison showed that the block was present in a single copy in nonhuman primate genomes, indicating human-specific amplification and dispersal. This paper established the key concepts of human-specific subtelomeric block amplification and population-level variation in block distribution — concepts directly relevant to C5 (named communities) and C6 (population-level clustering) in the current abstract.

### Mefford & Trask (2002) — Synthesis: the complex structure and dynamic evolution of human subtelomeres

Heather Mefford and Barbara Trask's 2002 review in Nature Reviews Genetics synthesized a decade of cytogenetic, molecular cloning, and early sequence-based work into a coherent model of human subtelomeric organization \cite{MeffordTrask2002}. The review introduced the term pseudohomologous region, enumerated the known shared duplicon blocks, discussed the f7501 olfactory receptor block and its population-level variation (including African enrichment at 16q and variable distribution elsewhere), and explicitly raised the possibility that subtelomeric block frequencies might carry population-history signal. The review also noted the limitations of single-reference analyses for a class of genomic region whose defining property is variable chromosomal location. For the current paper, Mefford & Trask 2002 is the anchor for claims about the patchwork architecture (C4), known clades such as the 4q/10q DUX4 community and the f7501 cluster (C5), and the long-standing inference of ongoing exchange (C8). It is also the paper whose qualitative population-history hypothesis the HPRC v2 analysis now confirms quantitatively.

## Findings most relevant to claims C2, C3, and C4 of the abstract

The cytogenetic and early molecular literature is most directly relevant to **C4** (extended interchromosomal homology at nearly all subtelomeres, comparable in scale to PAR2) and to the methodological motivation for **C2** (reference-free, all-to-all analysis).

### C4 — Extended interchromosomal homology

The central finding that subtelomeric regions are unusually similar across non-homologous chromosomes was built up by a series of converging observations. Brown et al. (1990) showed that a specific telomere-associated sequence had a polymorphic distribution across chromosome ends \cite{Brown1990}. Wilkie et al. (1991) showed that the terminal segment of chromosome 16p had been replaced by a sequence shared with other chromosome ends, and that the replacement allele was stable and heritable \cite{Wilkie1991}. Trask et al. (1998) showed that the OR gene block was present at up to a dozen chromosome ends in some individuals \cite{Trask1998}. Flint et al. (1997) provided a structural explanation: the distal subtelomeric domain is inherently multi-chromosomal in its homology relationships, separated from the more chromosome-specific proximal domain by internal telomeric tracts \cite{Flint1997}. Mefford & Trask (2002) synthesized these into the patchwork model: nearly every chromosome end carries some interchromosomal shared sequence, and the combination of blocks present at any given end is polymorphic \cite{MeffordTrask2002}.

The HPRC v2 analysis confirms and extends all of these observations simultaneously: 83.2% of 18,827 subtelomeric sequences have interchromosomal matches above the 95% identity threshold, at median length 105 kb, extending to 41 of 48 chromosome arms. The "comparable to PAR2" framing in the abstract (C4) is the quantitative translation of the qualitative insight already present in the literature: PAR1 and PAR2 are the extreme cases of interchromosomal subtelomeric homology, and the new analysis shows that dozens of non-PAR chromosome ends harbor shared sequence approaching PAR2's scale.

### C2 — Motivation for reference-free, all-to-all analysis

The cytogenetic era established why a reference-based analysis cannot adequately characterize subtelomeric regions. The key observations are: (1) the same sequence block can occupy different chromosome ends in different individuals (Brown 1990, Trask 1998) \cite{Brown1990, Trask1998}; (2) even within a single individual, a block may be present in extra copies or absent from its "canonical" location (Wilkie 1991) \cite{Wilkie1991}; (3) the NIH probe set (1996) found cryptic rearrangements at subtelomeres in a substantial fraction of clinical cases, indicating that reference sequence coordinates were inadequate for capturing the true diversity \cite{NIH1996}; and (4) Riethman's mapping project (2004) found that even with the Human Genome Project's finishing resources, subtelomeric assemblies were incomplete or ambiguous for many termini \cite{Riethman2004}. Together, these observations motivate an approach that does not assign sequences to a reference genome location before comparing them — exactly the implicit pangenome graph strategy implemented in the current analysis.

### C3 — Dataset requirements

The early FISH and molecular studies made it clear that subtelomeric variation required large sample sizes to capture. Trask et al. (1998) already noted that the OR block was present at different chromosome ends in different individuals from the same population, and Mefford & Trask (2002) remarked that the frequency of specific subtelomeric blocks at particular chromosome ends varies substantially between populations \cite{Trask1998, MeffordTrask2002}. These observations are the intellectual ancestors of C3's requirement for 465 near-complete assemblies: single-genome or small-cohort analyses cannot resolve population-level community structure in a class of regions defined by polymorphic interchromosomal sharing.

What is contested in this literature is the relative contribution of different mechanisms to creating the observed pattern. The cytogenetic era identified several processes — NAHR, gene conversion, nonhomologous end-joining, simple heterochromatin extension — as candidates for subtelomeric exchange. The current analysis documents the end-products (sequence communities, cross-arm affinity) and provides pedigree-level evidence of recent exchange, but the mechanistic assignment of specific patch types to specific exchange mechanisms remains an open question that the cytogenetic literature raised and that the current study does not fully resolve.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

The cytogenetic foundations topic overlaps substantially with several of the other topics in this fanout.

**Topic 03 (Pseudohomologous regions concept)** picks up directly from this topic's endpoint. The naming and theoretical elaboration of PHRs as a class was done between 1997 and 2005 by Flint, Mefford, Trask, and Linardopoulou — all papers cited in this review. Topic 03 focuses on the conceptual development of the PHR model and its refinement through early sequence-based analyses, whereas the current topic emphasizes the cytogenetic detection methods and the empirical demonstrations of subtelomeric polymorphism that preceded formal naming.

**Topic 05 (Rouyer PAR1 and the pseudoautosomal region)** is directly introduced here by the Rouyer 1986 paper. PAR1 and PAR2 are the most intensively studied interchromosomal subtelomeric homologies, and the cytogenetic techniques (FISH, STS mapping, molecular cloning) developed to characterize PAR1 were subsequently applied to non-PAR subtelomeres. The current abstract uses PAR2 as the scale benchmark for C4, making the PAR literature essential background.

**Topic 06 (DUX4/D4Z4 and FSHD)** traces back to this topic at two points: the recognition of subtelomeric exchange as a source of copy-number variation (relevant to the DUX4 copy-number diversity noted in C5), and the identification of chromosome 4q/10q as a recurring cross-chromosomal pair in FISH studies (Trask 1998 mentions olfactory receptor blocks; the 4q/10q D4Z4 exchange is a parallel phenomenon). The cytogenetic techniques that enabled PHR research were the same techniques used to study D4Z4 in the FSHD context.

**Topic 14 (Pedigree and direct exchange evidence)** inherits the methodological and conceptual framework of this cytogenetic topic. The pedigree analysis in Andrea's chapter 14 is possible because the community structure discovered by FISH-based and molecular-cloning approaches established which chromosome ends are expected to share sequences: the 538 cross-chromosomal patches in the WashU pedigree fall overwhelmingly within the Leiden communities, which are the population-scale quantification of the sharing patterns first sketched by cytogenetic probes.

## Open questions and recent developments

### Persistence of reference assembly gaps

Despite the Human Genome Project, the National Institutes of Health probe effort (1996), and Riethman's mapping program (2004), subtelomeric regions remained among the most incompletely assembled portions of the human reference genome until Telomere-to-Telomere (T2T) assembly methods were developed. Logsdon et al. (2021) completed chromosome 8q and revealed that its subtelomeric region harbored complex segmental duplications not visible in GRCh38 \cite{Logsdon2021}. Nurk et al. (2022) closed all gaps in the first T2T human assembly (CHM13), including subtelomeric intervals that had resisted assembly for decades \cite{Nurk2022}. The HPRC v2 dataset (C3) is possible only because these assembly methods have been applied at scale. The cytogenetic era correctly diagnosed the problem (subtelomeric sequences are highly variable and hard to assemble) but could not solve it with the available technology.

### Quantitative confirmation of classical claims

Several qualitative claims from the cytogenetic era have now been quantitatively confirmed or refined. The Flint/Mefford two-domain model is confirmed on 39/48 arms with precise boundary positions ranging from 15 to 445 kb from the telomere \cite{Flint1997, MeffordTrask2002}. The Mefford (2002) observation that the f7501 OR block is enriched in African populations at 16q is confirmed at p = 6.6 × 10⁻²⁷ across 465 near-complete assemblies in the HPRC v2 analysis. The Trask (1998) observation that OR block distribution varies between individuals is confirmed and extended: the current analysis detects OR-family community structure at population scale across five continental ancestries (C6).

### Clinical translation and the subtelomere deletion screening era (2000–2010)

The complete FISH probe set published in 1996 spawned a decade of clinical subtelomeric screening, motivated by the observation that 7–8% of children with unexplained moderate-to-severe intellectual disability carry submicroscopic subtelomeric deletions or rearrangements detectable by FISH \cite{Flint2003}. De Vries et al. (2001) developed a checklist of phenotypic features to pre-select patients for subtelomeric screening \cite{deVries2001}. These clinical studies reinforced the cytogenetic picture: subtelomeric regions are unusually recombinogenic, and cryptic rearrangements at chromosome ends are a significant cause of congenital anomalies. The clinical era also produced the first systematic catalogs of which chromosome arms are most commonly involved in deletions and duplications, providing independent evidence that certain chromosome ends — particularly those with large shared duplicon blocks — are more rearrangement-prone than others.

### Transition to array-CGH and molecular karyotyping

By 2005–2010, FISH-based subtelomere screening was being replaced by array comparative genomic hybridization (aCGH) and eventually by whole-genome sequencing. Veltman et al. (2002) and Rooms et al. (2004) applied array-CGH to subtelomeric regions, enabling higher throughput and quantitative copy-number measurement \cite{Veltman2002}. These methods confirmed the FISH-based estimates of rearrangement frequency while revealing additional complexity: many apparent deletions were copy-number variations rather than simple losses, and some regions showed recurrent instability across unrelated patients. The transition to sequencing-based approaches set the stage for the pangenome analyses of the T2T and HPRC era.

### What remains unsettled

Three major questions from the cytogenetic era remain open. First, the precise exchange mechanisms generating PHRs are not fully resolved. NAHR, gene conversion, and break-induced replication have all been proposed; the current paper's pedigree data classify 133 events as gene-conversion-like, but mechanistic discrimination requires direct molecular analysis of the exchange junctions. Second, the evolutionary timescale of specific interchromosomal homologies is known only approximately: some blocks are human-specific, others are shared with great apes, and the history of individual pairs has been inferred from FISH and comparative sequencing rather than from population-scale data. Third, the functional consequences of subtelomeric variation — whether different block combinations affect telomere length, nuclear positioning, or gene expression at nearby loci — were hypothesized by several groups in the cytogenetic era but have not been resolved at population scale. The current HPRC v2 analysis provides the sequence communities and 3D contact data that are prerequisites for testing these functional hypotheses, but the direct demonstration of functional consequences remains a major open problem in the field.
