# Topic 12: HPRC v1 and v2 population pangenomes — lineage of pangenome reference construction

## Claim map

This review anchors the HPRC v1–v2 lineage to the current abstract as follows. The literature directly supports **C1** (HPRC v2 companion-paper framing: the subtelomere analysis is positioned as a companion to the HPRC v2 main manuscript) and **C3** (466 near-complete haplotype assemblies from HPRC v2 as the substrate). It supports **C2** indirectly, by tracing the conceptual and technical lineage that made reference-free, all-to-all pangenome graphs possible at population scale. It provides motivating background for **C6** (population-genetic analyses) and **C4** (genome-wide identity survey) by establishing that earlier short-read population studies systematically undercharacterized complex regions, including subtelomeres, that HPRC v2 now resolves at haplotype resolution.

## Era overview

The idea of replacing the single human reference genome with a collection of diverse haplotype-resolved assemblies emerged gradually over fifteen years, driven by growing evidence that a single reference genome captures only one slice of human genetic diversity and fails to represent most structural variation. The 1000 Genomes Project, launched in 2008, was the first large-scale attempt to characterize genetic variation systematically across human populations [@hprc_1000g2012; @hprc_1000g2015]. Its short-read sequencing approach produced an invaluable catalogue of single-nucleotide variants and small indels from over 2,500 individuals, but structural variants — insertions, deletions, inversions and duplications larger than 50 bp — remained poorly characterized. Sudmant et al.'s companion analysis of structural variation in 2,504 genomes documented 68,818 deletions, 2,929 inversions and 14,256 duplications but depended on read-pair signatures that systematically undercount insertions and complex rearrangements [@hprc_sudmant2015].

Two converging developments catalyzed the shift toward pangenome references. First, long-read sequencing technologies matured. Pacific Biosciences HiFi reads and Oxford Nanopore ultralong reads enabled the de novo assembly of individual haplotypes at near-T2T quality, with contiguous scaffolds spanning segmental duplications, centromeres, and subtelomeres that fragmented short-read assemblies entirely. Second, population diversity in reference construction became an ethical and scientific imperative. As of 2021, over 86% of genome-wide association study participants were of European ancestry; genetic risk scores trained on European reference haplotypes performed poorly in non-European individuals, and the structural variants most likely to affect complex traits were also the least representable by GRCh38 [@hprc_wang2022]. Population pangenomics — building a shared reference from many diverse, haplotype-resolved genomes — responded to both pressures simultaneously. The 1000 Genomes era established the question (what is the full scope of human population variation?); the HPRC answered it at a new level of resolution.

## Key papers in chronological order

**1000 Genomes Project Phase 1 (2012)** catalogued 38 million SNPs, 1.4 million short indels and over 14,000 larger deletions from 1,092 individuals across 14 populations, establishing the first population-scale haplotype reference and demonstrating the value of integrating diverse ancestry [@hprc_1000g2012]. This consortium also pioneered the community-based model that the HPRC later adopted: shared data, shared methods, explicit ancestry representation goals.

**1000 Genomes Project Phase 3 and companion SV paper (2015)** scaled the project to 2,504 individuals across 26 populations [@hprc_1000g2015; @hprc_sudmant2015]. The SV companion by Sudmant et al. identified 68,818 sequence-resolved deletions and provided the first population-stratified structural variant map, documenting enrichment of SVs on GWAS-identified haplotypes. Crucially, the paper acknowledged that short-read methods were adequate for deletions but systematically missed mobile-element insertions and large segmental duplications — a limitation that HPRC v2 subtelomere analyses now address directly.

**Audano et al. (2019) and the HGSVC pilot** Long-read sequencing of 15 diverse human genomes resolved 99,604 SVs [@hprc_audano2019]. One finding was striking: a ninefold enrichment of variable SVs within the last 5 Mb of chromosomes — precisely the zone that overlaps subtelomeric pseudohomologous regions (PHRs). This paper established that reference-based approaches undercounted subtelomeric variation by orders of magnitude and provided a template for the assembly-first approach the HPRC later scaled.

**gnomAD-SV (Collins et al. 2020)** assembled 433,371 structural variants from 14,891 diverse genomes sequenced with short reads [@hprc_collins2020]. As the largest population SV resource at the time, gnomAD-SV demonstrated the epidemiological importance of structural variants (responsible for 25–29% of rare protein-truncating events per genome) but also its inherent ceiling: complex tandem repeats, mobile-element insertions, and segmental-duplication rearrangements remained systematically unresolved.

**HGSVC2 (Ebert, Audano et al. 2021)** sequenced 64 haplotypes from 32 diverse individuals with multiple long-read and strand-specific platforms, discovering 107,590 SVs, 68% of which short-read methods missed [@hprc_ebert2021]. The study established that SV hotspots cluster in segmental duplications (many of which are subtelomeric), and provided a proof of concept that a panel of diverse, haplotype-resolved assemblies — rather than any single reference — was necessary for accurate population SV characterization.

**T2T-CHM13 (Nurk et al. 2022)** completed a single human genome from telomere to telomere for the first time [@hprc_nurk2022]. The 3.055-Gbp assembly added 199 Mbp of sequence absent from GRCh38, including complete centromeres, the short arms of all five acrocentric chromosomes, and full subtelomeric intervals previously missing or misassembled. This demonstrated that near-complete assemblies were achievable and set the quality benchmark for HPRC v1 assemblies.

**hifiasm (Cheng et al. 2021)** introduced a phased assembly graph approach that uses HiFi reads' long span and high accuracy to produce haplotype-resolved diploid assemblies without parental trio data when Hi-C phasing is available [@hprc_cheng2021]. This assembler became the primary tool for HPRC v1 assemblies and was adopted for most HPRC v2 genomes, enabling the 466-haplotype substrate used in the subtelomere analysis.

**Verkko (Rautiainen et al. 2023)** extended T2T assembly to diploid chromosomes using multiplex de Bruijn graphs anchored by ultralong ONT reads [@hprc_rautiainen2023]. Verkko complements hifiasm for the most challenging regions (centromeres, D4Z4 arrays, rDNA) and was used for a subset of HPRC v2 assemblies. The combination of hifiasm and Verkko across HPRC v2 allowed nearly complete diploid assemblies that resolved the complex subtelomeric regions studied in the present manuscript.

**The Human Pangenome Project vision paper (Wang, Miga, Phillippy et al. 2022)** articulated the strategic goals and ethical framework for building a representative human pangenome reference: improved accuracy in diverse populations, representation of previously inaccessible variation, and equitable genomic medicine [@hprc_wang2022]. It established the 350-individual target that became HPRC v2's ambition.

**HPRC v1 (Liao et al. 2023)** delivered 47 phased diploid assemblies (94 haplotypes) from diverse individuals, organized as a graph-based human pangenome reference [@hprc_liao2023]. The assemblies covered more than 99% of expected sequence with 99%+ accuracy. The paper demonstrated that graph-based alignment (using Minigraph-Cactus and pggb) outperformed GRCh38-linear alignment for variant discovery, especially for SVs in repetitive and segmentally duplicated regions. For the present subtelomere manuscript, HPRC v1 is the proof-of-concept predecessor: the same community, assemblers, and quality benchmarks were used at 5× larger scale in HPRC v2.

**Minigraph-Cactus (Hickey et al. 2024)** and **pggb (Garrison et al. 2024)** provided the two principal graph-construction pipelines used in HPRC v1 and v2 [@hprc_hickey2024; @hprc_garrison2024]. Minigraph-Cactus scales efficiently to hundreds of genomes by combining reference-anchored SV discovery with progressive cactus-graph alignment; pggb uses an all-to-all wfmash alignment followed by graph induction (seqwish) and normalization (smoothxg), avoiding any reference bias. For subtelomeres specifically, pggb is the natural fit because PHR regions lack a canonical reference organization and must be analyzed in a reference-free manner. The present manuscript's implicit pangenome — all-vs-all wfmash alignment of 18,827 subtelomeric sequences — is a conceptual extension of the pggb approach applied to a sub-genomic scale.

**HPRC Data Release 2 (2025, in submission)** expanded the HPRC pangenome to 232 individuals (approximately 464 haplotypes; 466 including CHM13) using PacBio HiFi (60× coverage) plus ONT ultralong reads (≥30× over 100 kb), with phasing from Hi-C or trio data [@hprc_hprcv2_2025]. The release announced on 12 May 2025 achieved approximately threefold reduction in misassemblies relative to HPRC v1 and produced near-complete coverage across all chromosome arms. This dataset is the direct substrate for the present subtelomere manuscript: the 18,827 telomere-adjacent flanks analyzed, the 15,668 PHR sequences, and the population-genetic analyses all derive from HPRC v2.

## Findings most relevant to claims C1 and C3 of the abstract

**C1 (HPRC v2 companion-paper framing)** is not supported by any single prior paper but is established by the institutional and publication architecture of the HPRC. HPRC v1 set a precedent: the 2023 Liao et al. Nature paper was the main resource paper, and several companion analyses (subtelomere work, population genetics, epigenomes, disease applications) were either simultaneous or closely following. For HPRC v2, the same companion-paper model applies. The present subtelomere manuscript is positioned as the companion to the HPRC v2 main paper (in submission, 2025), which will describe the 232-individual cohort, assembly methods, quality metrics and graph construction. The companion framing matters for C1 because it explains why the abstract leads with HPRC v2: the 466-haplotype dataset is not an auxiliary resource from which the authors extracted subtelomeres, but the primary deliverable of a coordinated consortium effort, and the subtelomere analysis is one of its first biological payoffs.

What the prior literature shows is that companion analyses of population pangenome datasets have been highly productive. Ebert et al. (2021) used the HGSVC2 cohort to discover SV hotspots; gnomAD-SV used the gnomAD cohort to characterize disease-relevant SVs; the 1000G SV companion (Sudmant et al.) was published simultaneously with the main Phase 3 paper. The HPRC v1 release already enabled the first high-resolution population-scale view of segmental duplications, centromeres and acrocentric short arms. HPRC v2's fivefold scale increase over v1 unlocks the subtelomere question specifically because subtelomeric PHR communities are small enough that 94 haplotypes (v1) provides statistically insufficient sampling for many chromosome ends. At 466 haplotypes, the Leiden community assignments are stable, population-stratified analyses become meaningful, and cross-arm affinity can be estimated per continental group [@hprc_liao2023; @hprc_hprcv2_2025].

**C3 (466 near-complete haplotype assemblies)** is directly supported by the HPRC v2 data release. The specific count requires a note: the HPRC v2 release describes 232 individuals × 2 haplotypes = 464 phased haplotypes, plus CHM13 (the T2T reference genome used as an anchor) for a total of 465 or 466 depending on convention. Andrea's report consistently cites 465 (233 individuals in the pipeline input counting CHM13 separately), while the abstract uses 466. The CROSSWALK notes that this discrepancy is a naming convention rather than a data discrepancy: the subtelomere analysis includes CHM13 alongside the 465 HPRC v2 haplotypes as one additional haplotype-equivalent unit, giving 466 effective entries in the analyses. The prior population-pangenome literature supports the C3 claim by providing historical context for why this number matters: HPRC v1's 94 haplotypes was already five to tenfold more than any prior haplotype-resolved dataset; HPRC v2's 466 represents another fivefold increase, now covering all major superpopulations at a depth sufficient for population-stratified analyses (AFR, AMR, EAS, EUR, SAS, OCE) and for detecting alleles present at 1–5% global frequency.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

The HPRC v1–v2 lineage is the enabling substrate for nearly every adjacent topic in this fanout. Three connections are especially direct.

**Topic 03 (Pseudohomologous regions concept)** and **Topic 07 (Concerted evolution and NAHR)** depend on HPRC v2 for the population-scale PHR detection that upgrades the old cytogenetic PHR model. Mefford and Trask (2002) observed patchwork architecture in a few cloned chromosome ends; Linardopoulou et al. (2005) inferred subtelomeric exchange from sequence architecture in a single reference. The present analysis runs the same conceptual test on 466 haplotypes simultaneously, making the community structure a population object rather than a reference annotation. The pangenome infrastructure — wfmash all-vs-all alignment, pggb/seqwish graph construction, odgi similarity matrices — converts the old qualitative PHR concept into a quantitative, population-scalable analysis pipeline.

**Topics 05 (acrocentric rDNA/Robertsonian)** and **Topic 14 (OR4F olfactory gene cluster)** rely on the HPRC v2 assemblies' quality in formerly inaccessible genomic regions. Short-read references routinely collapsed or misassembled acrocentric short arms and OR gene clusters at subtelomeres. HPRC v2's hifiasm+verkko assemblies, quality-checked to near-T2T completeness, provide the first population-scale resolved sequences for these arms. The olfactory receptor pseudogenization gradient documented in the current analysis (62.1% pseudogene rate, ranging from 11.1% at chr7p to 99.8% at chr15q) is only observable because the HPRC v2 assemblies correctly resolve the multi-copy, near-identical OR loci that collapsed in GRCh38 and earlier long-read drafts.

**Topic 11 (Pedigree-based recombination detection)** connects because the WashU and CEPH1463 pedigrees used in the current analysis are themselves HPRC-adjacent: T2T-quality assemblies from pedigree members (Cechova et al. 2025, Porubsky et al. 2025) were generated using the same hifiasm/verkko pipeline developed for HPRC. The HPRC v2 pangenome's community definitions (the 15 arm-level Leiden communities) provide the background against which pedigree recombination events are classified; without the population-scale community structure from HPRC v2, a recombination patch between chr22q and chr13p would be a curiosity rather than an interpretable event falling inside a known community boundary.

## Open questions and recent developments

Despite the progress HPRC v2 represents, several important questions remain open in population pangenomics, directly relevant to how the present subtelomere manuscript should be framed.

**Completeness at complex regions.** HPRC v2 achieves near-complete coverage for most chromosome arms, but the most complex subtelomeric intervals — particularly highly repetitive acrocentric short arms and the largest PHR blocks — still harbor assembly gaps or misjoins in a subset of haplotypes. The current analysis specifically filters for 500 kb subtelomeric flanks with sufficient PHR signal, excluding 7 of 48 arms that fall below threshold. Whether this reflects genuine absence of PHR signal or residual assembly incompleteness is an open question that a T2T-quality pangenome (HPRC v3 or analogous) would resolve. Logsdon et al. and the T2T-CHM13 consortium showed that centromeres and acrocentric short arms require ultralong reads at high coverage to resolve; extending T2T standards to 466 haplotypes remains computationally intensive.

**Reference-free vs. reference-anchored analyses.** The two dominant pangenome graph approaches — pggb (reference-free, all-to-all) and Minigraph-Cactus (reference-anchored, incremental) — give concordant results for large, unique SVs but diverge for complex, multi-allelic variation at segmental duplications and subtelomeres [@hprc_garrison2024; @hprc_hickey2024]. For the present analysis, the choice was deliberately reference-free (all-vs-all wfmash alignments, then community detection). This avoids the reference bias that would make PAR1 or GRCh38-annotated subtelomeric features appear as fixed anchors; instead, each PHR's community membership emerges from the data. Whether a reference-anchored approach would recover the same 15 communities, or would introduce systematic biases at arms with the most unusual PHR architecture, is worth exploring as HPRC v3 graph construction methods mature.

**Population representation.** HPRC v2's 232 individuals prioritize AFR, EAS, EUR, SAS and AMR ancestry but have limited representation from Oceania, the Middle East, and isolated indigenous populations. The present analysis reports that subtelomeric Fst recovers the out-of-Africa topology across 9 community-informative arms, but the statistical power for within-continental-group differentiation is limited by HPRC v2's sample sizes. Broader population sampling — as envisioned by the Global Pangenome Alliance and related consortia — would allow asking whether specific PHR communities show signatures of local adaptation or bottleneck effects in particular groups.

**Long-read population SV catalogues.** Sirén et al. (2025) recently produced the first long-read, population-scale SV catalogue from 1,019 diverse genomes, identifying over 100,000 sequence-resolved biallelic SVs and 300,000 VNTRs [@hprc_siren2025]. This resource complements HPRC v2 by providing allele-frequency estimates for thousands of SVs, including many in subtelomeric regions, at a scale that HPRC v2's 232 individuals cannot achieve alone. The combination of HPRC v2 assembly quality and 1000G-scale SV catalogues is likely the immediate next frontier: using the HPRC v2 graph as an anchor to genotype structural variants across the Sirén et al. cohort would allow quantifying population-specific PHR allele frequencies without generating full assemblies for each sample.

**Graph-based genotyping for subtelomeric variants.** Tools like PanGenie (Ebler et al. 2022) use k-mer fingerprints from short-read data to genotype SVs defined in a pangenome graph, enabling application of HPRC v2's detailed structural variant catalogue to the hundreds of thousands of already-sequenced biobank cohorts [@hprc_ebler2022]. Extending this to the subtelomeric communities identified in the present analysis — asking whether community membership for a specific chromosome arm is heritable, associated with telomere length, or correlated with disease — would represent the translational payoff of the HPRC v2 substrate. The 15 arm-level communities could in principle be used as structural haplotype tags, much as conventional haplotype blocks are used in GWAS. Whether this is tractable given the multi-copy, high-identity structure of PHR sequences is an open methodological question.

**HPRC v2 companion publication.** As of May 2025, the HPRC v2 main paper is in submission and the data have been pre-publication released. The final paper will specify the exact assembly statistics, population breakdown, and graph properties that the present subtelomere companion cites as C1 and C3. Minor numbers in the abstract (466 vs. 465 haplotypes) may be resolved by the companion publication's convention; the current manuscript should either use the same number as the companion or include a Methods note clarifying the 466 = 232 × 2 + CHM13 convention.
