# Topic 04: Sex chromosome pseudoautosomal regions, 1986-present

## Era overview

The modern literature on human pseudoautosomal regions began when molecular markers made it possible to see that a small part of the X and Y chromosomes behaves neither like ordinary X-linked sequence nor like the male-specific Y. In the mid-1980s, MIC2/CD99 and telomeric X-Y probes showed pseudoautosomal inheritance: fathers could transmit alleles through either sex chromosome, and recombination in male meiosis was concentrated near the Xp/Yp tips \cite{sexchrompars_goodfellow1986,Rouyer1986}. The core question was initially cartographic. How long was the pairing segment, where did the recombining interval stop, and what prevented recombination from entering sex-determining Y-specific DNA? Boundary-walking and population surveys then placed the proximal PAR1 boundary near an Alu insertion on the Y chromosome and turned the pseudoautosomal boundary into a model for abrupt recombination suppression \cite{sexchrompars_pritchard1987,sexchrompars_ellis1989,sexchrompars_ellis1990}.

By the 1990s, the topic expanded from mapping into evolution. Discovery of a second pseudoautosomal region at Xq/Yq showed that the sex chromosomes carry two terminal shared intervals, not one, but the two intervals are not functionally equivalent \cite{sexchrompars_freije1992}. PAR1 is approximately 2.7 Mb and supports the obligate X-Y crossover needed for male sex-chromosome segregation. PAR2 is roughly 320-400 kb, has a different origin, and undergoes much less recombination \cite{sexchrompars_ciccodicola2000,sexchrompars_charchar2003}. This asymmetry is central to the current subtelomere paper: PAR1 and PAR2 are both authentic X-Y homology clades, but only PAR1 is the canonical obligate-recombination engine.

Genome-era work placed PARs inside the larger history of sex chromosome divergence. The human X chromosome is partitioned into evolutionary domains and strata, including the X-conserved region, X-added region (XAR), pseudoautosomal intervals, and the X-transposed region (XTR) duplicated from X to Y in recent human evolution \cite{sexchrompars_lahn1999,sexchrompars_ross2005,sexchrompars_skaletsky2003}. These categories matter because X-Y homology is not one mechanism. PARs maintain homology by recombination; XTR maintains very high similarity because it is recently transposed but is outside the normal PAR crossover system; XAR reflects deeper additions to the sex chromosomes. The present manuscript's NJ-tree clades C14 and C15 therefore sit in a well-established sex-chromosome landscape: Xq/Yq recovers PAR2, Xp/Yp recovers PAR1, and any nearby arms drawn into the clade must be interpreted against the special biology of true PAR exchange.

## Key papers in chronological order

Goodfellow et al. established MIC2 as a pseudoautosomal gene and showed measurable recombination between MIC2 and the sex-determining locus, making pseudoautosomal inheritance a gene-level phenomenon rather than only a cytogenetic observation \cite{sexchrompars_goodfellow1986}. Rouyer et al. then mapped a gradient of sex linkage across the Xp/Yp pairing region, giving the field a recombination-distance model for PAR1 and making the short-arm PAR the benchmark for male X-Y exchange \cite{Rouyer1986}.

Pritchard et al. used long-range restriction mapping to place MIC2 close to Y-specific sequence, narrowing the transition between recombining and non-recombining portions of the sex chromosomes \cite{sexchrompars_pritchard1987}. Ellis et al. subsequently identified an Alu insertion on the Y chromosome at the pseudoautosomal boundary and argued that this insertion helped define the proximal edge of PAR1 \cite{sexchrompars_ellis1989}. Their population follow-up showed that the boundary carries distinct X and Y histories, which is important for later debates over whether the boundary is fixed or polymorphic \cite{sexchrompars_ellis1990}.

Freije et al. identified PAR2 near Xq/Yq through microsatellite inheritance in CEPH pedigrees, showing that the long-arm X-Y telomeres also exchange \cite{sexchrompars_freije1992}. Graves, Wakefield and Toder then synthesized comparative evidence that PAR1 and PAR2 have different evolutionary origins; PAR1 was treated as a relic of added autosomal material, whereas PAR2 required more recent and more complex events \cite{sexchrompars_graves1998}. Lahn and Page's evolutionary-strata paper placed these recombining termini beside the non-recombining strata of the human X, making PARs the remaining exchange windows after successive steps of recombination suppression \cite{sexchrompars_lahn1999}.

The sequence era refined both ends. Ciccodicola et al. sequenced the Xq/Yq pseudoautosomal region and separated PAR2 into subregions with distinct gene regulation and evolutionary histories \cite{sexchrompars_ciccodicola2000}. Charchar et al. used comparative mapping to argue that PAR2 formed through multiple events, not a simple single duplication \cite{sexchrompars_charchar2003}. Skaletsky et al. sequenced the euchromatic male-specific Y and distinguished X-degenerate, X-transposed, ampliconic and pseudoautosomal sequence classes, providing the Y-side vocabulary needed to separate PARs from XTR \cite{sexchrompars_skaletsky2003}. Ross et al. then completed the chromosome-X reference analysis that named XAR, XCR, PAR1, PAR2 and XTR in one framework \cite{sexchrompars_ross2005}.

From 2007 onward, review and mapping papers turned these reference facts into genetic models. Mangs and Morris summarized PAR origin, function and disease; Flaquer et al. explained why PARs need special treatment in genetic epidemiology; and a sex-specific genetic map quantified the contrast between PAR1 and PAR2 recombination \cite{sexchrompars_mangs2007,sexchrompars_flaquer2008,sexchrompars_flaquer2009}. Hinch et al. then produced a fine-scale PAR1 recombination map from pedigrees and LD, showing intense male recombination and hotspot structure within PAR1 \cite{sexchrompars_hinch2014}. More recent papers have moved from static maps to polymorphism and mechanism: PAR1 length polymorphism and active extended PARs \cite{sexchrompars_mensah2014,sexchrompars_poriswanish2018,sexchrompars_poriswanish2025}, molecular features of PAR pairing \cite{sexchrompars_chu2017,sexchrompars_acquaviva2020}, population-genetic dynamics \cite{sexchrompars_monteiro2021,sexchrompars_bergman2022}, and T2T-resolution sex-chromosome assemblies \cite{sexchrompars_miga2020,sexchrompars_rhie2023,sexchrompars_hallast2023,sexchrompars_bellott2024}.

## Findings most relevant to claim C5 of the abstract

This topic directly anchors abstract claim **C5**, the cladistic analysis claim. The current abstract states that neighbor-joining trees recover known relationships, including Xp/Yp and Xq/Yq. In the sex-chromosome literature, those two relationships are not optional or speculative: Xp/Yp is PAR1, the obligate short-arm pairing region, and Xq/Yq is PAR2, the smaller long-arm pseudoautosomal region \cite{Rouyer1986,sexchrompars_freije1992,sexchrompars_mangs2007}. Thus, when the manuscript's similarity analysis recovers C15 as the Xp/Yp clade and C14 as the Xq/Yq clade, it is recovering the two best-known positive controls for subtelomeric X-Y homology. This is especially valuable because most other subtelomeric communities are interpreted by analogy to repeated exchange, duplication or concerted evolution. PARs provide the case where recombination is already part of the definition.

PAR1 gives the stronger mechanistic support. Genetic and sperm-typing studies consistently show that male X-Y exchange is concentrated in PAR1 at rates far above the genome average \cite{sexchrompars_flaquer2009,sexchrompars_hinch2014}. Human deletion evidence also supports a functional lower bound: complete loss of PAR1 can disrupt spermatogenesis, while partial deletions leaving a small centromeric PAR1 segment can still be transmitted paternally, implying that a surprisingly small remaining interval may suffice for X-Y pairing and crossover in some men \cite{sexchrompars_fukami2020}. This matters for the manuscript's interpretation of C15: near-identity between Xp and Yp is not merely shared ancestry but a maintained recombination substrate. The CROSSWALK's note that TAR1 is nearly absent from PAR1 also fits this interpretation: PAR1 does not need the same repeat-mediated subtelomeric machinery proposed for other PHRs because it has an established obligate crossover system.

PAR2 supports C5 differently. It is a true Xq/Yq homology and a valid positive control for the Xq/Yq clade, but the literature repeatedly treats it as shorter, more recently assembled, and less clearly essential for male disjunction than PAR1 \cite{sexchrompars_ciccodicola2000,sexchrompars_charchar2003,sexchrompars_monteiro2021}. Hinch et al. note that PAR2 crossovers are rare and approximately genome-average, unlike the extreme PAR1 landscape \cite{sexchrompars_hinch2014}. Therefore, C14 should be framed as "known PAR2 homology recovered" rather than as a second obligate-crossover equivalent to PAR1. If the NJ tree places an additional arm such as 18q near the Xp/Yp cluster, the PAR literature provides a constraint on interpretation: X-Y sequence identity within PAR1 is a mechanistically maintained sex-chromosome feature, whereas autosomal co-clustering would require separate subtelomeric duplication or exchange evidence rather than being folded into true pseudoautosomal biology.

The main contested points are boundary placement and boundary plasticity, not whether PAR1 and PAR2 exist. Cotter et al. showed that diversity across the X chromosome does not create a clean population-genetic cliff exactly at the annotated boundary, and Mensah et al. and Poriswanish et al. showed that some human Y chromosomes carry extended PAR1 sequence created by recurrent X-Y NAHR \cite{sexchrompars_cotter2016,sexchrompars_mensah2014,sexchrompars_poriswanish2018,sexchrompars_poriswanish2025}. Bellott et al. responded to a 2023 assembly-based boundary shift claim by re-examining genetic and sequence evidence and narrowing the canonical PAR1 boundary to a small interval near the historically mapped PAB \cite{sexchrompars_bellott2024}. For the current manuscript, that debate is useful rather than destabilizing: it says that PAR boundaries can be biologically plastic in individuals, but the canonical PAR1/PAR2 clades remain firm positive controls for C5.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

The closest adjacent topic is the broader pseudohomologous-region concept. PHRs and PARs both concern telomere-adjacent interchromosomal homology, but they should not be merged conceptually. PARs are the named sex-chromosome intervals where X and Y recombine and pseudoautosomal inheritance is observed. PHRs are an operational class of subtelomeric high-identity regions across the genome, many of which are inferred from alignment, community structure and pedigree patches rather than from classic sex-chromosome transmission. The PAR literature is therefore a benchmark: it gives one end of the mechanistic spectrum, where homologous recombination is obligatory and genetically mapped, against which weaker or more indirect PHR exchange evidence can be compared.

This topic also intersects the sex-chromosome assembly and pangenome-method topics. PAR1, PAR2 and XTR have been difficult to represent cleanly in reference genomes because their X and Y copies are nearly identical or recently related. Ross et al. and Skaletsky et al. supplied the first reference-era classification of XAR, XTR and PARs, but T2T-X and T2T-Y assemblies changed the practical baseline by adding sequence through formerly unresolved pseudoautosomal and ampliconic regions \cite{sexchrompars_ross2005,sexchrompars_skaletsky2003,sexchrompars_miga2020,sexchrompars_rhie2023}. Hallast et al.'s 43-Y assemblies then showed that Y structural variation remains extensive even after a complete reference sequence \cite{sexchrompars_hallast2023}. For the current manuscript, this reinforces the logic of an implicit pangenome graph: a reference-only X/Y representation risks forcing nearly identical PAR sequence into one coordinate convention, whereas a haplotype-aware all-to-all comparison can recover the clades as sequence relationships.

The topic also touches the meiotic-bouquet and 3D-genome topics. PAR1 is the clearest example of a short subtelomeric interval whose physical pairing is required for chromosome segregation. Mouse work has shown that PAR DNA and chromosomal axis structure can be remodeled to ensure DNA-break formation in the tiny sex-chromosome pairing segment \cite{sexchrompars_acquaviva2020}. PAR-TERRA work in mouse embryonic stem cells adds an RNA-mediated pairing model for sex-chromosome homologous regions \cite{sexchrompars_chu2017}. These papers do not prove the current human subtelomeric Hi-C model, but they make the general claim biologically plausible: chromosome ends are not passive sequence tails; they can be specialized nuclear and meiotic pairing platforms.

Finally, PARs intersect disease and dosage topics. PAR genes escape X inactivation, and SHOX dosage explains a substantial fraction of clinically recognized PAR1 phenotypes \cite{sexchrompars_mangs2007,sexchrompars_flaquer2008}. That is adjacent rather than central to the current abstract, but it matters for interpretation of gene-content analyses. A PAR gene's two-copy state in both sexes is not equivalent to ordinary X-linked dosage, and PAR copy-number changes can have phenotypic consequences independent of the subtelomeric community model.

## Open questions and recent developments

Recent work has shifted the field from "where are the PARs?" to "how stable are PAR boundaries, how much sequence is required for function, and how do complete assemblies change what we think we know?" The extended-PAR literature is the clearest example. Mensah et al. identified recurrent human PAR1 length polymorphism caused by X-to-Y translocation; Poriswanish et al. showed that ePAR segments can recombine in sperm; and a 2025 UK Biobank analysis expanded the scale to approximately 218,300 46,XY males, estimating ePAR incidence, recurrent origins across multiple Y haplogroups, reciprocal X deletions, and phenotype associations \cite{sexchrompars_mensah2014,sexchrompars_poriswanish2018,sexchrompars_poriswanish2025}. PAR1 is therefore not simply a fixed textbook interval. It is a recombination system with recurrent structural variants that can create new pseudoautosomal sequence in living populations.

At the same time, the 2024 boundary debate warns against over-reading assembly contiguity as recombination proof. Bellott et al. argued that historical genetic maps, crossovers, polymorphisms and sequence comparisons still support the original PAR1 boundary and narrow the likely position rather than shifting it by hundreds of kilobases \cite{sexchrompars_bellott2024}. This is a useful standard for the current manuscript: clades in a sequence-similarity tree are strong evidence of homology, but boundary claims and recombination claims require genetic, pedigree or gametic support. The manuscript's pedigree patches are therefore critical for the broader PHR story; for PARs, the analogous evidence has been accumulating for four decades.

Another open issue is why PAR1 is so efficient. Fine-scale maps establish extreme recombination and hotspot structure, and great-ape population genetics shows recombination-associated mutation and GC-biased processes shaping PAR1 evolution \cite{sexchrompars_hinch2014,sexchrompars_bergman2022}. Mouse molecular work suggests that repeat arrays, axis elongation, ANKRD31/MEI4-related factors and delayed synapsis can help force DNA breaks into the PAR \cite{sexchrompars_acquaviva2020}. How directly those mouse mechanisms translate to human PAR1 remains unsettled. Human data from Fukami et al. suggest that even a much reduced PAR1 can support spermatogenesis in some families, but this does not identify the molecular features that make the residual interval competent \cite{sexchrompars_fukami2020}.

PAR2 remains the larger interpretive gap. It is a valid Xq/Yq pseudoautosomal interval and a positive control for the manuscript's C14 clade, but its evolutionary origin, low recombination, transcriptional regulation and functional necessity differ from PAR1 \cite{sexchrompars_ciccodicola2000,sexchrompars_charchar2003,sexchrompars_monteiro2021}. Future pangenome-scale sex-chromosome assemblies should be able to ask whether PAR2 varies structurally across human haplotypes in ways that older references missed, and whether weak PAR2 recombination leaves detectable population-genetic signatures distinct from nearby Xq/Yq subtelomeric homology.

For the present manuscript, the practical recommendation is to use PAR1 and PAR2 as calibrated controls, not as generic examples. C15 should be described as recovery of the canonical PAR1 Xp/Yp relationship, with any 18q proximity treated as a separate subtelomeric observation needing independent support. C14 should be described as recovery of the shorter PAR2 Xq/Yq relationship. Together they strengthen C5 because they show that the NJ/community pipeline finds the two known sex-chromosome pseudoautosomal clades before asking readers to accept newer autosomal subtelomeric clades.
