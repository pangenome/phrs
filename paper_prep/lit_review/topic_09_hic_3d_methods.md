# Topic 09: Hi-C, Pore-C, single-cell 3D, and mouse meiotic Hi-C — methods and findings, 2009–present

## Claim map

This review anchors the Hi-C / 3D genome methodology and results literature to the current abstract's claims as follows. The literature directly supports **C7** ("Bulk and single-cell Hi-C in GM12878 and sperm, together with mouse meiotic Hi-C peaking at zygotene (Mantel ρ=0.715), tie sequence homology to nuclear-envelope proximity through the meiotic bouquet"). It provides partial support for **C8** (the recombination-via-proximity mechanistic thesis) by connecting the empirical Hi-C results to the meiotic bouquet and the LINC complex mechanism. It supports **C5** indirectly, because the named community clades (4q–10q, 10p–18p, acrocentrics) are the same structures that appear enriched in subtelomeric Hi-C contact maps. It supplies methodological substrate for **C2** (3D contact as an independent validation of the pangenome-graph communities) and background context for **C4** (prior bulk Hi-C showed non-random inter-chromosomal contacts at specific loci, but only the T2T-aware analysis resolves subtelomeres fully).

## Era overview

The chromosome conformation capture (3C) framework, introduced by Dekker and colleagues in 2002, provided the first molecular handle on spatial proximity between specific genomic loci. Hi-C — the genome-wide generalization — was reported by Lieberman-Aiden and colleagues in 2009 and constitutes the foundational paper for this entire field \cite{hic3d_liebermanaiden2009}. By coupling proximity-ligation with massively parallel paired-end sequencing, Hi-C converted the three-dimensional nuclear organization of a genome into a symmetric contact-frequency matrix: the frequency with which any two loci were cross-linked in formaldehyde reflects their average nuclear proximity. The 2009 map, built at 1 Mb resolution from human GM06990 lymphoblastoid cells, immediately established two canonical structures. First, chromosome territories: contacts within chromosomes far exceed contacts between chromosomes, even accounting for genomic distance. Second, genome-wide compartments: the Hi-C matrix at megabase scale decomposes into a checkerboard of open (A) and closed (B) chromatin, with A–A and B–B contacts enriched relative to A–B.

The following decade was dominated by progressive increases in resolution and throughput. Dixon and colleagues identified topological domains (TADs) — megabase-scale self-interacting insulated units — in both mouse and human cells using Hi-C at 40 kb resolution \cite{hic3d_dixon2012}. Imakaev and colleagues solved the normalization problem with iterative correction and eigenvector decomposition (ICE), which removes distance-dependent and locus-specific biases without requiring any genomic annotation \cite{hic3d_imakaev2012}. The landmark in situ Hi-C paper from Rao and colleagues in 2014 pushed resolution to near-kilobase scale in GM12878 B-lymphoblastoid cells, identifying over ten thousand chromatin loops (predominantly CTCF-convergent) and six sub-compartment classes \cite{hic3d_rao2014}. Because GM12878 is the very cell line used in the current manuscript's bulk Hi-C analysis — and the cell line from which Tan et al.'s Dip-C single-cell data derive — this paper sets the reference for what subtelomeric contact patterns in an LCL look like at high resolution.

The methodological landscape broadened toward the end of the decade. Single-cell Hi-C approaches revealed cell-to-cell variability in chromosome architecture hidden by population averages. Long-read-based Pore-C and CiFi methods demonstrated that repetitive and duplicated regions suppressed by short-read mapping could, for the first time, be reliably profiled in three dimensions. Mouse meiotic Hi-C pinpointed the cell-cycle stage at which subtelomeres are most tightly co-localized in the nucleus. Together, these advances created the methodological substrate for the current paper's central experiment: using multiple independent 3D-genome technologies at T2T-quality reference coordinates to test whether subtelomeric sequence-similarity communities predict nuclear proximity.

## Key papers in chronological order

**Lieberman-Aiden et al. 2009 (Science).** Hi-C foundational paper. At 1 Mb resolution, the human genome shows compartment organization (A/B) and fractal-globule polymer compaction. Inter-chromosomal contacts exist at detectable but low levels; small, gene-rich chromosomes cluster more strongly in the nucleus \cite{hic3d_liebermanaiden2009}. This paper defines the experimental and analytical vocabulary used in all downstream work.

**Dixon et al. 2012 (Nature).** By applying Hi-C at 40 kb resolution to mouse and human ES and differentiated cells, Dixon and colleagues identified TADs — contiguous intervals of elevated self-interaction bounded by sharp transitions. TAD boundaries are enriched for CTCF binding and are evolutionarily conserved between mouse and human \cite{hic3d_dixon2012}. TADs are the sub-compartment structure most relevant to subtelomeric regions, because PHRs reside at chromosomal extremities where TAD organization is often incomplete or structurally distinct from canonical gene-rich domains.

**Imakaev et al. 2012 (Nature Methods).** ICE normalization makes quantitative comparison of contact frequencies between loci and between experiments possible \cite{hic3d_imakaev2012}. The current manuscript's within-community (W) vs. between-community (B) enrichment tests and Mantel correlations depend directly on ICE-corrected matrices (via the hicexplorer 3.7.4 pipeline); reporting the B/W statistic implicitly credits this normalization framework.

**Nagano et al. 2013 (Nature).** First single-cell Hi-C. By processing individual nuclei rather than millions, Nagano and colleagues showed that while A/B compartments and TADs are present in single cells, chromosome interactions are highly variable at the scale of individual loops or topological boundaries \cite{hic3d_nagano2013}. This paper motivates the single-cell extensions — Dip-C and sperm scHi-C — used in the current manuscript, because it establishes that population-averaged bulk Hi-C both masks and reveals cell-to-cell variation.

**Rao et al. 2014 (Cell).** In-situ Hi-C in GM12878 at ~1 kb resolution. Identified >10,000 chromatin loops, mostly anchored at convergent CTCF motifs \cite{hic3d_rao2014}. The GM12878 data in the current manuscript (CHM13 at Mantel rho=0.66; HG002 at rho=0.66; five diploid HPRC samples) are all LCL datasets processed through the same hicexplorer pipeline, making the Rao 2014 landmark the baseline expectation for what subtelomeric inter-chromosomal contacts should look like in this cell type.

**Stevens et al. 2017 (Nature).** 3D structures of individual mammalian genomes by single-cell Hi-C. Stevens and colleagues combined single-cell Hi-C with structural modeling (via restraint-based energy minimization) to recover whole-genome 3D structures at ~100 kb resolution in single mouse embryonic stem cells \cite{hic3d_stevens2017}. Crucially, they found that A/B compartments and lamina-associated domains are consistently organized at the genome level in every cell, while TADs and loops are variable. This finding is important for the current manuscript: it suggests that the community-based co-localization measured by bulk Hi-C and the Dip-C Mantel test is driven by consistent structural features (compartments, lamina contacts) rather than cell-to-cell loop variability.

**Tan et al. 2018 (Science) — Dip-C.** The first method to reconstruct diploid 3D genome structures of single human cells \cite{Tan2018}. Applied to GM12878 B-lymphoblastoid cells (the same LCL line used throughout the current manuscript), Dip-C enables haplotype-resolved 3D positioning of each genomic locus. The current manuscript remaps 16 of the Tan 2018 cells to the T2T-CHM13v2.0 reference, then tests whether subtelomeric sequence-similarity communities predict intra-nucleus proximity: 6.9% closer within-community (Wilcoxon p=3.8e-04, Mantel rho=0.296 p=0.002). The "supplementary S" pseudo-community of arms with zero PHR signal is 11% farther apart — a clean negative control.

**Alavattam et al. 2019 (Nature Structural & Molecular Biology).** Hi-C through mouse spermatogenesis, showing attenuated chromatin compartmentalization in meiosis \cite{hic3d_alavattam2019}. TADs are largely lost in pachytene spermatocytes; A/B compartments persist but weakly. Round spermatids show an intermediate state; mature sperm have re-established radial Rabl-like organization. This establishes the expected backdrop for the Xu 2025 sperm data \cite{Xu2025}: sperm lack TADs, which explains why the community-based enrichment in sperm (W/B=0.401, 60% closer) must arise from compartment-level or territory-level co-organization rather than loop-based proximity.

**Patel et al. 2019 (EMBO Journal).** Mouse meiotic Hi-C at multiple prophase I stages, characterizing how chromosome loops and axes reorganize relative to gene transcription \cite{Patel2019}. Key finding: meiotic chromatin is organized into large loops (500 kb–1.6 Mb) emanating from a proteinaceous axis containing meiotic cohesins. The current manuscript uses the Zuo 2021 dataset for the quantitative Mantel analysis, but Patel 2019 provides essential background for the loop-size prediction: if PHR regions (median 105 kb) fit within a single meiotic loop, they are brought into the spatial proximity of the chromosome axis, placing them adjacent to the nuclear-envelope-anchored telomere — exactly the configuration the meiotic bouquet hypothesis requires.

**Ulahannan et al. 2019 / Deshpande et al. 2022 (Nature Biotechnology) — Pore-C.** Oxford Nanopore sequencing of cross-linked, restriction-digested chromatin concatemers yields multi-way chromatin contacts without amplification \cite{hic3d_deshpande2022}. Unlike Hi-C (which captures pairwise contacts), Pore-C reads carry information about three or more loci on a single long read, enabling phase-aware contact information and improved coverage in repetitive regions due to longer read mapping. The current manuscript applies Pore-C to HG002 (alongside CiFi); the HG002 Pore-C dataset shows Mantel rho=0.657, indistinguishable from the HG002 bulk Hi-C, confirming that the subtelomeric signal is technology-agnostic and not an artifact of short-read multi-mapping.

**Zuo et al. 2021 (Nature Communications) — Stage-resolved meiotic Hi-C.** Hi-C of sorted mouse spermatocytes across four meiotic prophase I stages: leptotene, zygotene, pachytene, and diplotene \cite{Zuo2021}. The current manuscript's central mouse finding: per-arm-pair Spearman correlation between subtelomeric sequence similarity and Hi-C contact frequency peaks at zygotene (Mantel rho=0.715, p=4.4e-55, n=344 arm pairs). Leptotene=0.680, pachytene=0.677, diplotene=0.574. The zygotene peak is the meiotic stage when the bouquet is most tightly formed — telomeres are maximally clustered at the nuclear envelope, and homolog pairing is actively proceeding. The diplotene attenuation is expected: by diplotene, the bouquet has dissolved as crossover resolution and chromosome desynapsis proceed.

**Xu et al. 2025 (Nature Communications) — Sperm single-cell 3D.** Developed an enhanced 3C assay enabling whole-genome 3D structure reconstruction for single mammalian sperm at haploid resolution \cite{Xu2025}. Human sperm retain A/B compartments and chromosome territories but lack TADs. The current manuscript remaps 20 sperm cells to PHR-specific coordinates and finds W/B=0.401 (60% closer within-community, Fisher p=3.9e-51, Mantel rho=0.202 p=0.023). The "supplementary S" negative control arms are 40% farther apart in sperm than within the zero-signal community. This sperm signal suggests that community-based nuclear organization is maintained through spermiogenesis, consistent with a Rabl-like configuration (centromere-telomere polarity) that positions homologous chromosome ends in proximity even in post-meiotic haploid cells.

**CiFi (Cramer et al., 2024/2025, Nature Communications).** Chromosome conformation capture with PacBio HiFi long reads \cite{hic3d_cifi2025}. CiFi concatenates cross-linked chromatin fragments into multi-kilobase HiFi reads (350 bp–2 kb segments), improving read mapping across centromeres, segmental duplications, and repetitive subtelomeric regions relative to short-read Hi-C. The current manuscript applies CiFi to HG002 alongside Pore-C; the CiFi W/B and Mantel results corroborate the bulk Hi-C and Pore-C, providing four independent technology validations for the subtelomeric 3D signal. In the RPE-1 analysis (three independent datasets), async and mitotic CiFi data reinforce the same community-based enrichment seen in LCL bulk Hi-C.

**Nurk et al. 2022 (Science) — T2T-CHM13.** The first complete, gapless human genome assembly, including all centromeres, short arms of acrocentric chromosomes, and subtelomeric regions that were previously unresolved \cite{hic3d_nurk2022}. For Hi-C analysis of subtelomeres, T2T-CHM13 is transformative: prior GRCh38-aligned Hi-C could not reliably map reads to repetitive PHR regions, causing under-counting of true subtelomeric contacts and over-counting of multi-mapping artifacts. The current manuscript's entire Hi-C analysis uses T2T coordinates (CHM13v2.0 and haplotype-resolved HPRC assemblies), making T2T-CHM13 a methodological prerequisite rather than a biological finding.

## Findings most relevant to claim C7 of the abstract

The abstract's C7 is: "Bulk and single-cell Hi-C in GM12878 and sperm, together with mouse meiotic Hi-C peaking at zygotene (Mantel ρ=0.715), tie sequence homology to nuclear-envelope proximity through the meiotic bouquet."

The prior literature most directly relevant to this claim addresses three sub-questions: (1) do subtelomeres show detectable non-random inter-chromosomal contacts in existing Hi-C data? (2) What is the correct cellular and reference context in which to interpret those contacts? (3) Is there an existing mechanistic hypothesis connecting sequence homology to nuclear proximity?

**Subtelomeric inter-chromosomal contacts.** Standard bulk Hi-C maps, including the Rao 2014 GM12878 dataset, show suppression of inter-chromosomal contacts relative to intra-chromosomal contacts as a general rule. This suppression has two causes: true biology (chromosome territories) and technical artifact (multi-mapping of repetitive reads to the wrong locus). Because subtelomeric regions are repetitive and were incompletely assembled in GRCh38, previous Hi-C analyses of subtelomeric contacts were contaminated by both. The current manuscript's key technical contribution to C7 is the demonstration that, using T2T coordinates and the flanking-region paradox as an internal control, the subtelomeric signal is larger in flanking unique-sequence regions than in the repetitive PHR itself: HG002 flanking B/W=0.002 vs. PHR B/W=0.027, 13× enrichment in flanking signal. This rules out multi-mapping as the source of the signal.

**Reference context: T2T and haplotype-resolved.** The current analysis uses CHM13 and haplotype-resolved HPRC assemblies for all six Hi-C LCL samples (five diploid HPRC individuals plus CHM13). The Mantel correlation between subtelomeric sequence similarity and Hi-C contact frequency ranges from rho=0.656 (CHM13) to rho=0.827 (NA19036 at 10 kb resolution) across samples. An exclusion-control experiment demonstrates that the signal strengthens after removing acrocentric or sex-chromosome communities — the strongest communities — consistent with a genuine distributed signal rather than a few dominant pairs driving the correlation. The RPE-1 dataset (a non-lymphoblastoid cell type) shows similar enrichment, arguing for a broad structural feature rather than a GM12878-specific artifact.

**Single-cell 3D and the meiotic bouquet.** The Dip-C analysis of 16 GM12878 cells (Tan 2018 data remapped to T2T) \cite{Tan2018} shows 6.9% within-community proximity enrichment (p=3.8e-04). The sperm scHi-C (Xu 2025, 20 cells) shows 60% closer within-community (p=3.9e-51) \cite{Xu2025}. These two single-cell results bracket an important biological contrast: lymphoblastoid cells (somatic diploid) show a modest but significant enrichment; mature sperm (haploid post-meiotic) show a strong enrichment despite lacking TADs. The mouse meiotic result (Zuo 2021, rho=0.715 at zygotene) \cite{Zuo2021} connects these observations mechanistically through the meiotic bouquet: during zygotene, telomeres attach to the nuclear envelope via MAJIN-SUN1-KASH5 LINC complexes, cluster into the bouquet, and undergo rapid movements driven by cytoskeletal motors. This places subtelomeric sequence on the nuclear surface, in proximity to other subtelomeres, precisely when ectopic recombination is most likely. The Patel 2019 mouse Hi-C data \cite{Patel2019} confirms that meiotic chromatin is organized into large loops (mean ~800 kb at zygotene), consistent with PHRs (median 105 kb) fitting within a single loop and thus being positioned near the nuclear-envelope-anchored telomere.

**What remains contested.** The causal direction is not established by Hi-C data alone. Three interpretations are compatible with the empirical C7 evidence: (a) sequence similarity drives nuclear proximity (the bouquet hypothesis); (b) nuclear proximity drives sequence similarity by enabling ectopic exchange (the feedback-loop hypothesis); (c) some third factor (e.g., lamina association via D4Z4-CTCF-lamin tethering) independently determines both sequence community membership and 3D position. The causal loop cannot be closed without LINC complex mutant Hi-C data from mouse subtelomeric regions at T2T resolution. Existing LINC mutant experiments (SUN1 W151R; Zuo 2021 data from GSE155142) show globally attenuated telomere clustering, but no analysis targeting subtelomeric sequence-similarity communities in the mutant has been published. This is the highest-priority next experiment named in the current manuscript's testable-predictions section.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

**Topic 04 — PAR and sex-chromosome subtelomeres.** PAR1 (chrX_p/chrY_p) and PAR2 (chrX_q/chrY_q) are the clearest cases in which subtelomeric sequence homology drives nuclear co-localization. Rao 2014's GM12878 Hi-C shows a discrete inter-chromosomal contact block between chrX and chrY at their PAR regions; the current manuscript's Mantel analysis shows that removing PAR communities causes the Mantel correlation to increase (from rho=0.657 to rho=0.790 in HG002), confirming that PAR co-localization is so strong that it partially inflates the reference estimate. The PAR comparison is an important calibration: PAR proximity is driven by obligate crossover, not by the bouquet-mediated ectopic-recombination mechanism the current manuscript proposes for non-PAR subtelomeres. Topic 04's lit review should establish this distinction as a boundary condition for interpreting C7.

**Topic 03 — PHR concept and the Flint/Mefford two-domain model.** The Hi-C findings of the current manuscript map onto the architectural predictions of Flint et al. 1997 and Mefford and Trask 2002. If the distal (many-chromosome-sharing) domain and the proximal (restricted sharing) domain have different chromatin characters, they should show different Hi-C signals. The current manuscript's flanking-region paradox — where the unique-sequence region centromere-ward of the PHR shows stronger Hi-C signal than the PHR itself — may reflect the two-domain boundary: the unique flanking sequence may be in closer nuclear proximity to community partners than the most-distal repetitive domain, either because it is less multi-mapping-suppressed or because it is the functional unit of locus-specific nuclear positioning. Topic 03's lit review establishes the prediction that the two-domain architecture should manifest in Hi-C, which is tested here.

**Topic 11 — Pedigree recombination.** The pedigree evidence (538 inter-chromosomal patches, 92% within Leiden communities) provides the direct evidence for ongoing exchange that C7's proximity hypothesis predicts. If community members are brought into proximity by the bouquet, then ectopic recombination between community members should produce pedigree-observable patches — and this is exactly what is found. The Hi-C evidence and the pedigree evidence are methodologically independent: Hi-C measures average nuclear proximity; the pedigree measures actual exchange events. Their consistency is the cross-technology validation that gives C7 and C8 their combined force. Topic 11's literature review should cite the bouquet-proximity mechanism as the theoretical framework within which pedigree patches are interpreted.

**Meiotic bouquet biology (partially covered by adjacent topics).** The meiotic bouquet mechanism — telomere attachment to the nuclear envelope via MAJIN–SUN1–KASH5 LINC complexes, followed by cytoskeletal-driven rapid chromosome movements — is the bridge between the Hi-C data (which is a contact-frequency measurement) and the biological process (which is a physical event in prophase-I nuclei) \cite{hic3d_harper2004}. Harper et al. 2004 review the conserved features of bouquet formation across eukaryotes; the key mammalian-specific point is that LINC-mediated telomere clustering in mammals occurs at exactly the leptotene–zygotene transition, coinciding with peak DSB formation and the initiation of homology search. The Koszul 2008 actin-motor meiotic chromosome dynamics study \cite{hic3d_koszul2008} (initially in yeast) and subsequent mammalian LINC complex work establish that these movements are essential for efficient homolog pairing, not merely incidental chromosome shuffling.

## Open questions and recent developments

**T2T-aware Hi-C for repetitive subtelomeres.** The most technically consequential open question is whether T2T-quality references fundamentally change the Hi-C contact landscape at subtelomeres. The current manuscript's entire analysis uses T2T coordinates, but the benchmark comparison — Mantel rho with GRCh38 vs. CHM13 for the same cell line — is not explicitly reported. Nurk et al. 2022 \cite{hic3d_nurk2022} show that ~200 Mb of sequence newly resolved in T2T-CHM13 is predominantly repetitive; sub-setting to the subtelomeric windows used in the current manuscript would quantify how much of the Hi-C subtelomeric signal was previously invisible. The HiCExplorer pipeline (Wolff et al. 2018) \cite{hic3d_wolff2018} used in the current analysis is T2T-compatible via reference substitution, but the specific choice of mappability cutoffs and bin sizes for repetitive regions is a methodological decision that affects sensitivity at PHR loci.

**Long-read 3D genomics for repetitive regions.** CiFi \cite{hic3d_cifi2025} and Pore-C \cite{hic3d_deshpande2022} are emerging as the preferred tools for Hi-C analysis of repetitive and duplicated genomic regions because long reads can be mapped with higher specificity even in high-identity paralogs. The current manuscript applies both to HG002, and shows concordance with bulk Hi-C. However, these datasets have lower genome-wide coverage than the six bulk Hi-C LCL samples (which reach genome-wide saturation at 5 kb resolution). A systematic comparison of the Mantel correlation as a function of read-pair coverage and reference quality, across short-read Hi-C, Pore-C, and CiFi, would establish the quantitative sensitivity tradeoff and is not yet published. The scNanoHi-C method \cite{hic3d_scnanoHiC2023} — which combines nanopore long reads with single-cell resolution — has not yet been applied to subtelomeric community analysis; its application would extend the Dip-C and sperm single-cell analyses with the mapping advantages of long reads in repetitive regions.

**Human meiotic Hi-C.** A critical gap: the Zuo 2021 zygotene-peak result that drives the abstract's C7 mechanistic claim is from mouse spermatocytes \cite{Zuo2021}. Human meiotic Hi-C is technically challenging because human male meiosis cannot be synchronized or purified to a single prophase-I stage with the same efficiency as mouse spermatogenesis. Stage-resolved chromatin conformation dynamics in human meiosis remain an open challenge; a 2024 preprint (Vara et al.) has begun to address this but the data are not yet at the resolution or stage-purity of the Zuo 2021 mouse dataset \cite{hic3d_vara2019}. The cross-species inference from mouse zygotene to human prophase-I rests on three well-established homologies — LINC complex structure, bouquet timing, and loop-axis organization — but the direct human zygotene Hi-C correlation of subtelomeric sequence similarity is a prediction rather than an observation.

**Single-cell Hi-C of meiotic cells.** The scNanoHi-C2 framework \cite{hic3d_scnanoHiC2_2025} has recently been applied to mouse embryonic germ cells, revealing dynamic 3D reorganization during meiotic entry. Extension to stage-pure meiotic prophase I cells in mouse — combined with PHR community analysis — would allow the Mantel correlation trajectory (leptotene → zygotene → pachytene → diplotene in Zuo 2021 bulk data) to be validated at single-cell resolution, testing whether the zygotene peak arises from a subset of cells or uniformly across the prophase-I population.

**LINC complex mutant Hi-C.** The highest-priority unresolved question (named as a testable prediction in the current manuscript) is whether the bouquet mechanism specifically drives the subtelomeric community-based proximity signal. Zuo 2021 generated SUN1 W151R mutant zygotene Hi-C data (GEO: GSE155142) that show globally attenuated telomere clustering. Re-analysis of this dataset with PHR community coordinates and the Mantel test used in the current manuscript would provide a direct test: if the Mantel rho drops in the mutant relative to wild-type, the LINC-bouquet mechanism is implicated as causal. If the rho is preserved, the proximity signal must arise from another mechanism — likely the Rabl configuration or nuclear-lamina tethering — that survives bouquet disruption.

**Alavattam 2019 and the meiotic compartment context.** The Alavattam 2019 dataset \cite{hic3d_alavattam2019} demonstrates that A/B compartments are attenuated but present at zygotene and pachytene, while TADs are largely lost. This context is important for interpreting the Zuo 2021 subtelomeric signal: the community-based enrichment at meiotic stages cannot be a TAD-mediated effect (TADs are gone), and it is likely driven by a compartment-like organization that positions community arms in the same nuclear hemisphere, or by direct telomere-tethering effects. The resolution of the current manuscript's meiotic analysis is 1 Mb, which does not allow distinction between these mechanisms; higher-resolution meiotic Hi-C from Patel 2019 \cite{Patel2019} suggests that loop anchors and compartment boundaries shift substantially relative to somatic cells, implying that PHR loci may enter chromatin environments quite different from the GM12878 bulk Hi-C context.

**Population-scale and haplotype-resolved Hi-C.** The current manuscript uses six Hi-C samples (five HPRC diploid individuals plus CHM13) with per-haplotype assignment of PHR regions. The finding that per-individual Spearman correlations (NA19036 rho=0.827; HG02148 rho=0.809) exceed the aggregate Mantel rho (rho=0.66) suggests that within-individual community assignments are tighter than the population average, likely because PHR region boundaries vary between haplotypes. Haplotype-resolved Hi-C at deep coverage — approaching the level of the 2024 sperm scHi-C dataset \cite{Xu2025} — would allow the subtelomeric contact map to be decomposed by haplotype, testing whether the cross-arm haplotype or the self-arm haplotype shows stronger community proximity (a testable prediction that reached a null result in the current manuscript due to small N).
