# Topic 10: Pangenome Graph Methods and the Implicit Pangenome Graph, 2017–present

## Claim map

This review anchors to **C2** (implicit pangenome graph: reference-free, all-to-all, ~12% pairwise sampling, no chromosomal partitioning) as the primary claim. The methodological lineage reviewed here explains why the all-vs-all pairwise alignment set produced by wfmash *is* the pangenome graph for this project, why IMPG transitive-closure querying is the canonical operation on that graph, and why an implicit rather than explicit graph representation is methodologically superior for subtelomere analysis. It also supports **C1** (HPRC v2 companion-paper framing) by documenting the computational lineage from which the current work descends, and supports **C4** and **C5** indirectly by explaining why reference-free all-to-all alignment can recover interchromosomal homology that partitioned or reference-biased approaches miss.

## Era overview

The inadequacy of a single linear reference genome for representing population-level sequence variation was recognized long before the practical tools to address it existed. The human reference genome (GRCh38) captures one haplotype of every locus and systematically underrepresents regions where multiple structurally distinct haplotypes co-exist — a bias acute enough that it was called out in the original human genome sequencing publications and revisited repeatedly over the following two decades [@IHGSC2001; @Eichler2001]. The pangenome graph movement, which coalesced between roughly 2015 and 2025, responded to that inadequacy by representing a collection of genomes as a sequence graph in which paths encode individual haplotypes and nodes encode shared sequence segments.

Two technical threads set the stage. The first was partial order alignment (POA), which Lee, Grasso, and Sharlow introduced in 2002 as a graph-based generalization of multiple sequence alignment [@Lee2002]. POA represented a multiple alignment as a directed acyclic graph (DAG), allowing each new sequence to be aligned to the existing graph rather than to a reduced profile, and introduced the concept of "homologous recombination" as an edit operator that the graph naturally accommodates. This idea of a sequence graph as a direct object of alignment, not merely a storage format, is the conceptual seed for almost every later pangenome tool. The second thread was efficient approximate long-read alignment at whole-genome scale. As long-read assemblers began producing chromosome-length contigs, the field needed fast methods to compare whole-genome assemblies against each other and against reference sequences — work that eventually led to MashMap [@Jain2018] and its derivatives.

The pangenome graph field crystallized around 2017–2019 with the vg toolkit and the GFA format. Garrison et al.'s 2018 vg paper established the foundational toolkit for constructing, manipulating, and using variation graphs for read alignment, variant calling, and haplotype inference at the scale of the human genome [@Garrison2018]. The Annual Review paper by Eizenga et al. in 2020 consolidated the field's concepts, vocabularies, and open problems [@Eizenga2020]. The period 2020–2022 then saw differentiation into two philosophically distinct graph-building strategies — reference-seeded progressive construction (minigraph, Minigraph-Cactus) versus reference-free all-to-all construction (seqwish, pggb) — with important consequences for which variation classes the resulting graph captures. The T2T-CHM13 assembly in 2022 and the HPRC draft pangenome in 2023 then provided the high-quality haplotype-resolved assemblies that made human pangenome graphs practical at population scale [@Nurk2022; @Liao2023]. The IMPG tool, which treats an all-vs-all pairwise alignment file as an implicit pangenome graph and supports transitive-closure queries without ever materializing the explicit GFA, is the most recent conceptual step in this lineage and is the direct computational substrate for the current subtelomere study.

## Key papers in chronological order

**Lee, Grasso, and Sharlow (2002) — Partial order alignment.** Published in *Bioinformatics*, this paper introduced the POA algorithm, which aligns sequences to a directed acyclic graph representation of an existing multiple alignment rather than to a linear profile [@Lee2002]. The key insight — that a graph can directly serve as the alignment target, and that traversals through the graph encode alternative evolutionary histories — is the conceptual ancestor of variation graphs, seqwish, and smoothxg. For the subtelomere project, POA matters because smoothxg (the graph normalization step in pggb) applies blocked POA to locally resolve the tangled alignments that arise when many near-identical subtelomeric sequences are placed in the same graph neighborhood.

**Garrison et al. (2018) — vg: variation graph toolkit.** Published in *Nature Biotechnology*, this paper launched the variation graph framework as a practical toolkit for human genomics [@Garrison2018]. A variation graph is a bidirected sequence graph in which nodes hold DNA sequence, edges connect compatible sequence adjacencies, and paths record individual haplotype traversals. The vg toolkit provided implementations for graph construction from VCF+reference, de novo from whole-genome alignments, or from multiple sequence alignments; indexing via generalized compressed suffix arrays (GCSA2); and read mapping with improvements in accuracy over a linear reference, especially at structural variant loci. The paper documented reduced reference bias compared to BWA-MEM across thousands of SNPs and structural variants in NA12878. This work established the conceptual and data-format vocabulary (GFA, GBWT, GCSA) that all subsequent pangenome tools have either adopted or responded to.

**Jain et al. (2018) — MashMap: fast approximate long-read alignment.** Published in *Bioinformatics*, this paper introduced MashMap, a sketching-based algorithm that estimates alignment identity from minimizers and MinHash before performing full base-level alignment [@Jain2018]. MashMap enabled rapid whole-genome-to-genome comparison — essential for constructing all-vs-all alignment sets at HPRC scale — and its internal similarity estimation is the conceptual precursor of wfmash's prefiltering step. The k-mer-sparsified prefilter that produces the ~12% pairwise evaluation rate in the current subtelomere study descends directly from MashMap's design.

**Eizenga et al. (2020) — Pangenome Graphs (review).** Published in the *Annual Review of Genomics and Human Genetics*, this paper surveyed the field's concepts, data structures, algorithms, and open problems [@Eizenga2020]. It defined graph genome representations, distinguished sequence graphs from variation graphs from de Bruijn graphs, discussed path-aware indexing (GBWT), and laid out the read-alignment, variant-calling, and genotyping use cases. The review also identified the bias-vs-completeness tradeoff at the core of all pangenome graph construction approaches: reference-seeded methods are faster but inherit reference biases, while all-vs-all methods are unbiased but expensive. This framing directly motivates the current project's use of all-to-all wfmash alignment as the implicit graph substrate.

**Li et al. (2020) — Minigraph: reference pangenome graph construction.** Published in *Genome Biology*, this paper proposed the minigraph approach to constructing reference-anchored pangenome graphs [@Li2020]. Minigraph aligns new assembly sequences to an existing graph using a seed-chain-extend strategy with minimizer seeds, adding structural variation as new graph bubbles. The rGFA data model it introduced allows each segment and link to carry an explicit linear coordinate reference, enabling back-projection to GRCh38 coordinates from any graph position. Minigraph is computationally efficient because it only aligns where sequences diverge from the growing graph, but its reference-anchored design means that sequences in highly variable or poorly assembled regions of the reference — such as subtelomeres — may not be incorporated correctly, and interchromosomal homologies that lack a reference-genome representation will not form graph edges.

**Guarracino, Heumos et al. (2022) — odgi: pangenome graph manipulation.** Published in *Bioinformatics*, this paper described odgi, a toolkit for space-efficient in-memory representation and manipulation of pangenome graphs in GFA format [@GuarracinoHeumos2022]. The odgi data structure records graph node sequences and path step deltas with variable-length integer encoding and cache-oblivious memory layout, achieving a substantially smaller memory footprint than naive adjacency lists at the scale of hundreds of haplotype-resolved assemblies. The `odgi similarity` subcommand — which computes pairwise Jaccard similarity from shared path traversals over any genomic window — is the direct tool used in the subtelomere project to produce the 245-million-pairwise-entry similarity matrix that drives community detection. The paper also described graph layout algorithms (path-guided SGD) that enable visualization of the graph's internal topology.

**Garrison and Guarracino (2022/2023) — Unbiased pangenome graphs (seqwish).** Published in *Bioinformatics*, this paper described seqwish, an algorithm for inducing a variation graph losslessly from a set of pairwise alignments in PAF format [@GarrisonGuarracino2023]. seqwish treats the alignment set as an implicit graph (each alignment is an equivalence claim that two sequence intervals overlap), computes transitive closures of overlapping sequence segments via an interval tree, and emits a GFA graph in which every input nucleotide is represented exactly once. The algorithm operates in external memory with lock-free parallel methods, making it practical for hundreds of gigabase-scale assemblies. The "unbiased" quality the paper emphasizes is that the resulting graph does not privilege any input sequence as a reference: all haplotypes contribute symmetrically to the graph topology. This is philosophically identical to the current study's reference-free framing, and seqwish is the graph-induction step in pggb.

**Nurk et al. (2022) — T2T-CHM13 complete human genome.** Published in *Science*, this paper completed the first truly gapless human genome assembly, closing all 22 autosomes plus the X chromosome to telomere-to-telomere continuity [@Nurk2022]. T2T-CHM13 adds 200 million base pairs to GRCh38, resolving all centromeric satellite arrays, the rDNA arrays on all five acrocentric short arms, and subtelomeric segmental duplications that had resisted short-read assembly. For the current study, T2T-CHM13 is both a data input (the reference anchor for subtelomeric flank extraction) and an existence proof: if a single complete assembly can expose this much previously inaccessible sequence, 465 near-complete assemblies can expose interchromosomal homology at population scale.

**Hickey et al. (2023/2024) — Minigraph-Cactus.** Published in *Nature Biotechnology*, this paper described the Minigraph-Cactus pipeline, which combines minigraph's rapid structural variant enumeration with the Cactus progressive aligner's base-level accuracy [@Hickey2024]. Minigraph-Cactus was the primary graph-building tool for the HPRC v1 draft pangenome. It achieves a pragmatic balance between speed and completeness, but its hierarchical progressive alignment still requires a reference genome as the root of the Cactus tree, meaning it inherits the reference's chromosomal partitioning structure. Minigraph-Cactus graphs do not naturally represent interchromosomal homology between telomere-adjacent regions on non-homologous chromosomes, which is precisely what the current subtelomere study is designed to detect. This contrast underscores why the current project uses an all-to-all implicit graph approach rather than Minigraph-Cactus.

**Liao et al. (2023) — HPRC draft pangenome reference.** Published in *Nature*, this paper presented the first draft human pangenome reference from the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium, assembling 47 phased diploid genomes and using both pggb and Minigraph-Cactus graphs for downstream analyses [@Liao2023]. The paper demonstrated that pangenome-based short-read analysis reduces small-variant errors by 34% and increases structural variant detection by 104% compared to GRCh38-only workflows. HPRC v1 is the predecessor dataset whose methods and data the current HPRC v2 companion project extends to 232 individuals (465 near-complete assemblies). The current study thus inherits the HPRC coordinate and assembly framework while adding the implicit pangenome graph methodology for subtelomere-specific analysis.

**Garrison, Andreace et al. (2024) — Building pangenome graphs (pggb).** Published in *Nature Methods*, this paper formally described the pggb pipeline: wfmash all-to-all alignment → seqwish graph induction → smoothxg POA normalization → gfaffix simplification [@Garrison2024pggb]. The paper demonstrated pggb on human chromosome 6 MHC assemblies, the Drosophila pangenome, and whole-human-chromosome datasets, showing that the resulting graphs capture sequence variation without reference bias at all scales from SNPs to megabase-scale structural variants. The pggb philosophy — that the all-to-all PAF set produced by wfmash implicitly defines the complete homology relationship among all input sequences, and that graph induction and normalization are downstream operations that materialize those relationships as GFA — is the direct precursor of the IMPG implicit-graph framing in the current project.

**Guarracino, Kille, and Garrison (2023–present) — IMPG: implicit pangenome graph.** Available at https://github.com/pangenome/impg, this tool takes the logical endpoint of the pggb implicit-graph philosophy: instead of materializing an explicit GFA from the all-vs-all alignment set, IMPG treats the PAF set directly as the implicit graph and answers range queries by projection through the alignment network [@IMPG2023]. The core operation is `impg query -x`: given a genomic interval on any sequence in the PAF, IMPG finds every interval in every other sequence that is aligned (directly or transitively) to the query region. Transitive closure is implemented via cache-oblivious interval trees over compact CIGAR-delta representations, making it both memory-efficient and fast for the ~88 GB PAF set produced from the subtelomere project's 18,827 sequences. IMPG's output — the set of all homologous loci reachable from a query by any chain of overlapping pairwise alignments — is the operational definition of a pseudohomologous region community in the current study.

## Findings most relevant to C2 of the abstract

Claim C2 states: "We construct an implicit pangenome graph — a reference-free, all-to-all alignment sampling roughly 12% of pairwise haplotype combinations without chromosomal partitioning." Each element of this claim has a specific methodological justification rooted in the literature reviewed above.

**Reference-free, without chromosomal partitioning.** Reference-seeded graph builders such as Minigraph [@Li2020] and Minigraph-Cactus [@Hickey2024] inherit the chromosomal coordinates of the reference genome at the root of their construction. When the reference genome places chromosome 10p and chromosome 18p on separate chromosomes, those tools will never build an edge connecting them, even if the two loci are 95%-identical over 150 kb. This is not a bug but a design choice: reference-anchored graphs prioritize coordinate stability over completeness. The current study requires the opposite priority. Subtelomeric pseudohomologous regions are by definition interchromosomal: their defining property is that they share high-identity sequence with loci on other chromosomes, often at locations that no single reference genome assigns to both. An all-vs-all alignment that treats every haplotype sequence as a co-equal query and target will discover every such interchromosomal relationship regardless of chromosomal origin. The seqwish paper [@GarrisonGuarracino2023] named this property "unbiased" and proved it by showing that the resulting graphs capture structural variants that progressive methods miss; for subtelomeres, unbiasedness is not a desirable feature but a requirement.

**Implicit graph and the ~12% sampling rate.** wfmash's k-mer prefiltering evaluates approximately 12% of the C(n, 2) ≈ 1.77 × 10⁸ possible pairwise combinations among the 18,827 subtelomere-anchored flanks. This is not a random sample — wfmash is selective — but it is sparse relative to the complete pairwise comparison space. The Erdős-Rényi connectivity argument establishes that 12% is amply sufficient for transitive-closure reachability: for n = 18,827, the ER connectivity threshold is p* = log(n)/n ≈ 5.2 × 10⁻⁴, and 12% is approximately 230× above that threshold. A random sub-graph of G(n, p) at p = 0.12 is w.h.p. densely connected, meaning IMPG's transitive-closure query from any subtelomeric locus can in principle reach every other subtelomere through chains of overlapping pairwise alignments. No chromosomal partitioning bias constrains this reachability: the only constraint is the 95% identity threshold, which selects for relatively recent, high-identity homology rather than for chromosomal co-location. The MashMap/wfmash design [@Jain2018] is what makes this sparsely evaluated comparison efficient enough to run at pangenome scale over 88 GB of PAF output.

**Transitive closure as the canonical query operation.** The IMPG `query -x` flag implements transitive closure: a depth-first traversal of the alignment graph in which each collected interval becomes the seed of a new query, recursively expanding the result set until no new intervals are discovered. This operation converts the sparse but densely-connected implicit graph into an operationally useful concept: the set of all loci homologous (at ≥95% identity, directly or indirectly) to any query region. For subtelomere analysis, transitive closure is the correct operation because interchromosomal PHR communities have complex multi-hop connectivity: arm A may be directly aligned to arm B and arm C, while arm B is directly aligned to arm D; a transitive query from A recovers A, B, C, and D as members of the same community, even if A-D direct alignments are absent from the PAF. The odgi similarity matrix [@GuarracinoHeumos2022] downstream of pggb graph construction captures a related but distinct relationship — Jaccard similarity over shared graph traversal — which is continuous rather than binary and is used for community detection, but the underlying connectivity structure is first established by IMPG transitive-closure projection during the PHR extraction step.

**What is contested.** The 12% figure itself is not yet documented in a citable publication — it is derived from wfmash's k-mer prefiltering behavior over the on-disk PAF set, and the Erdős-Rényi connectivity argument connecting it to reachability has been provided by the lead author but is not yet in print. This is a methods gap that the companion paper must fill. More broadly, the implicit-vs-explicit tradeoff involves genuine trade-offs: implicit graphs cannot be directly visualized with standard pangenome graph viewers, do not carry path-level annotation, and cannot be indexed for read alignment. The current study's analytical pipeline (wfmash → IMPG → odgi similarity → Leiden community detection) therefore uses the implicit graph for the discovery phase and materializes partial explicit graphs (via pggb on the detected PHR sequences) for the downstream similarity computation step. Understanding this two-phase architecture — implicit graph for all-to-all range queries, explicit graph for local similarity matrix computation — is essential for methods reproducibility.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

Pangenome graph methods are the methodological spine that makes every other topic in this review experimentally tractable. Three cross-topic intersections stand out.

**Topic 3 (pseudohomologous regions concept) and the transition from cytogenetics to graphs.** The PHR concept [@MeffordTrask2002] was defined operationally in terms of FISH hybridization patterns and cosmid clone sharing before sequence-level analysis was possible at scale. The crucial methodological transition — from clone-based patchwork models to graph-based community models — required both the T2T-quality assemblies reviewed in Topic 3 and the pangenome graph tools reviewed here. The current study's implicit pangenome graph over 18,827 telomere-anchored flanks is, in a precise sense, the sequence-level realization of what Mefford and Trask described qualitatively as "an exchange-prone patchwork of duplicated blocks." The graph methodology reviewed here is what converts the old cytogenetic concept into a population-scale measurable object.

**Topic 5 (acrocentric rDNA and Robertsonian translocations) and the Guarracino 2023 acrocentric study.** Guarracino et al. (2023) demonstrated, using the same wfmash all-to-all / odgi similarity / Leiden community detection pipeline as the current study, that the short arms of all five human acrocentric chromosomes form a single pangenome community [@Guarracino2023]. That work was a direct methodological precedent for applying the same pipeline to all 48 chromosome arms' subtelomeric flanks. The implicit-graph framework generalized naturally from acrocentric short arms to all subtelomeres; the only change was the sequence set being compared.

**Topic 7 (concerted evolution and NAHR) and the detection of ongoing exchange.** Concerted evolution, as reviewed in Topic 7, is classically inferred from sequence homogeneity patterns that differ from neutral divergence expectations. The pangenome graph approach converts "homogeneity pattern" into "graph community membership": sequences that belong to the same Leiden community at 95% identity are, by construction, the set of loci that have been subject to recent gene-conversion-like exchange. This converts concerted evolution from a retrospective population-genetic inference into a directly observable graph-structural property. The pedigree-observed recombination patches (Topic 11) then provide direct empirical confirmation that the community boundaries detected by the pangenome graph reflect ongoing exchange, not merely ancestral sequence composition.

## Open questions and recent developments

**Coordinate system and back-projection.** The implicit-graph approach sacrifices reference coordinates: IMPG's output is a BED/BEDPE file of sequence-relative intervals, not GRCh38 coordinates. Downstream analysis must either work entirely in haplotype-relative coordinates (as the current study does) or implement a liftover from sequence-relative to reference coordinates. The minigraph rGFA model [@Li2020] and the GBZ format [@Siren2022] offer data structures for maintaining reference-relative coordinates through graph operations, but both assume a reference-seeded construction that the implicit approach explicitly avoids. This is a genuine methodological gap: the current study's community calls cannot be directly overlaid on a GRCh38 annotation track without additional liftover steps.

**Scalability of transitive closure.** IMPG's transitive closure is efficient for the current dataset (18,827 sequences, ~88 GB PAF) but the depth of the closure — how many recursive rounds of interval expansion are needed before convergence — can grow with dataset size and identity threshold. For lower-identity queries (say, 80%) the graph becomes denser and transitive closure can merge distant chromosomal arms into a single giant component, masking biologically meaningful community structure. The 95% identity threshold used in the current study was chosen partly to avoid this "giant component" collapse, but the sensitivity of community structure to identity threshold and the maximum practical dataset size for IMPG-based analysis are not yet well characterized in the literature.

**The HPRC v2 and population-scale implicit graphs.** The HPRC v1 draft pangenome covered 47 individuals; HPRC v2 covers 232. The current subtelomere study uses the HPRC v2 assembly set and is therefore among the first publications to demonstrate the pangenome graph methods stack (wfmash, IMPG, pggb, odgi) at 465-assembly scale. The pggb paper [@Garrison2024pggb] demonstrated scalability to ~90 haplotypes in the MHC and to the Drosophila pangenome, but the computational requirements for 465-assembly human subtelomere analysis at chromosomal scale were not anticipated in that paper. The current study's computational details (cluster hours, memory per node, PAF file size) will be valuable benchmarks for the community.

**Distinguishing alignment-as-graph from graph-as-alignment.** The implicit-graph framing blurs a conceptual distinction that the earlier explicit-graph literature kept sharp. In vg and pggb's explicit GFA, the graph is a first-class data object: paths, edges, and nodes have stable identifiers, and downstream tools can traverse the graph structure directly. In IMPG's implicit graph, the graph only exists as a query interface over the PAF: there are no stable node identifiers, no traversable adjacency structure, and no path encoding. For range-projection queries — the specific operation needed for subtelomere PHR extraction — the implicit approach is faster and more memory-efficient. For operations that require traversing the graph topology (reading variant calls, visualizing graph structure, aligning reads to the pangenome), the explicit GFA is necessary. Future work may develop a hybrid data structure — perhaps the PAF as the implicit backbone with on-demand materialization of explicit subgraph neighborhoods — that captures the advantages of both. The interaction between IMPG and tools like gfapack, BandageNG [@Wick2015], and Bandage, which visualize GFA-format explicit graphs, highlights this unresolved gap.

**Alignment-free pangenome methods.** A parallel methodological thread is pursuing pangenome graph construction without pairwise alignment altogether. AlfaPang (2024) uses k-mer-based graph construction at a fraction of the computational cost of wfmash-based all-to-all alignment. For the subtelomere problem — where sequences share high identity over tens to hundreds of kilobases — alignment-based methods outperform k-mer methods at detecting long blocks of homology, but alignment-free approaches may become relevant as dataset sizes grow. The relationship between implicit alignment graphs (IMPG) and alignment-free pangenome constructions is an open area of methodological comparison.

**IMPG in broader population genetics contexts.** The original motivation for IMPG was to extract homologous loci from a large, pre-computed all-vs-all alignment without re-running the full alignment — a common task when building pangenome graphs iteratively or when subsetting a reference pangenome to a specific genomic region of interest. Beyond subtelomere analysis, IMPG has been applied to acrocentric short arms [@Guarracino2023], to extracting region-specific sub-pangenomes for repeat-rich loci like D4Z4, and to iterative refinement of PHR boundaries. The tool's combination of cache-oblivious interval trees with compact CIGAR storage makes it broadly applicable wherever an all-vs-all PAF exists and a range-projection query is needed, positioning it as infrastructure for a class of pangenome analyses that were previously impractical.
