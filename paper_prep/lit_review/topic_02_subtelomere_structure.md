# Topic 02: Subtelomere structural biology — TAR1, (TTAGGG)n ITS, and the duplication-rich boundary zone

## Claim map

This review focuses on the structural biology of human subtelomeres as a sequence and repeat landscape: the TAR1 satellite family, internal telomeric sequences (ITS), the telomere/subtelomere boundary organization, and the broader duplication-rich architecture of the most distal chromosome-end regions. It directly anchors **C4** (extended interchromosomal homology at nearly all subtelomeres, comparable in scale to PAR2) and **C2** (the implicit pangenome graph: reference-free, all-to-all, no chromosomal partitioning). The structural biology literature explains *why* a reference-free approach was necessary: the canonical structural features of subtelomeres — TAR1, ITS, subtelomeric repeat elements (SREs) — are present at virtually every chromosome end but in polymorphic, multiply-derived arrangements that no single reference haplotype can adequately represent. Awareness of this structural landscape is also prerequisite to understanding the community results of C5 and the 3D proximity evidence of C7.

---

## Era overview

The subtelomere emerged as a distinct structural entity during the early 1990s, when molecular biologists first attempted to clone and sequence the regions immediately adjacent to the then-newly-discovered mammalian telomeric repeat (TTAGGG)n. The telomere itself had been characterized in 1988 by Moyzis and colleagues, who established that human chromosome ends are capped by tandem arrays of (TTAGGG)n. Within two years, it became apparent that the DNA immediately internal to those arrays had its own unusual organization: repeat-rich, cross-chromosomal, and deeply polymorphic. 

The pivotal early observation was published by Brown and colleagues in 1990. Working with clones derived from four different human telomere regions, they identified two families of repeated sequences that were present at multiple—but not all—chromosome ends and showed polymorphic distributions among individuals. The most prominent family was designated TAR1 (Telomere-Associated Repeat 1), a variably-sized (0–2 kb) sequence immediately adjacent to the (TTAGGG)n arrays, detectable by Southern hybridization as a polymorphic set of bands varying across chromosomes and individuals. TAR1 was the first indication that human chromosome ends, while capped by the same hexameric repeat, are not structurally interchangeable once one moves a few kilobases inward. The paper established the subtelomere as a region of shared but polymorphic repeated DNA and identified the chromosome-end patchwork phenomenon that would preoccupy the field for the next three decades.

Concurrently, Wilkie and colleagues identified a stable length polymorphism of up to 260 kb at the tip of chromosome 16p, arising through occasional exchanges between nonhomologous subtelomeric regions \cite{subtelstruct_Wilkie1991}. This was among the first direct evidence that interchromosomal exchange actively shapes subtelomeric architecture in living human populations. Hastie and Allshire in 1989 had also noted that (TTAGGG)n sequences occur at intrachromosomal positions throughout the genome, not just at chromosome ends \cite{subtelstruct_HastieAllshire1989}—a finding that would later be systematized under the classification of interstitial telomeric sequences (ITS) and incorporated into explanations of subtelomere domain organization.

These early papers defined the central questions that drove subtelomere structural biology through the 1990s and 2000s: (1) What is the full sequence content of the distal 50–500 kb of each human chromosome arm? (2) Why are TAR1 and ITS present at nearly every chromosome end? (3) What is the relationship between TAR1, ITS, duplicated blocks, and the single-copy DNA proximal to them? These questions proved difficult to answer from standard reference assemblies, setting the stage for the pangenome-era approaches that C2 and C4 represent.

## Key papers in chronological order

**Brown et al. 1990** described TAR1, establishing it as the archetypal subtelomere-adjacent repeat. The original clones covered four chromosome ends and identified two repeat families, one of which (TAR1) was the focus of subsequent structural studies. Their Southern blot data showed that TAR1 hybridizes to a polymorphic ladder of bands across individuals, consistent with copy-number and positional variation \cite{subtelstruct_Brown1990}. The TAR1 canonical sequence is approximately 1.6 kb, and parts of it are present within 2 kb of nearly all sequenced (TTAGGG)n-adjacent DNA sequences—a statement confirmed two decades later at population scale by the HPRC v2 analysis (94.6% TAR1 prevalence across 18,827 flanks; 0.5% at PAR1 which uses obligate meiotic crossover rather than repeat-mediated exchange).

**Wilkie et al. 1991** used restriction mapping of chromosome 16p to establish that allelic differences in subtelomeric length can span hundreds of kilobases, and that this variation most plausibly reflects periodic interchromosomal exchange at chromosome ends \cite{subtelstruct_Wilkie1991}. This paper primed the field to think about subtelomeric length polymorphism as a population-genetic signal rather than merely a technical complication.

**Flint et al. 1997** introduced the two-domain architectural model: a distal subdomain (the most terminal 10–50 kb) shared among many chromosome ends at high identity, and a more proximal subdomain shared among fewer ends, often separated by degenerate internal telomere repeats \cite{Flint1997}. This model directly predicts the ITS distribution observed by Riethman (2004) and confirmed at pangenome scale in the current analysis (ITS co-localize with domain breakpoints on 11/19 arms; two-domain structure present on 39/41 testable arms).

**Riethman et al. 2004** constituted the first systematic sequence-level survey of all 41 genetically distinct human telomere regions \cite{Riethman2004}. They assembled subtelomeric sequences for each arm using available draft and finished sequence, and made several quantitative observations that anchor C4: (i) subtelomeric repeats comprise ~25% of the most distal 500 kb and ~80% of the most distal 100 kb; (ii) the assemblies are enriched >25-fold in (TTAGGG)n-like sequences (ITS) relative to the rest of the genome, with 114 islands identified; (iii) TAR1 is present near all sequenced telomere-adjacent regions. This paper provided the first genome-wide estimate of the scale of the duplicated zone, establishing the ~80 kb as the figure often cited for the "most distal heavily-duplicated region" of human chromosome arms.

**Nergadze et al. 2007** synthesized what was known about interstitial telomeric sequences, classifying them into short ITS, subtelomeric ITS, fusion ITS (chromosome 2q13 remnant), and heterochromatic ITS \cite{subtelstruct_Nergadze2007}. Their review established that ITS are distinct from TAR1—they represent tandem arrays of (TTAGGG)n that are degenerate (variant motifs dominate over canonical sequence) and that were presumably incorporated during ancient recombination events. The finding that ITS often mark duplicon-to-duplicon junctions within subtelomeres was later extended by Ambrosini et al. and is consistent with the domain boundary co-localization reported in the current analysis.

**Ambrosini et al. 2007** constructed the first comprehensive duplicon-block catalogue for human subtelomeres, identifying 11 block families (the subterminal families A–F plus specialized blocks) and showing that the bimodal identity distribution of subtelomeric paralogous blocks (peaks at ~91% and ~98%) reflects at least two evolutionary time periods of duplication events \cite{Ambrosini2007}. The 98% peak likely reflects recent exchange or gene conversion. This paper provides direct support for C4 in that it quantifies the scale and prevalence of the shared duplicated zone, and it predates C4's pangenome-scale confirmation by 15 years.

**Riethman 2008** extended the copy-number analysis to document massive allelic disparities in subtelomeric structure, particularly in the most distal 100 kb \cite{subtelstruct_Riethman2008}. Large variant alleles (>300 kb) were found at many chromosome ends, consistent entirely with subtelomeric repeat sequences. This paper reinforced that no single reference haplotype could adequately represent the full allelic spectrum of human subtelomeres—precisely the motivation for the reference-free, all-to-all approach in C2.

**Stong et al. 2014** improved subtelomeric sequence assemblies by filling telomere-adjacent gaps using clone-based resources, and then used the improved assemblies to map CTCF and cohesin binding sites at subtelomeres \cite{Stong2014}. They found CTCF/cohesin co-occupancy within 3 kb of the start of terminal repeat tracts at many subtelomeres, as well as enrichment near ITS islands and the boundaries of subtelomeric repeat elements (SREs). This paper is pivotal because it connected subtelomeric repeat organization (TAR1, ITS, SRE boundaries) to a chromatin architecture regulated by sequence-specific binding proteins—providing the molecular bridge between the repeat landscape and three-dimensional genome organization that C7 later tests at population scale.

**Young et al. 2020** used BioNano optical mapping of 154 human genomes from 26 populations to provide the first large-scale catalogue of subtelomeric haplotype diversity \cite{subtelstruct_Young2020}. They resolved many previously ambiguous subtelomeric structures in the GRCh38 reference, catalogued novel long-range haplotypes, and determined duplicon-block frequencies on each chromosome arm across populations. Their data confirmed Riethman's allelic disparity observations at population scale and showed that subtelomeric structural variation is pervasive, population-differentiated, and not fully captured by any single reference genome.

**Logsdon et al. 2021** and **Nurk et al. 2022** completed the first gapless telomere-to-telomere assemblies of a human chromosome and then a full human genome (CHM13), respectively \cite{subtelstruct_Logsdon2021, subtelstruct_Nurk2022}. These assemblies, by closing all previous subtelomeric gaps, made it possible for the first time to characterize the full sequence from (TTAGGG)n arrays through TAR1 through the duplicated zone and into unique flanking sequence, without interruption by reference gaps. The T2T assemblies confirmed that the most distal subtelomeric regions are among the most repeat-dense in the genome, validating decades of inference from incomplete assemblies.

**Gershman et al. 2022** analyzed the complete T2T-CHM13 genome for repeat content, transcription, and CpG methylation \cite{Gershman2022}. For subtelomeres, they confirmed that the pericentromeric and subtelomeric regions are dominated by satellite and segmental duplication sequences. They also characterized the TERRA promoter elements (29-bp CpG-rich repeats) at roughly half of subtelomeres. This analysis provided the first complete repeat annotation of human subtelomeres in a gapless reference, confirming that the repeat landscape is even more complex than the pre-gap-filling assemblies suggested.

**Sholes et al. 2022** used single-molecule chromatin fiber sequencing (Fiber-seq) to show that telomere fibers contain three consecutive chromatin domains: the telomeric cap, a highly accessible TAR1/CpG island boundary element, and subtelomeric heterochromatin \cite{subtelstruct_Sholes2022}. CTCF occupancy precisely demarcates the accessible boundary from the adjacent compacted subtelomeric region. Thirty-nine of 46 telomeres in CHM13 harbored TAR1-adjacent CTCF-CpG elements in a stereotyped pattern. This is the most mechanistically detailed characterization of the TAR1 chromatin architecture and directly confirms Stong et al.'s earlier ChIP-seq inference from incomplete assemblies.

## Findings most relevant to claims C2 and C4 of the abstract

### C4: Extended interchromosomal homology at nearly all subtelomeres, comparable to PAR2

The structural biology of TAR1 and the duplicated SRE zone directly explains why C4 is true. The phenomenon that the current HPRC v2 analysis quantifies—18,827 subtelomeric flanks of which 83.2% (15,668) have detectable inter-chromosomal matches, with median PHR region length 105 kb—has its mechanistic basis in the repeat architecture first described by Brown (1990) and Riethman (2004). TAR1 is present at ~95% of all subtelomeres; the duplicated SRE zone (dominated by the same shared block families described by Ambrosini 2007) spans tens to hundreds of kilobases at nearly every arm.

The PAR2 comparison in the abstract (C4: "comparable in scale to the canonical PAR2") gains its framing from this literature. PAR2 on the Xq/Yq arms spans roughly 330 kb of high-identity sequence shared obligately between X and Y. Riethman (2004) established that the distal ~80–100 kb of most chromosome ends is similarly dominated by shared repetitive sequence (SREs comprising ~80% of the most distal 100 kb). The current analysis extends this to show that the median PHR extends to 105 kb, with many arms having PHRs of 200–500 kb—within the same order of magnitude as PAR2, and extending across nearly all 41 arms rather than just one arm pair. The structural biology reviewed here provides the mechanistic underpinning for why this is so: TAR1, (TTAGGG)n ITS, and the duplicated block families each contribute to the signal detected by all-to-all pairwise alignment at ≥95% identity.

The 18,352 ITS islands across 8,321 sequences (53.1%) in the HPRC v2 analysis, with median length 79 bp, represent the population-scale quantification of what Riethman (2004) and Nergadze (2007) first catalogued from reference assemblies. Critically, 47.2% of these islands are variant-dominant (containing <50% canonical TTAGGG hexamer), with TGAGGG, TTGGGG, and TCAGGG accounting for the majority—matching the variant motif distribution first characterized in the reference genome by Ambrosini et al. (2007). This population-scale confirmation of the structural observation is a direct test of the decades-old claim that these islands are ancient remnants, not active telomeres.

A contested aspect of C4 is whether the ITS co-localize with domain boundaries as Flint's model predicts. The current analysis finds co-localization within 25 kb on only 11/19 arms with detectable ITS, while Ambrosini et al. claimed that ITS "almost always co-localize to duplicon boundaries." This discrepancy may reflect that Ambrosini tested the reference genome's internal duplicon junctions, whereas the current analysis tested the PHR outer boundary—a different structural feature. The claim that ITS mark the distal/proximal domain boundary therefore remains supported but not comprehensively tested.

### C2: Reference-free, all-to-all, no chromosomal partitioning

The case for C2's reference-free approach rests directly on the structural biology literature. The defining property of TAR1, ITS, and SRE blocks is that their copy number, location, and allelic state are all highly variable across individuals and chromosome arms. Riethman (2008) documented massive allelic disparities. Young et al. (2020) showed that many ambiguous subtelomeric structures in GRCh38 are actually resolved as distinct haplotypes in an optical mapping population. Grigorev et al. (2021) showed that even the terminal (TTAGGG)n arrays carry extensive haplotype diversity in their repeat-motif composition \cite{subtelstruct_Grigorev2021}. Each of these papers makes the same underlying point: a single reference haplotype cannot represent the allelic and paralogous variation space of human subtelomeres. The implicit pangenome graph approach (C2)—which aligns all 18,827 flanks against each other without choosing a reference and without partitioning by chromosome—is the technical response demanded by this structural reality. Reference-based analyses of subtelomeres systematically undercount shared sequence because reference-mapping tools suppress multi-mappers, and they misclassify allelic variation as inter-chromosomal exchange (or vice versa) because the reference haplotype's repeat arrangement may not match the query haplotype's.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

**Topic 03 (pseudohomologous regions concept)** is the immediate neighbor in the conceptual hierarchy: the PHR concept (Mefford & Trask 2002, Linardopoulou 2005) was built on the structural foundation described here. TAR1 and ITS are structural features of PHRs, not simply separate entities. The Flint (1997) two-domain model, which is the architectural predecessor of the current analysis's PHR definition, was inferred partly from the observation that ITS islands lie between structurally distinct subdomains. Topic 03's central claim—that PHRs are dynamic exchange substrates rather than fixed sequence features—depends on Topic 02's structural insight that TAR1 and SREs form a shared repeat landscape which mediates recognition between non-homologous chromosome ends.

**Topic 07 (concerted evolution / NAHR)** depends on this topic for the mechanistic substrate. Nonallelic homologous recombination between chromosome ends requires the shared high-identity sequences documented in this literature. The ~98% identity peak in Ambrosini's bimodal distribution is the sequence substrate that permits NAHR; the TAR1 element at the telomere/subtelomere boundary provides the most consistently shared anchor. Topic 07's population-genetics and recombination-rate framing cannot be interpreted without knowing what the shared sequence looks like structurally.

**Topic 08 (meiotic bouquet / nuclear envelope)** connects to this topic through the observation that TAR1 near-absence at PAR1 (0.5% in the HPRC v2 analysis) is mechanistically informative. PAR1 uses an obligate meiotic crossover mechanism that differs from the repeat-mediated exchange that characterizes most subtelomeres. The meiotic bouquet brings all chromosome ends into proximity, but the structural features reviewed here—particularly the shared TAR1 and SRE landscape—are what allow productive ectopic exchange when non-homologous chromosome ends are co-localized at the nuclear envelope. Topic 08's physical proximity hypothesis (C7) thus requires Topic 02's structural substrate to produce the observed exchange outcomes.

**Topic 05 (acrocentric rDNA and Robertsonian)** is relevant because acrocentric p-arms have unusually high cross-arm affinity rates (up to 100% in some arms) which the current analysis explains partly through the complete homogenization of shared SRE blocks among acrocentric short arms. The acrocentric subtelomere-adjacent repeat landscape is structurally distinct from other arms in ways documented by Riethman (2004) and Stong (2014) and confirmed at population scale in the HPRC v2 analysis.

## Open questions and recent developments

**The T2T era has resolved the sequence but not the dynamics.** The Nurk et al. (2022) T2T-CHM13 assembly and the subsequent HPRC pangenome (Liao et al. 2023) \cite{subtelstruct_Liao2023} have for the first time provided gapless subtelomeric sequences for a complete genome and for 233 diverse individuals respectively. Yet the question of what TAR1 actually *does* remains open. The HPRC v2 analysis shows TAR1 to be a "passenger" rather than a facilitator of exchange (cross-arm rates are indistinguishable between TAR1+ and TAR1- sequences in the only community where both types are available—acrocentric p-arms). This is statistically inconclusive because TAR1 is nearly universal (99.6% of non-PAR sequences), leaving no adequate control population. Whether TAR1's CTCF-CpG island chromatin boundary function (Sholes et al. 2022, Stong et al. 2014) actively promotes or suppresses exchange at the boundary it demarcates remains untested in a genome-wide way.

**ITS function at domain boundaries.** Ambrosini et al. (2007) claimed that ITS almost always co-localize with internal duplicon boundaries, suggesting a role in generating or stabilizing complex repeat organization. Nergadze et al. (2007) reviewed evidence that ITS are hotspots for chromosomal instability in yeast and some human disease contexts. The HPRC v2 analysis confirms the population-scale presence and variant composition of ITS, but cannot directly test whether they facilitate or merely accompany domain boundary formation. Recent work in model systems (PNAS 2024) shows that ITS promote gross chromosomal rearrangement via multiple mechanisms, strengthening the case for functional relevance. Whether this applies to the subtelomeric ITS in human germline or somatic cells is an open question.

**TAR1 and telomere length homeostasis.** The Sholes et al. (2022) single-molecule data showed that telomeres adjacent to TAR1 elements are significantly longer and more variable in length than those without TAR1. This suggests TAR1 participates in telomerase access or processivity regulation, possibly through the CTCF-CpG island boundary. This is a mechanistic hypothesis without a direct test. Grigorev et al.'s (2021) finding of extensive haplotype diversity in telomere repeat motif composition—with higher paternal inheritance of telomeric variants—points to a dynamic system in which structural features of the subtelomere influence the composition and length of the terminal array itself.

**Reference-free population-scale structural annotation.** Young et al.'s (2020) optical mapping study was the first to catalogue subtelomeric haplotypes at population scale (154 genomes, 26 populations), but was limited to ~500 kb resolution. The HPRC v2 pangenome enables base-pair-resolution population-scale annotation of TAR1, ITS, and SRE organization across 466 haplotypes—a qualitative advance. The open question is whether specific TAR1/ITS structural configurations are population-stratified, as Mefford & Trask (2002) speculated, or whether structure at this level is primarily determined by chromosome arm identity rather than ancestry. The HPRC v2 analysis finds population-differentiated cross-arm affinity (Hudson Fst 0.10–0.15 for AFR vs. non-AFR at specific arm pairs), suggesting that structural state is ancestry-informative at the community level. Whether this signal propagates to TAR1 copy number or ITS variant composition at individual arms remains to be tested.

**FSHD and subtelomeric disease as a window.** The D4Z4 macrosatellite at 4q35 (contraction of which causes FSHD via permissive DUX4 expression) represents the best-characterized disease involving subtelomeric repeat structure \cite{Lemmers2010}. The HPRC v2 analysis confirms that chr4_q and chr10_q belong to the same Leiden community (C1) with 43.4% structural discordance, exceeding the ~20% estimated by Southern blot. The pathogenic potential of D4Z4 contraction depends critically on the 4qA/4qB permissive background, which is itself defined by a subtelomeric sequence variant distal to the last D4Z4 repeat. This makes the 4q/10q subtelomere one of the most clinically relevant examples of the general principle that subtelomeric structural state (repeat number, block orientation, SRE boundary position) has functional consequences. T2T-era work is now resolving D4Z4 arrays at full repeat resolution for the first time \cite{subtelstruct_Nurk2022}, and recent analyses flag pitfalls in diagnosing FSHD from short-read data due to complex D4Z4 repeat landscape in the T2T era.

The major unsettled question across all these threads is the causal architecture of the subtelomeric repeat landscape. The structural biology reviewed here provides an increasingly detailed inventory—TAR1 location and chromatin state, ITS content and variant composition, SRE block organization, copy number variation—but converting that inventory into mechanistic claims about which structural features drive exchange, protect ends from inappropriate recombination, or regulate telomere function requires either experimental perturbation or population-genetic signatures that distinguish active mechanism from historical record. The HPRC v2 pangenome provides the population-genetic substrate for the latter approach; the structural biology reviewed here defines what features to look at.
