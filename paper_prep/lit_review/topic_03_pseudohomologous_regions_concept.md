# Topic 03: The pseudohomologous region concept, 1997-present

## Claim map

This review anchors the pseudohomologous region (PHR) concept to the current abstract claims as follows. The literature directly supports **C4** (extended interchromosomal homology at nearly all subtelomeres), **C5** (named communities/clades such as PARs, acrocentric short arms, 10p-18p, 4q-10q), and **C8** (ongoing exchange and concerted evolution). It supports **C2-C3** only indirectly, by explaining why a pangenome-scale, reference-free analysis was needed. It supports **C6-C7** mostly as a rationale rather than as direct evidence: older cytogenetic and clone-era work predicted population structure and nuclear-proximity effects, but did not measure them at HPRC scale.

## Chronological narrative

The PHR concept is best understood as a name given to a problem that predated the name. By the early 1990s, telomere-associated clones had already shown that human chromosome ends were not simple single-copy transitions into terminal (TTAGGG)n repeats. Brown and colleagues described polymorphic telomere-associated DNA, including chromosome-end sequences that were shared or variably arranged across individuals, while Wilkie and colleagues mapped a stable length polymorphism of up to 260 kb at 16p and argued that it probably arose through occasional exchange between nonhomologous subtelomeric regions [@Brown1990; @Wilkie1991]. These papers do not yet support the abstract's C4 in a genome-wide sense, and they do not establish the PHR term. Their importance is conceptual: they made chromosome ends into polymorphic exchange substrates rather than fixed reference termini.

Flint et al. then supplied the architectural predecessor of the PHR model [@Flint1997]. By comparing sequenced ends from human chromosomes 4p, 16p and 22q, and by placing these observations beside yeast subtelomeres, they proposed a two-domain organization: a very distal domain shared among many chromosome ends, and a more proximal domain shared with fewer ends, often separated by degenerate internal telomere-repeat tracts. This is one of the clearest predecessors of C4, because it explicitly ties distance from the telomere to breadth of interchromosomal sharing. It is also the key predecessor for the current project's "two-domain Flint/Mefford model" result: the HPRC v2 analysis reports that sharing decreases with distance from the telomere on 39/48 arms, with two-segment breakpoints on 39/41 testable arms and internal telomeric sequence co-localization on a subset of arms. That is not merely citation support; it is a direct pangenome-scale retest of the model.

The late-1990s Trask-lab FISH and cosmid work transformed the two-domain model into a chromosome-end patchwork model. Trask et al. showed that olfactory-receptor gene family members occur in large blocks duplicated polymorphically near chromosome ends [@Trask1998]. In the present manuscript this history matters for C5, because the f7501/olfactory-receptor block is still a recognizable community signal rather than a historical curiosity: the current analysis reproduces f7501 distribution at population scale and detects population-enriched arm placements. The caveat is important for Q&A: Trask et al. support the existence of subtelomeric block polymorphism and interchromosomal sharing, but not the current abstract's population-scale community calls or the 466-haplotype data frame.

Mefford and Trask's 2002 review is the foundational synthesis for the PHR concept [@MeffordTrask2002]. It gave the field a compact language for subtelomeres as dynamic, variable transitions between chromosome-specific DNA and terminal telomere repeats. It emphasized patchworks of duplicated blocks, recurrent exchange, copy-number and location variation, and the possibility that block frequencies might carry population-history signal while also warning that exchange history could make them unreliable phylogenetic markers. Der-Sarkissian et al. made this warning concrete by showing that proterminal f7501 and DNF92 markers vary across a subset of normal chromosome ends and that marker coexistence is itself structured [@DerSarkissian2002]. For the current manuscript, Mefford and Trask are direct support for C4 and C8, and partial support for C5; Der-Sarkissian is supporting evidence that location frequencies are population variables, not fixed annotations. Together they explain why C2 is methodologically necessary: a reference-only analysis cannot resolve a class of regions whose defining property is that reference location, copy number and allelic state are all unstable.

In parallel, the broader segmental-duplication field made subtelomeres part of a genome-wide duplication story. Bailey et al. framed recent segmental duplications as a major force in human genome evolution [@Bailey2002]. Fan et al. and Martin et al. showed that the ancestral chromosome 2 fusion region and its paralogous subtelomeric regions carry complex histories of duplication, telomere-origin sequence, and interstitial capture [@Fan2002; @Martin2002]. These studies refine the PHR concept by challenging any simple tree of subtelomeric block history. A chromosome end can donate sequence to another end, become interstitial through fusion, or retain signatures of old telomeric context after the original event is obscured. This is background support for C8, not direct support for the abstract's new communities.

Riethman's 2004 assembly program moved the field from probe-based inference toward sequence-level subtelomere atlases [@Riethman2004]. The key contribution was practical and conceptual: assemble the 41 genetically distinct human telomere-adjacent regions well enough to compare their duplicated blocks. This work underlies C4 because it showed that the most distal subtelomeric intervals are enriched for shared duplicated sequence, but it also exposed a limitation. The available assemblies represented particular reference haplotypes and were incomplete or ambiguous for many termini. This is one reason the current manuscript's HPRC v2 framing matters: the PHR concept was never just "the sequence next to the telomere in the reference"; it was always an allelic and paralogous population object that reference assemblies undersampled.

Linardopoulou et al. sharpened the concept by explicitly calling human subtelomeres hot spots of interchromosomal recombination and segmental duplication [@Linardopoulou2005]. They mapped paralogy blocks across subtelomeres, inferred repeated transfers among chromosome ends, identified mechanistic signatures at breakpoints, and highlighted recurrent pairs including 10p-18p. This is direct historical support for C5's 10p-18p clade and strong support for C8's "ongoing exchange" frame, with one qualification: Linardopoulou et al. primarily inferred historical transfers and mechanisms from sequence architecture, whereas the current abstract adds direct pedigree-observed patches. Their work supports the claim that the architecture is exchange-derived; it does not by itself prove the current generation-to-generation recombination signal.

Ambrosini et al. then recast the patchwork as a duplicon-block catalogue [@Ambrosini2007]. Their block families, subterminal families and one-copy telomere-adjacent regions provide a useful vocabulary for asking which chromosome ends are shared, private or intermediate. They also reported a bimodal sequence-identity distribution, with a high-identity peak plausibly reflecting recent exchange or ongoing gene conversion. Riethman's copy-number review sharpened the same point from the allelic side: the most distal 500 kb and especially the most distal 100 kb are dominated by subtelomeric repeats, with major consequences for transcript dosage, terminal chromatin and telomere-near function [@Riethman2008]. The present manuscript's 95% identity threshold deliberately captures Ambrosini's recent-exchange component, so Ambrosini is direct support for method choice and C4 scale; Riethman is support for treating PHR variation as copy-number and allele-organization biology rather than just alignment nuisance. The current project also refines Ambrosini: four of six one-copy regions are confirmed, but chr7q and chr12q form a private pair detectable only at population scale, and Ambrosini's gene-content blocks map systematically onto Leiden communities. This is a good example of "supports but is superseded": the old catalogue is right in broad architecture, but single-reference one-copy labels need population-scale correction.

Rudd et al. contributed two refinements. Their sister-chromatid exchange work showed that chromosome ends are unusually recombinogenic even within mitotic cells [@Rudd2007]. This supports the plausibility of C8 but should not be oversold as evidence for interchromosomal PHR exchange; sister-chromatid exchange is a different event class. Their primate chromosome-fission analysis later showed that once a locus is placed near a chromosome end it can undergo extensive lineage-specific structural change, including nonhomologous end joining and retrotransposition [@Rudd2009]. This challenges a too-narrow PHR model in which all similarity is maintained only by homologous recombination. The better claim is that PHRs are exchange-rich outcomes shaped by several repair and rearrangement mechanisms, with the current manuscript adding population-scale and pedigree evidence for ongoing exchange among sequence-similarity communities.

Stong et al. represent the assembly-based inheritance of the PHR concept [@Stong2014]. Improved subtelomere assemblies and annotation of CTCF/cohesin organization made it possible to treat subtelomeres as mappable regulatory and structural domains, not only as FISH signals or duplicon lists. This paper supports C2-C4 indirectly because it demonstrates the payoff of better assemblies, and it supports C7 as background by connecting subtelomeric sequence organization to chromatin architecture. It does not, however, establish the current Hi-C claim. The manuscript's C7 rests on bulk and single-cell 3D analyses plus exclusion controls; Stong is the bridge that made subtelomere assemblies compatible with such analyses.

The 2018-2022 long-molecule and telomere-sequencing era further refined the concept by separating telomere-repeat haplotypes from subtelomeric PHRs and by showing that even the physical end of a chromosome can be reference-misaligned. Shao et al. used BioNano and nanopore data to identify chromosome-end extension events, including inherited heterozygous extensions that could be modeled as chimeric chromosome ends [@Shao2018]. Optical mapping across 154 genomes then showed that large-scale subtelomeric structural variation is widespread and that many termini remain poorly represented by standard references [@Young2020]. Long-read telomere sequencing showed extensive telomeric repeat-motif heterogeneity across haplotypes and populations [@Grigorev2021]. Finally, NPGREAT used linked reads plus ultralong nanopore reads to improve CHM13 subtelomere assemblies across representative segmental-duplication structures [@Adam2022]. These papers are not direct support for PHR communities, because they focus on chromosome-end extension, structural maps, telomeric repeat haplotypes or local assembly rather than all-to-all subtelomeric homology. They are nevertheless important refinements: the PHR boundary must exclude the terminal telomere tract, and population-scale subtelomeric variation cannot be inferred reliably from short-read references alone.

T2T-CHM13 and HPRC changed the scale of the question. Logsdon et al.'s complete chromosome 8 and Nurk et al.'s T2T-CHM13 assembly closed many formerly inaccessible terminal and duplicated regions [@Logsdon2021; @Nurk2022]. The HPRC draft pangenome then demonstrated that diverse, haplotype-resolved assemblies could be organized as a population reference [@Liao2023]. These studies support C2-C3 as enabling literature rather than as PHR evidence. They make the current abstract credible: 466 near-complete haplotype assemblies and a reference-free all-to-all implicit pangenome graph are exactly the technical response demanded by three decades of subtelomere work. The PHR concept becomes operational rather than descriptive: a PHR is the telomere-adjacent interval in a haplotype that shares high-identity sequence with other chromosome ends, and communities are inferred from the resulting similarity graph rather than imposed from cytogenetic labels.

The current manuscript therefore inherits three versions of the PHR concept. The first is the Flint/Mefford architectural concept: distal multi-end sharing, proximal restricted sharing, and internal telomeric repeats as possible boundary marks. The second is the Linardopoulou/Ambrosini evolutionary concept: subtelomeres are patchworks of duplicated blocks moved among ends at high rate. The third is the Stong/T2T/HPRC operational concept: the regions can now be assembled, aligned, clustered and tested across many haplotypes. The novelty claimed in C4-C8 is strongest where all three versions converge: the old two-domain and block models are recovered, known clades are reproduced, novel clades appear, population structure becomes measurable, 3D contact tracks sequence communities, and pedigree patches fall overwhelmingly inside those communities.

The main challenge to the PHR concept is not that the old literature contradicts it. It is that "PHR" can be overextended. PAR1 and PAR2 are true pseudoautosomal regions with obligate sex-chromosome recombination; acrocentric short arms include rDNA-adjacent homogenization; D4Z4 at 4q-10q has disease-linked macrosatellite biology; and f7501/OR blocks reflect duplicon histories that can be older than the recent high-identity threshold. The current manuscript should therefore use PHR as an operational umbrella for telomere-adjacent interchromosomal homology, while being precise about mechanism. The literature supports saying that these regions have been shaped by recurrent exchange, duplication, gene conversion-like processes, nonhomologous repair and selection on gene content. The pedigree result is what upgrades that long-standing model from inferred history to observed ongoing exchange in the present dataset.

For the talk, the clean chronological line is: 1990-1997 discovered polymorphic chromosome ends and the two-domain model; 1998-2002 named the dynamic patchwork and introduced the PHR frame; 2004-2007 converted the patchwork into sequence blocks, hot spots and duplicon families; 2014-2021 improved assemblies and long-molecule views exposed reference limitations; 2022-present T2T/HPRC made the pangenome-scale test possible. The claim-anchored punchline is that C4-C5 are not surprising de novo observations: they are the genome-wide realization of a model built from a few chromosome ends. What is new is the scale, the reference-free graph implementation, the population and 3D layers, and the direct pedigree evidence that the old "pseudohomology" is not only fossil sequence history but an active substrate for subtelomeric concerted evolution.
