# Topic 13: Subtelomere Population Genetics and Fst Out-of-Africa Structure

## Claim map

This topic anchors **C6** (population-genetic analyses resolve subtelomeric clustering across human ancestries) in the current abstract. It provides both the theoretical framework for population differentiation statistics (Fst estimators, structured coalescent) and the empirical literature demonstrating that subtelomeric block-frequency variation tracks the out-of-Africa demographic history. It supports **C5** (named cladistic communities) indirectly by explaining why the most frequently exchanged arms show erased population signal while moderately exchanged arms retain it. It contextualises **C8** (ongoing recombination shapes subtelomeres) through the population-genetic lens: the observed AFR-deepest-split Fst topology reflects the cumulative balance between ancestral allele-frequency differences established before the out-of-Africa bottleneck and subsequent exchange that partially homogenises those frequencies. The topic is not the primary substrate for **C2–C4** (graph architecture, sample set, interchromosomal homology scale) but those claims required the population framework to be meaningful: a 465-haplotype all-to-all pangenome graph is the enabling resource for testing C6 at adequate power.

## Era overview

The intersection of subtelomere biology and formal population genetics developed slowly. For the first decade of serious subtelomere work—roughly 1986 to 1996—researchers focused on chromosome-end structure, telomere-length polymorphism, and the cloning of telomere-adjacent repeat families, without directly asking whether subtelomeric exchange frequencies varied in a systematic way across human populations \cite{Rouyer1986, Brown1990, Wilkie1991}. The conceptual machinery for such an analysis—unbiased Fst estimators, the coalescent under structured migration, and the serial-founder-effect model of human demographic history—was being developed in parallel by population geneticists studying allozymes and then DNA polymorphisms \cite{subtel_popgen_lewontin1972, subtel_popgen_weir1984, subtel_popgen_hudson1992}.

The pivotal moment came when Trask and colleagues used fluorescence in situ hybridization to show that specific subtelomeric cosmid probes—particularly the f7501 probe recognising block-2/block-3 junction sequence—were present on additional chromosome arms at elevated frequency in individuals of African origin compared to non-African individuals \cite{Trask1998}. This was the first empirical indication that subtelomeric haplotype frequencies might carry population-history signal analogous to the classical markers that had defined the out-of-Africa topology. Mefford and Trask's 2002 review crystallised the question: "measuring the frequency of specific subtelomeric blocks on particular chromosomes might inform phylogenetic studies of modern humans," while cautioning that recurrent exchange could also "be an unreliable indicator of the relationships of human populations" \cite{MeffordTrask2002}.

This cautionary duality set the research agenda for the next two decades. On one side, investigators documented more examples of population-enriched subtelomeric haplotypes. On the other, population geneticists formalised the conditions under which a biallelic structural marker subject to recurrent homogenisation can nonetheless carry ancestry signal: provided that the per-generation exchange rate is low relative to coalescence time in the ancestral population, the initial frequency difference established before the OOA bottleneck will persist, albeit attenuated, in descendant populations. The HPRC v2 analysis conducted by Andrea Guarracino and reported in chapters 04 and 12 of the end-to-end report provides the first adequately powered genome-wide test of this expectation, using Hudson Fst on cross-arm versus self-arm haplotype membership across nine arm/community pairs \cite{subtel_popgen_hudson1992}. The result—AFR deepest split with non-AFR populations nearly undifferentiated—is the direct empirical anchor for abstract claim C6.

## Key papers in chronological order

**Lewontin (1972)** established the benchmark that approximately 85 percent of classical human protein polymorphism occurs within rather than between populations, with only ~15 percent between geographic groups \cite{subtel_popgen_lewontin1972}. This apportionment provides the null expectation against which subtelomeric differentiation should be measured. Structural markers with Fst of 0.10–0.15 (as observed at AFR-enriched subtelomeric arms in the HPRC v2 dataset) are moderately but meaningfully differentiated by this standard.

**Weir and Cockerham (1984)** provided the most widely used unbiased Fst estimator, correcting for finite sample sizes and appropriate for binary allelic markers—precisely the kind of data produced by cross-arm versus self-arm calls at subtelomeric loci \cite{subtel_popgen_weir1984}. Their approach remains standard in population genetics pipelines and provides the basis for comparing subtelomeric differentiation with genome-wide averages.

**Hudson, Slatkin, and Maddison (1992)** introduced an alternative Fst estimator based on nucleotide diversity: Fst = (Dxy − πx − πy) / 2Dxy, where Dxy is mean between-population sequence dissimilarity and π is within-population heterozygosity \cite{subtel_popgen_hudson1992}. This estimator is sample-size-independent under the coalescent, making it appropriate when superpopulation sample sizes differ—as they do in the HPRC v2 cohort (37–93 individuals per superpopulation). Andrea's chapter 04 uses Hudson Fst specifically for this reason.

**Trask et al. (1998)** demonstrated that olfactory-receptor-gene-containing duplicon blocks vary polymorphically at chromosome ends and identified African-enriched signals at specific loci using FISH \cite{Trask1998}. The f7501 cosmid probe—corresponding to subtelomeric block-2/block-3 junction sequence in the Ambrosini nomenclature—was fixed on 3q, 15q, and 19p in all surveyed individuals but appeared on additional arms, including 16q and 7p, at higher frequency in individuals from African pygmy populations. This observation launched the hypothesis that subtelomeric haplotype frequencies reflect population history.

**Mefford and Trask (2002)** synthesised accumulated FISH, Southern blot, and early sequence data into the "complex patchwork" model of human subtelomeres and raised the population-phylogenetics question explicitly \cite{MeffordTrask2002}. They introduced the key empirical observation that cross-arm haplotype frequency at the chr4q/chr10q D4Z4 locus is approximately 20% by Southern blot, while noting qualitative enrichment of other block types in African versus non-African individuals. They argued that a large-scale survey across diverse populations using both the frequency of specific blocks on particular chromosomes and new molecular markers would be informative about human evolutionary history.

**Der-Sarkissian et al. (2002)** conducted one of the more rigorous early population surveys using cosmid probes including f7501 and DNF92 across a set of normal chromosomes, confirming structured variation at specific chromosome arms consistent with recent ectopic exchange rather than stable allele inheritance \cite{DerSarkissian2002}. This study confirmed the exchange-dominated model while leaving open whether genuine population differentiation persists beneath the exchange noise.

**Rosenberg et al. (2002)** used 377 microsatellite loci in 1,056 individuals from 52 populations to demonstrate five major continental clusters with the out-of-Africa topology—AFR at greatest distance from all other groups, followed by EAS branching before SAS/EUR/AMR clustering \cite{subtel_popgen_rosenberg2002}. This paper established the canonical phylogenetic benchmark: any new molecular marker carrying genuine ancestry signal should reproduce this topology, making it the standard against which subtelomeric Fst trees should be evaluated.

**Ambrosini et al. (2007)** catalogued the duplicon block landscape of human subtelomeres into 11 subtelomere-specific blocks and 6 subterminal families, identified the bimodal identity distribution with high-identity (98%) and lower-identity (91%) peaks, and provided the vocabulary for asking which blocks are population-enriched versus uniformly distributed \cite{Ambrosini2007}. Their identification of subtelomere-specific block families—distinct from pericentromeric duplicon families—enabled subsequent population surveys to classify specific arms by block content rather than relying solely on FISH probes.

**Li et al. (2008)** used 642 microsatellite loci in 938 individuals from 51 populations to demonstrate that genetic diversity decreases monotonically with geographic distance from sub-Saharan Africa, consistent with a serial founder-effect model of OOA dispersal \cite{subtel_popgen_li2008}. This paper formalised the "diversity gradient" expectation: loci in the ancestral African population had the longest time to accumulate variation and the largest effective population size, so African samples should retain higher haplotype diversity at any neutrally evolving locus. The Mefford-and-Trask prediction that block frequencies should reflect population history follows from this principle.

**Stong et al. (2014)** substantially improved human subtelomere assemblies by filling telomere-adjacent gaps using clone-based resources and providing annotation of CTCF/cohesin binding across all subtelomeric loci \cite{Stong2014}. Their analysis identified 18 of 46 chromosome arms as "highly variable," where structurally distinct haplotypes appear in more than 10% of surveyed genomes—a direct motivation for population-scale long-read sequencing.

**Sudmant et al. (2015)** analysed copy-number variation at segmental duplications genome-wide in 2,504 individuals from 125 populations and found that duplications—disproportionately concentrated at subtelomeric and pericentromeric regions—show fundamentally different population-genetic properties from deletions, with higher between-population differentiation and stronger evidence of selection signals \cite{Sudmant2015}. This established the conceptual principle that subtelomeric structural variants carry population signal at higher Fst than the genome-wide SNP average, even when the specific exchange mechanism (NAHR, gene conversion) partially homogenises frequencies.

**Levy-Sakin et al. (2019)** provided the first large-scale population survey of subtelomeric structural haplotypes using BioNano optical mapping of 154 genomes from 26 populations sampled from the 1000 Genomes Project \cite{subtel_popgen_levysakin2019}. They confirmed African-enriched paralogy-block frequencies at chromosome 16q (p < 0.05 after Bonferroni correction) in an assembly-independent framework and showed that most other arms have near-uniform block frequencies across superpopulations. This intermediate-scale result set the stage for the HPRC v2 full-pangenome analysis.

**Young et al. (2020)** extended this optical-mapping approach, providing a comprehensive catalogue of subtelomeric structural variation at population scale and noting that while some chromosome arms show significantly different frequencies in the African superpopulation, the majority show no significant superpopulation stratification—consistent with exchange-dominated arms that have equilibrated frequencies globally \cite{Young2020}.

## Findings most relevant to C6

Abstract claim C6—"Population-genetic analyses resolve subtelomeric clustering across human ancestries"—is grounded in two complementary analytical results from Andrea's chapter 04 and chapter 12.

The first is the Fisher 2×5 contingency test of cross-arm versus self-arm frequency across the five HPRC superpopulations. Of 19 arm/community pairs tested (from all 11 multi-arm communities), 10 pass the Benjamini-Hochberg-corrected threshold. The strongest single signal is chr16q (C3 community), where 70% (60/86) of cross-arm-affinity haplotypes belong to African-ancestry individuals, versus 35% (54/154) of self-arm haplotypes—a corrected p-value of 4.7 × 10⁻⁴. This reproduces and precisely quantifies the African enrichment at the f7501 locus originally identified by FISH two decades earlier \cite{Trask1998, MeffordTrask2002, Young2020}. The chr4q D4Z4 locus (C1) also shows African enrichment of cross-arm haplotypes (36% versus the 28.8% African baseline), consistent with the higher chr4q/chr10q discordance rates in African populations noted by Mefford and Trask.

The second result is the pairwise Hudson Fst analysis across nine significant arm/community pairs, reported in chapter 12 as novel contribution #19 ("Subtelomeric Fst mirrors out-of-Africa history"). Mean Fst across all pairs is 0.044. AFR versus non-AFR pairs reach 0.10–0.15, while non-AFR pairs (AMR-EUR, EAS-SAS, etc.) range from 0.00 to 0.02. A neighbour-joining tree of these Fst distances recovers the expected out-of-Africa topology: AFR deepest split (distances 0.73–0.81 to all non-AFR groups), then EAS branching, then SAS/AMR/EUR clustering with the AMR-EUR pair showing the closest relationship (distance 0.22). This is precisely the topology recovered by Rosenberg et al. 2002, Li et al. 2008, and the 1000 Genomes Project using SNPs, microsatellites, and genome-wide CNV data \cite{subtel_popgen_rosenberg2002, subtel_popgen_li2008, subtel_popgen_1000g2010}.

The fact that nine arms out of the full complement carry the population signal—while acrocentrics, PARs, and other fully homogenised arms carry none—is mechanistically coherent: arms where exchange has completely homogenised the population (C7 silhouette = −0.029, C14 silhouette = −0.163) cannot carry Fst signal because the "alleles" have been erased. Arms where exchange is rare enough that ancestral differences persist—chr16q, chr4q, chr9q—retain the signal. This arm-by-arm heterogeneity is itself informative: it predicts that Fst will be highest at arms with intermediate exchange frequency, which is consistent with the theoretical expectation that the population-genetic information content of a biallelic structural marker peaks when the per-generation exchange rate is roughly comparable to the reciprocal of the ancestral effective population size.

Bhatia et al.'s 2013 analysis of FST estimation with rare variants is specifically relevant here \cite{subtel_popgen_bhatia2013}. Cross-arm subtelomeric haplotypes at non-acrocentric arms typically have minor allele frequency 0.05–0.40 in the HPRC dataset, placing them in the "common" range where standard Fst estimation is well-behaved. The Hudson estimator used by Andrea is the recommended approach under exactly these conditions.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

**Topic 03 (PHR concept)** provides the structural vocabulary for what is being differentiated at the population level. The Flint/Mefford two-domain model—distal multi-arm sharing versus proximal arm-restricted sharing—predicts that Fst should be higher at proximal loci where ectopic exchange is less frequent, preserving ancestral allele frequency differences. Andrea's two-domain boundary analysis (chapter 04) places arm-specific breakpoints at 10–445 kb from the telomere, and arms with breakpoints closer to the telomere (smaller high-exchange zones) should, on average, retain more population signal in their proximal subtelomeric regions. The population-genetics and architectural analyses converge on the same set of arms as carrying the signal.

**Topic 07 (NAHR, concerted evolution)** is the mechanistic counterpart of the population structure story. NAHR and gene conversion at subtelomeres are the within-generation exchange processes that, accumulated over evolutionary time, homogenise allele frequencies and erase the population signal that Mefford and Trask sought. The per-generation exchange rates estimated from pedigree data (topic 11) provide direct quantitative input to models of how quickly population signal decays at each arm. Arms with high pedigree-detected exchange rates should show low Fst; the HPRC data is consistent with this prediction, though a formal correlation analysis with per-arm exchange rates has not yet been published.

**Topic 05 (acrocentric p-arms and rDNA)** represents the extreme case where population signal is fully erased. The acrocentric p-arms forming community C7 show paralog distance smaller than allelic distance—complete interchromosomal homogenisation—and correspondingly near-zero Fst. Understanding why these arms are so thoroughly homogenised while autosomal subtelomeres are not requires knowledge of the rDNA-adjacent context reviewed in topic 05.

**Topic 04 (sex chromosomes and PARs)** provides the structurally distinct PAR cases where "allele frequency" is set by obligate crossover (PAR1) or near-obligate exchange (PAR2), making Fst at these loci reflect sex-linked demography rather than the autosomal out-of-Africa gradient. The chrX_p PAR1 result (18/18 non-cross-arm haplotypes exclusively African) is consistent with rare retention of the ancestral PAR1 configuration in the population with the highest genetic diversity, but it differs mechanistically from the autosomal Fst signal.

## Open questions and recent developments

**Is the out-of-Africa Fst topology robust to exchange history?** The critical caveat of the HPRC v2 result is that only 10 of 19 arm/community pairs show significant population differentiation, and the topology is derived from a subset of structurally distinguishable arms. A formal coalescent model jointly estimating arm-specific exchange rates and population history from allele frequency data has not been developed. Anderson et al.'s 2008 analysis of Drosophila subtelomeric DNA found strikingly reversed patterns in African versus North American samples—North American populations showed *higher* subtelomeric nucleotide diversity than Africans, opposite to the euchromatic norm—suggesting that local exchange architecture at the telomere tip can override demographic expectations in ways that vary species by species and arm by arm \cite{subtel_popgen_anderson2008}. Human subtelomeres are structurally quite different from Drosophila telomeres (no retrotransposon-based extension, strong duplicon block organisation), but the Drosophila data is a cautionary reminder that the exchange mechanism modulates population-genetic signatures in ways that may not be captured by standard neutral-locus Fst theory.

**What quantitative relationship exists between exchange frequency and phylogenetic resolution?** The Mefford-and-Trask (2002) warning was empirically motivated but lacked a formal model. Bhatia et al.'s (2013) demonstration that rare variants inflate Fst estimates is directly relevant to subtelomeric cross-arm haplotypes, which are often found at low frequencies in non-AFR populations \cite{subtel_popgen_bhatia2013}. If most of the AFR Fst signal at chr16q comes from a small number of non-AFR haplotypes that recently acquired the cross-arm sequence through exchange, the effective information content is lower than the uncorrected Fst suggests. Patterson et al.'s principal-component eigenanalysis framework \cite{subtel_popgen_patterson2006} could in principle be applied to a haplotype × arm-community matrix to identify the principal axes of subtelomeric population structure without the intermediate step of Fst estimation, providing an independent validation of the out-of-Africa topology and identifying which arms drive the structure.

**Can long-read population pangenomes resolve subtelomeric haplotype diversity at full scale?** The 1000 Genomes Project and subsequent large-scale sequencing efforts have produced rich population-level catalogues of SNP variation (2,504+ individuals) and increasingly of structural variation \cite{subtel_popgen_1000g2010, Sudmant2015}, but the most distal subtelomeric intervals are systematically underrepresented in short-read and even in medium-coverage long-read structural variant catalogs. Young et al. 2020's optical mapping across 154 genomes was the largest subtelomere-specific population survey before the HPRC v2 analysis, covering 26 populations at resolution sufficient to distinguish large paralogy block differences but not phased haplotype sequence \cite{Young2020}. The HPRC v2 resource with 465 haplotype-resolved long-read assemblies is a step-change in power, but 233 individuals from five super-populations is still modest compared to the SNP-based datasets used to characterise the out-of-Africa gradient. The next generation of analyses will require assembling subtelomeric haplotypes from the hundreds to thousands of diverse individuals now becoming available from initiatives such as HGSVC3, 1000 Genomes ONT, and UK Biobank long-read projects.

**Do subtelomeric structural haplotypes carry population-specific selection signatures?** The Sudmant et al. 2015 genome-wide CNV scan identified Fst outliers consistent with positive selection at segmental-duplication loci \cite{Sudmant2015}. Subtelomeric arms carrying gene families with dosage-sensitive functions—olfactory receptors (OR4F, associated with olfactory perception differences), immunoglobulin-related pseudogenes (IL9RP1/3/4), and D4Z4 macrosatellites (linked to FSHD muscular dystrophy risk)—are candidates for population-specific selection acting on subtelomeric copy number or sequence content. The HPRC v2 data includes the resolution needed to test whether AFR-enriched cross-arm haplotypes at specific arms show elevated population differentiation beyond neutral demographic expectations, but this test has not been published in the current manuscript.

**How does the out-of-Africa subtelomeric Fst topology change with expanded African sampling?** Population genetic studies consistently show that sub-Saharan African populations are not monolithic: different African groups (West African, East African, San, Khoe, etc.) carry substantially different allele-frequency spectra, and studies with deeper African sampling show finer-grained within-Africa structure that places the deepest human split in southern African Khoe-San or East African hunter-gatherer populations rather than simply in "Africa" as a whole \cite{subtel_popgen_rosenberg2002, subtel_popgen_li2008}. The HPRC v2 African samples, while globally diverse, are predominantly of West African ancestry (consistent with HapMap Yoruba and similar reference populations). Extending the subtelomeric Fst analysis to include San, Hadza, Dinka, and other groups with deep coalescence times will test whether subtelomeric exchange frequencies track within-Africa population structure as well as the continental-level OOA gradient—a prediction that follows directly from the neutral ancestral-frequency model but has not yet been tested.

The 2022–2026 era of telomere-to-telomere assemblies and population pangenomics has begun to answer questions that FISH and clone-based methods could only approach qualitatively. The HPRC v2 analysis reported in the current manuscript is the first genome-wide, haplotype-resolved, population-stratified answer to the question Mefford and Trask raised in 2002: subtelomeric exchange-frequency variation does carry the out-of-Africa phylogenetic signal, and it does so at quantifiable Fst values consistent with moderately differentiated structural loci rather than either a null (no signal) or a perfect-marker outcome. This positions human subtelomeres as structurally and demographically interpretable elements of the genome rather than intractable patchworks where ancestry signals are irretrievably confounded with exchange history.
