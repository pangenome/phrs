# Topic 05: Acrocentric short arms — rDNA, NORs, and Robertsonian translocation

**Slug:** acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian
**Anchor claims:** C5 (acrocentric clade in subtelomeric similarity tree), C8 (concerted evolution at rDNA / ongoing exchange)
**Time range:** 1916–present

---

## Era overview

The five human acrocentric chromosomes — 13, 14, 15, 21 and 22 — share a structurally unique short arm whose biology has been the subject of investigation for more than a century. Their distinguishing feature is the nucleolus organizer region (NOR), which holds tandem arrays of the ~43 kb 45S rDNA repeat unit and is flanked by satellite DNA blocks, segmental duplications and the centromeric heterochromatin. Together these regions account for the majority of human ribosomal RNA gene copies and a substantial fraction of constitutive heterochromatin in the karyotype. They are also the canonical substrate for the Robertsonian translocation, an entire-arm fusion event with an empirical population frequency of roughly one per thousand newborns and a clinically documented role in pregnancy loss, infertility and trisomy syndromes \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_robertson1916,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_jarmuzSzymczak2014}.

The intellectual arc of the field begins with W. R. B. Robertson's 1916 description of whole-arm fusions in grasshoppers, which gave the rearrangement class its name \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_robertson1916}. Cytogenetic mapping in the 1970s — anchored by Henderson, Warburton and Atwood's in situ hybridization placement of the rDNA on the five acrocentric short arms — established the chromosomal address of the human ribosomal cistrons \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_henderson1972,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_schmickel1973}. Molecular work in the 1980s and 1990s then established that rDNA variants and satellite DNA blocks are shared across heterologous acrocentrics, a pattern that demands ongoing concerted evolution rather than independent drift \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_krystal1981,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_worton1988}. The third era opened with the T2T-CHM13 assembly and the HPRC pangenome, which finally produced sequence-level views of the acrocentric short arms and made an all-to-all population-scale similarity test feasible \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_nurk2022,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_vollger2022}. The result, made explicit by Guarracino and colleagues in 2023, is that the five acrocentric short arms behave as a single recombining clade — the canonical pseudohomology system — with the Robertsonian translocation as its most visible meiotic outcome \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_guarracino2023,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025}.

## Key papers in chronological order

**Robertson 1916** described whole-arm fusions in *Tettigidae* grasshoppers and established the rearrangement class that bears his name \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_robertson1916}. **Henderson, Warburton and Atwood 1972** localized human rDNA to the short arms of the five acrocentric chromosomes by *in situ* hybridization, producing the foundational chromosomal address used by every subsequent NOR study \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_henderson1972}. **Schmickel 1973** quantified the human rDNA gene complement at roughly 200 copies per haploid genome by hybridization, providing the first dosage estimate for the cistrons distributed across these five sites \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_schmickel1973}. **Krystal et al. 1981** used somatic cell hybrids to show that nonhomologous human acrocentric chromosomes carry the same intergenic-spacer length variants — direct evidence that sequence information moves between heterologous NORs and a foundational test of concerted evolution \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_krystal1981}. **Worton et al. 1988** mapped the orientation of the rDNA tandem arrays and confirmed the conservation of the 5′ end across acrocentrics \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_worton1988}. **Therman, Susman and Denniston 1989** analyzed satellite associations and the cohort of literature-reported Robertsonian translocations and noted that chromosomes 13 and 14 dominate the empirical distribution \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_therman1989}. **Page, Shaffer and Shaffer 1996** narrowed the molecular breakpoints of common rob(13;14) and rob(14;21) translocations to the boundary between satellite III subfamilies and rDNA \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_page1996}. **Bandyopadhyay et al. 2001** identified seven new satellite III subfamilies and mapped them across the acrocentric short arms, generating the molecular substrate for breakpoint analysis \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_bandyopadhyay2001}. **Bandyopadhyay et al. 2002** showed that common Robertsonian translocations are nearly always dicentric, that they form predominantly during female meiosis, and that nucleolar association is a plausible cause of the increased pairing of nonhomologous acrocentric short arms \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_bandyopadhyay2002}. **Stults et al. 2008** used optical pulsed-field gel electrophoresis to measure rDNA cluster lengths across multigenerational pedigrees and reported a meiotic rearrangement frequency exceeding 10% per cluster per meiosis \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_stults2008}. **Floutsakou et al. 2013** cloned and sequenced the proximal junction (PJ) and distal junction (DJ) regions flanking the rDNA on chromosome 21, found that PJ and DJ are 95% and 99% identical across the five acrocentrics, and identified the long noncoding distal junction transcript \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_floutsakou2013}. **Stimpson et al. 2014** linked nucleolar organization, rDNA stability and acrocentric chromosome integrity to telomere function, showing that telomere dysfunction triggers acrocentric short-arm decondensation and inter-arm fusion \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_stimpson2014}. **Jarmuz-Szymczak et al. 2014** further refined Robertsonian breakpoints using FISH on de novo and familial cases \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_jarmuzSzymczak2014}. **McStay 2016** synthesized the field as "genomic dark matter" and argued for the chromosomal context of NORs as a determinant of stability \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_mcstay2016}. **van Sluis et al. 2019** expanded the architectural picture and confirmed near-identity of acrocentric short arms across humans and chimpanzee \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_vanSluis2019}. **Nurk et al. 2022** delivered the T2T-CHM13 assembly that closed the acrocentric short-arm gaps for the first time \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_nurk2022}. **Vollger et al. 2022** quantified the segmental duplication content: 45.1 of 68.1 Mbp of newly resolved SD sequence in T2T-CHM13 falls within acrocentric short arms, and 54% of acrocentric SDs are copy-number variable in 268 diverse humans \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_vollger2022}. **Guarracino et al. 2023** used the HPRC v1 pangenome to demonstrate population-scale recombination between heterologous acrocentric chromosomes and made the pseudohomologous-region (PHR) framing explicit \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_guarracino2023}. **de Lima, Guarracino et al. 2025** completed assemblies of three Robertsonian chromosomes (rob(13q14q), rob(14q21q)) and identified the SST1 macrosatellite array — inverted on chromosome 14 — as the recombining homology that produces the dominant fusion product \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025}. **Cechova, Hartley et al. 2025** extended the analysis across complete great-ape acrocentric assemblies, showing recurrent segmental duplication, FRG1 amplification and centromere repositioning across 25 Myr of ape evolution \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_cechovaHartley2025apes}. **Hartley et al. 2026** developed a short-read genotyping method for ROB carriers and applied it to the UK Biobank (~490 k samples) plus a healthy newborn cohort, recovering the historical 0.11–0.12% prevalence and uncovering distal-junction copy-number variation in HPRC genomes \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_hartley2026biobank}.

## Findings most relevant to claims C5 and C8

**C5 — the acrocentric clade.** The abstract names "acrocentric short arms" as one of the recovered clades in the subtelomeric similarity tree. The current manuscript's chapter 01 places chr13_p, chr14_p, chr15_p, chr21_p and chr22_p together in community C7 with extreme cross-arm affinity (>85% on most p-arms, 100% on chr15_p), and the allele-vs-paralog Wilcoxon reverses sign at C7 with paralog distance smaller than allelic distance (p = 2.0e-7). This is exactly the homogenized-clade signature predicted by the literature: Krystal's IGS-variant sharing across heterologous chromosomes \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_krystal1981}, Floutsakou's 95–99% PJ/DJ identity \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_floutsakou2013}, and Stults' >10% per-meiosis rearrangement frequency at rDNA clusters \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_stults2008} all describe a system in which the five p-arms are sequence-interchangeable. Guarracino 2023's HPRC v1 pangenome graph already named C7 as the dominant acrocentric community at coarser scale \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_guarracino2023}; the v2 analysis adds population-genetic resolution and the gene-replacement score (1.000 at chr13_p→chr14_p, matching the PAR pairs).

**C8 — concerted evolution at rDNA / ongoing exchange.** The abstract's title commits to "concerted evolution" in the loose sense of ongoing recombination among the five chromosome ends. The literature provides three converging lines of evidence: first, the rDNA-variant-sharing tests of Krystal 1981 and Worton 1988 demonstrate that sequence information has moved between heterologous NORs at rates fast enough to homogenize variants on a population timescale \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_krystal1981,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_worton1988}; second, the Robertsonian translocation cohort itself (1/1000 newborns) provides direct empirical evidence that whole-arm exchange between heterologous acrocentrics occurs in the present generation at a measurable rate \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_therman1989,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_jarmuzSzymczak2014}; third, the de Lima/Guarracino 2025 SST1 model identifies a specific meiotic crossover mechanism — homologous recombination across an inverted SST1 macrosatellite array on chromosome 14 — as the molecular event that creates the dominant rob(13;14) and rob(14;21) products \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025}. The current manuscript's pedigree analysis (chapter 14) extends this with 538 directly observed inter-chromosomal patches in the WashU T2T pedigree, 92% within Leiden communities; the C7 acrocentric community is the most exchange-rich subgraph in that count, consistent with the cytogenetic and molecular literature predicting that these arms exchange continuously.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

The acrocentric short arms sit at the intersection of every other subtelomere topic in this paper. With **topic 03 (pseudohomologous regions)** they share the operational definition: PHRs are telomere-adjacent intervals that share high-identity sequence with other chromosome ends, and the acrocentric p-arms are the most extreme case of the pattern, with full sequence interchangeability rather than the partial-block patchwork seen at the q-arms or 10p–18p \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_floutsakou2013,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_guarracino2023}. With **topic 06 (D4Z4/DUX4)** they share the duplicated-block-with-disease-relevance template, but the acrocentric exchange rate is roughly 50× higher than the four-event 4q–10q transfer history in human evolution — the acrocentrics are the steady-state limit of the same process \cite{Lemmers2010,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_floutsakou2013}. With **topic 08 (meiotic bouquet/nuclear envelope)** they share the spatial mechanism: nucleolar association of the five p-arms during meiotic prophase I is the topological cause of the homogenization, and the same Hi-C signal that puts D4Z4 at the lamina puts the acrocentrics together at the nucleolus \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_bandyopadhyay2002,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_stimpson2014}. With **topic 09 (Hi-C/3D maps)** they provide the strongest exclusion-control test in the present manuscript: Mantel rho rises from 0.66 to 0.79 when the acrocentric p-arms are removed, indicating that the genome-wide signal does not depend on the C7 outlier. With **topic 10 (pangenome graphs / IMPG)** they are the proof-of-concept use case: the only reason a population-scale similarity test of these regions exists at all is the T2T-CHM13 plus HPRC v2 substrate, which closed the gaps that referenced-genome work could not \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_nurk2022,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_vollger2022}. With **topic 12 (HPRC population pangenomes)** they share the empirical scaffold; with **topic 13 (subtelomere population genetics / Fst)** they are the example where exchange is so fast that population-genetic signal is partly erased on the p-arms but partly retained in the polymorphic q-arm exchange frequencies. The acrocentric clade is therefore both the canonical example and the structural extremum of the manuscript's main thesis.

## Open questions and recent developments

Three substantive questions remain open. First, **mechanism**: while de Lima and Guarracino 2025 nailed down the SST1 macrosatellite as the homology used by the dominant Robertsonian fusions, the rarer rob(13;15), rob(14;15) and rob(15;22) products are not yet sequence-resolved, and it is not clear whether they share the same SST1 mechanism or use different homology blocks (rDNA itself, satellite III subfamilies, or the distal junction) \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_deLima2025,acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_jarmuzSzymczak2014}. Second, **rate**: Stults et al.'s estimate of >10% rDNA cluster rearrangement per meiosis \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_stults2008} is at the upper end of biology and has not been replicated at sequence resolution; the current manuscript's pedigree count (133 gene-conversion-like patches at score ≥0.81 in WashU T2T) provides one calibration point per generation but does not yet separate intra-NOR conversion from inter-arm transfer. Third, **clinical penetrance**: Hartley et al.'s 2026 UK Biobank genotyping recovered the historical 0.11–0.12% prevalence and identified hidden distal-junction copy-number variation, raising the possibility that a substantial fraction of carriers harbor structural variants of unknown clinical consequence \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_hartley2026biobank}.

Recent developments are accelerating: the Cechova/Hartley 2025 great-ape acrocentric atlas places human acrocentric architecture in 25 Myr of mosaic evolution \cite{acrocentric_rdna_robertsonian_cechovaHartley2025apes}, the WashU multi-generational T2T pedigree provides the first directly observed inter-chromosomal exchange events in a living family \cite{Cechova2025}, and the HPRC v2 release adds 465 near-complete assemblies that finally make population-scale tests of the homogenization rate feasible. Three predictions follow: that the SST1 mechanism extends to the rarer Robertsonian products by way of the inverted DJ; that the per-meiosis rearrangement rate, when measured at sequence resolution across a few hundred trios, will fall in the 1–10% range Stults predicted; and that clinically benign distal-junction variation will turn out to be common at population scale, in line with the Hartley et al. genotyping findings. The acrocentric short arms are no longer dark matter — they are the cleanest empirical example of subtelomeric concerted evolution we have.
