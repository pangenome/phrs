# Topic 05: Acrocentric short arms, rDNA, and Robertsonian translocations, 1916–present

## Claim map

This review anchors the acrocentric chromosome literature to the current abstract claims as follows. The literature directly supports **C5** (acrocentric short arms as the canonical pseudohomology clade: Xp/Yp + Xq/Yq via PARs, **acrocentrics**, 10p–18p, the {22q,21q,19q,1q,13q,17q} clade, 4q–10q DUX4) and **C8** (concerted evolution driven by ongoing recombination). It also provides the mechanistic and evolutionary backdrop that makes the HPRC v2 pangenome analysis comprehensible: the nucleolus as a nuclear neighborhood that concentrates acrocentric short arms and thereby generates ectopic-recombination opportunity, the rDNA homology that first made acrocentrics recognizable as a cohort, and the satellite and duplicon architecture that flanks rDNA arrays and constitutes the extended pseudohomologous region (PHR) detectable by the project's 95%-identity threshold. Guarracino 2023 is the direct predecessor of the current manuscript in the same research arc; de Lima/Guarracino 2025 resolves the mechanistic endpoint (SST1 breakpoint, chromosome fusion) that the predecessor predicted. The current manuscript adds pangenome scale, community detection, pedigree-observed patches, and 3D contact validation—none of which appear in either predecessor.

## Era overview

The acrocentric chromosomes occupy a singular place in human genetics: they are the only chromosomes whose identity as a group was recognized before DNA sequencing, grounded instead in cytological observation of secondary constrictions, satellite associations, and nucleolar activity. The story begins as early as 1916, when W.R.B. Robertson described centric-fusion events in grasshopper karyotypes—what we now call Robertsonian translocations—observing that telocentric or acrocentric chromosomes fuse at their centromeric regions to produce a larger metacentric chromosome [@Robertson1916]. The term "Robertsonian" was coined much later, but the phenomenon Robertson identified—whole-arm fusion at centromere-adjacent heterochromatin—became the defining pathology of the acrocentric group once human karyotyping was established.

The earliest human cytogenetics of the 1950s–1960s recognized chromosomes 13, 14, 15, 21, and 22 as the human acrocentrics: five pairs in which the centromere is positioned very near one end, leaving a tiny, structurally distinctive short arm. The short arms were observed to carry satellite DNA, to participate in "satellite associations" at the nucleolus during mitotic prophase, and to harbor secondary constrictions marking sites of ribosomal RNA synthesis. Henderson et al. established that these secondary constrictions correspond to ribosomal DNA (rDNA) clusters on the short arms of all five pairs [@Henderson1972], creating the framework still used today: each acrocentric short arm carries a nucleolus organizer region (NOR) comprising tandemly repeated 45S rRNA gene units flanked by characteristic spacer and satellite sequences.

The key question that drove early work was mechanistic: why does Robertsonian translocation occur at a frequency of approximately 1 per 1,000 newborns and almost exclusively between acrocentric chromosomes? The answer was gradually assembled across several decades. Proximity within the nucleolus emerged as the primary spatial driver: Guichaoua et al. showed directly that ribosomal genes from two different acrocentric bivalents can be found co-associated in the same nucleolar fibrillar center in meiotic spermatocytes and oocytes, providing a mechanism by which non-homologous rDNA arrays come into close enough contact for ectopic recombination to initiate [@Guichaoua1983]. Therman et al. later used large cytogenetic surveys to establish the nonrandom pattern of which acrocentric pairs preferentially participate in Robertsonian events in different individuals, showing that satellite-association frequencies predict translocation frequencies and that individual variation in NOR activity shapes individual variation in translocation risk [@Therman1989].

The molecular era sharpened this picture: Worton et al. determined that human rRNA genes are transcribed in the telomere-to-centromere direction on acrocentric short arms, and that the 5' ends of the arrays are conserved across all five pairs—suggesting shared ancient origin and ongoing sequence homogenization [@Worton1988]. By the 1990s it was understood that the proximal and distal flanking regions of rDNA arrays also share sequence features among acrocentrics, implying that homogenization extends beyond the rDNA arrays themselves into the adjacent duplicon architecture. This set the conceptual stage for Floutsakou et al.'s systematic characterization of the full shared genomic architecture around the NOR [@Floutsakou2013].

The 2000s–2010s brought two interrelated developments. First, the subtelomeric revolution initiated by Mefford, Trask and colleagues extended the PHR concept genome-wide, explicitly including acrocentric p-arms as a special case of the general rule that telomere-adjacent sequence is highly dynamic and shared across chromosome ends [@MeffordTrask2002; @Linardopoulou2005]. Second, improved clone-based and computational assemblies of acrocentric short arms began to resolve the duplicon blocks flanking rDNA: Floutsakou et al. characterized the proximal junction (PJ, ~207 kb) and distal junction (DJ, ~380 kb) regions bordering every NOR, establishing that the DJ contains a large inverted repeat that is unique to acrocentric short arms but conserved across all five pairs, while the PJ is enriched for pericentromeric segmental duplications common to other heterochromatic borders [@Floutsakou2013]. This work established that acrocentric p-arms are not simply rDNA plus satellite, but contain complex euchromatin-like architecture whose homology across chromosomes provides substrate for both concerted evolution and Robertsonian fusion.

The T2T era and the HPRC draft pangenome have, since 2022, transformed understanding of the acrocentric short arms from inference to direct sequence. Nurk et al.'s complete T2T-CHM13 assembly finally provided gapless sequences for all five acrocentric short arm pairs for the first time [@Nurk2022], enabling the resolution of rDNA array organization, satellite composition, DJ/PJ boundaries, and distal junction sequence in atomic detail. Altemose et al. used this foundation to characterize the short arms comprehensively, quantifying rDNA copy number, satellite repeat types (HSAT5, SATR1, GSATII, ACRO1, CER types), and their distribution relative to the rDNA array and the DJ inverted repeat [@Altemose2022]. The pangenome then added haplotype diversity: Guarracino et al. used the HPRC draft pangenome to show that the short arms of all five acrocentric pairs form a single inter-chromosomal recombination community at the pangenome scale, directly observing patches of recent exchange and linking them mechanistically to the elevated Robertsonian translocation rate [@Guarracino2023]. De Lima and Guarracino et al. subsequently resolved the breakpoint at atomic resolution, identifying SST1—a macrosatellite DNA embedded in the DJ-adjacent region on chromosomes 13, 14, and 21—as the common site at which fusions initiate, with a human-specific inversion on chromosome 14 enabling a crossover geometry that produces the fusion product [@deLima2025].

## Key papers in chronological order

**Robertson 1916** [@Robertson1916]: Published as a description of whole-arm chromosome fusions in grasshopper (*Brachystola*) karyotypes, this paper introduced the concept of centric fusion as a class of chromosomal rearrangement. Robertson was the first to note that two telocentric or acrocentric chromosomes fuse at their centromeric regions to produce a larger metacentric. This paper does not address human genetics, rDNA, or molecular mechanisms, but it established the observational basis and the name for the most common chromosomal structural variant in humans.

**Henderson et al. 1972** [@Henderson1972]: Using in situ hybridization with rRNA probes, Henderson and colleagues mapped ribosomal DNA to the secondary constrictions on the short arms of all five human acrocentric chromosome pairs (13, 14, 15, 21, 22), establishing the canonical layout of NORs in the human genome. This paper is the primary citation for the claim that human rDNA is confined to acrocentric short arms and provides the framework for all subsequent work on nucleolar association, Robertsonian translocation, and concerted evolution of rDNA repeats. The estimate of approximately 300 rDNA copies per diploid genome distributed across ten NOR-bearing chromosomes originates from this and closely related work.

**Guichaoua et al. 1983** [@Guichaoua1983]: The PNAS paper on the structural basis for Robertsonian translocations used electron microscopy of meiotic cells (spermatocytes and oocytes) to demonstrate that rDNA sequences from two different acrocentric bivalents physically associate in the same nucleolar fibrillar center during meiotic prophase. This "two-bivalents-one-fibrillar-center" observation is the earliest direct mechanistic evidence for the proximity-then-recombination model. It connects the spatial biology of the nucleolus to the cytogenetic phenomenon of Robertsonian translocation by showing that NOR-bearing non-homologues are brought into contact by the process of ribosome biogenesis itself.

**Worton et al. 1988** [@Worton1988]: This Science paper established that human rDNA genes on acrocentric short arms are all oriented in the same direction (transcribed telomere-to-centromere) and that the 5' end region of the rDNA cluster is conserved across all five pairs. This was early molecular evidence for trans-chromosomal sequence homogenization: conservation of 5' ends among ten non-homologous chromosomes implies either shared ancestry or ongoing sequence exchange, making acrocentrics the first documented example of what we now call concerted evolution of rDNA in humans.

**Therman et al. 1989** [@Therman1989]: A large cytogenetic survey established the nonrandom participation of specific acrocentric pairs in Robertsonian translocations. The study analyzed satellite-association frequencies in hundreds of cells from multiple individuals, showing that the chromosomes most frequently observed in satellite associations (co-clustering at the nucleolus during mitotic prophase) are also the ones most frequently fused in Robertsonian translocations in that individual, and that this preference varies across individuals. This made satellite-association frequency a predictor of translocation risk and connected individual-level NOR activity variation to population-level translocation type frequencies.

**Mefford and Trask 2002** [@MeffordTrask2002]: The landmark Nature Reviews Genetics synthesis defined the PHR concept for subtelomeres generally and explicitly treated acrocentric p-arms as a paradigmatic case—specifically, the most extreme case of interchromosomal sharing, where alleles are less similar to each other than paralogs across non-homologous chromosomes, and where cross-arm affinity approaches completeness. Their review introduced the "patchwork" model of subtelomeric organization and predicted that acrocentric sharing might provide phylogenetic signal while warning that exchange history could confound it. The current manuscript's HPRC v2 analysis both confirms this prediction (Fst tree recovers out-of-Africa topology) and validates the warning (Fst signal is partially confounded by recombination history).

**Linardopoulou et al. 2005** [@Linardopoulou2005]: Using extensive paralogous sequence analysis, this Nature paper established that human subtelomeres are hot spots of interchromosomal recombination and segmental duplication, characterizing the duplicon blocks exchanged among chromosome ends and inferring repeated transfer events. For acrocentric short arms specifically, the paper confirmed their status as a distinct community of extreme sharing, and identified the 10p–18p pair as another noteworthy case of a subtelomeric similarity cluster, providing direct historical support for the current manuscript's 10p–18p clade.

**Floutsakou et al. 2013** [@Floutsakou2013]: This Genome Research paper characterized the shared genomic architecture of the two regions flanking every NOR on human acrocentric short arms: the proximal junction (PJ, ~207 kb) and the distal junction (DJ, ~380 kb). The DJ was found to be dominated by a large, high-identity inverted repeat that is unique to acrocentric short arms but conserved across all five chromosome pairs, while the PJ is rich in pericentromeric segmental duplications. Both boundaries show evidence of ongoing interchromosomal exchange, establishing that the PHR of acrocentric p-arms extends well beyond the rDNA arrays themselves. The DJ sequence proved to be the functional anchor for rDNA-distal duplicon exchange and, as the de Lima/Guarracino 2025 paper later showed, the region containing the SST1 macrosatellite where Robertsonian fusions initiate.

**Nurk et al. 2022** [@Nurk2022]: The Science paper presenting the complete T2T-CHM13 human genome provided gapless sequences of all five acrocentric short arm pairs for the first time. This assembly resolved the full satellite repeat composition (HSAT5, SATR1, GSATII, ACRO1, ACRO138, CER types), the rDNA arrays, the DJ inverted repeat, and the pericentromeric transition zones. Without this reference, the Guarracino 2023 analysis of HPRC contigs and the de Lima/Guarracino 2025 breakpoint-resolution analysis would not have been possible.

**Altemose et al. 2022** [@Altemose2022]: This Genome Research paper used the T2T-CHM13 foundation to produce a comprehensive characterization of human acrocentric chromosome short arms—their sequence content, repeat composition, structural organization, and assembly challenges. It provided the first accurate estimates of rDNA copy number variation and the satellite repeat landscape, and established the conceptual framework (distal stalks, rDNA arrays, PJ/DJ boundaries) used in subsequent pangenome analyses.

**Guarracino et al. 2023** [@Guarracino2023]: Published in Nature, this paper is the direct predecessor of the current manuscript in the same research arc. Using the HPRC draft pangenome reference, Guarracino et al. applied an all-to-all comparison strategy to show that contigs from all five acrocentric short arm pairs cluster into a single community, and that the PHRs on these short arms are sites of active recombination. The paper provided sequence- and population-based confirmation of the hypotheses first developed from cytogenetic satellite-association studies. Key findings: (i) all five p-arm pairs cluster together at sequence level; (ii) the recombination is ongoing, not merely historical; (iii) the clustering and exchange explain the elevated rate of Robertsonian translocations. This paper anchors C5 directly and supports C8 by demonstrating ongoing exchange.

**de Lima, Guarracino et al. 2025** [@deLima2025]: Published in Nature in November 2025, this paper assembled complete sequences of three human Robertsonian chromosomes and identified the molecular mechanism of fusion. The breakpoint maps to SST1, a macrosatellite DNA within the DJ-adjacent region on chromosomes 13, 14, and 21. The chromosome 14 carries a human-specific inversion of a segment containing SST1, which enables a meiotic crossover event between the inverted copy on chromosome 14 and the non-inverted copy on chromosome 13 or 21, producing the fusion product. The paper shows that Robertsonian chromosomes have two centromeric arrays (explaining dicentric paradox) but only one active centromere, have lost all rDNA, and can propagate through unequal segregation. This completes the mechanistic arc that Guarracino 2023 opened.

## Findings most relevant to claims C5 and C8 of the abstract

**Claim C5** — "Neighbor-joining trees recover known relationships … acrocentric short arms" — is supported by a convergent body of evidence spanning six decades. The earliest anchor is Henderson 1972 NOR mapping, which established that the five acrocentric pairs constitute a functional grouping (all NOR-bearing) before any sequence data existed. Satellite-association and cytogenetic data (Therman 1989) established that these chromosomes cluster at the nucleolus more than any other pairing, which is the physical basis for the grouping. Molecular sequence analysis (Worton 1988, Floutsakou 2013) showed that the rDNA arrays and both flanking junction regions are conserved across all five pairs at high sequence identity, quantifying the homology that drives the cytological behavior. The key demonstration that the shared sequence detectable at the pangenome scale is not merely ancient frozen homology but the product of active, ongoing recombination comes from Guarracino 2023, who showed that HPRC contigs from all five pairs cluster together and exhibit patches of very recent exchange within the 95%-identity threshold. The current manuscript extends this: its "C7" community (in Leiden notation) recovers the five-pair acrocentric cluster independently, the allele-vs.-paralog Wilcoxon test shows a unique reversal for acrocentrics (the only community where paralogs are more similar than alleles, reflecting complete inter-chromosomal homogenization), and the cross-arm affinity rate approaches or exceeds 85% for most acrocentric p-arms—the highest of any community.

The specific claim that the acrocentric community is identifiable by "neighbor-joining trees of subtelomeric similarity" is consistent with the existing literature: any distance measure based on high-identity sequence alignment will cluster these five pairs together, because they share a median sequence identity of approximately 99% with each other—a hallmark of concerted evolution documented by Guarracino 2023. Whether the tree is built by NJ, UPGMA, or Leiden community detection, the result is the same: all five acrocentric pairs cluster, with no non-acrocentric chromosome falling inside that cluster. This robustness makes the acrocentric clade the most certain of all the communities named in C5, unlike the 10p–18p or {22q–17q–19q–21q–1q–13q} clades which are supported by narrower lines of evidence.

**Claim C8** — "concerted evolution and unorthodox recombination" — is most directly supported by the acrocentric literature, because acrocentrics are the canonical system in which concerted evolution was first recognized and mechanistically explained. The early evidence for concerted evolution came from rDNA: Worton et al. showed that 5' rDNA ends are conserved across ten non-homologous chromosomes, implying continuous homogenization [@Worton1988]. Stults et al. later showed that rDNA cluster lengths are highly variable within and between individuals and that the variation is meiotic in origin (>10% change per cluster per meiosis), directly demonstrating that rDNA arrays are in perpetual exchange [@Stults2009]. Floutsakou 2013 extended the concerted-evolution observation from rDNA to the flanking DJ: the DJ inverted repeat is 99%+ identical across all five chromosome pairs, which is inconsistent with neutral divergence from a common ancestor and requires ongoing trans-chromosomal sequence homogenization.

For the current manuscript, the most load-bearing argument for C8 is the pedigree data (chapter 14): 538 patches of recent inter-chromosomal exchange concentrated in acrocentric p-arms and other subtelomeric communities, 92% confined within Leiden communities. Among the acrocentric-specific patches, gene-conversion-like and crossover-like events are both present, and transmission across generations is directly observed. This is the "proof" of "ongoing exchange" in the loose concerted-evolution sense, moving from inference-from-sequence to direct observation of transmitted events. The de Lima/Guarracino 2025 SST1 breakpoint paper completes the picture by identifying the mechanistic substrate at which Robertsonian fusions (the extreme outcome of the same ectopic recombination) initiate—demonstrating that the same exchange substrate (DJ-adjacent macrosatellite) that drives ongoing sequence homogenization can, when a crossover occurs in the wrong orientation, produce a Robertsonian chromosome that propagates in the population.

**What is contested:** The precise fraction of concerted evolution attributable to gene conversion versus crossover exchange remains debated. Guarracino 2023 and the current manuscript's pedigree data both detect gene-conversion-like patches far more frequently than crossover-like patches (~133 vs. ~16 in the WashU pedigree, with the gene-conversion category dominating acrocentric-to-acrocentric exchange). Earlier population-genetic work had assumed crossover as the primary mechanism of rDNA homogenization (unequal crossing over within the rDNA arrays), but the pangenome data suggest that gene-conversion-like events are the predominant mode of inter-chromosomal exchange in the flanking PHR, consistent with biased gene conversion models. Whether the high rDNA mutation rate and unusual mutational spectrum recently documented by the pedigree study [@Porubsky2025de] reflect the same repair dynamics that drive gene conversion, or a distinct mechanism involving mismatch repair deficiency, remains an open question.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

**Intersection with Topic 03 (PHR concept, 1997–present):** The acrocentric system is the extreme case of the general PHR phenomenon described in Topic 03. The Flint/Mefford two-domain model (proximal vs. distal sharing) was characterized most clearly at specific chromosome ends (chr4p, chr16p, chr22q), but the model was anchored by the observation that acrocentric p-arms showed the most extreme, most complete sharing—a case where the "distal many-chromosome sharing domain" encompasses virtually the entire p-arm. Mefford and Trask's 2002 review, the foundational Topic 03 reference, explicitly singles out acrocentrics as the clearest example of complete inter-chromosomal identity. The current manuscript's allele-vs.-paralog Wilcoxon reversal at the C7 acrocentric community is the quantitative pangenome-scale confirmation of what Mefford and Trask described qualitatively.

**Intersection with Topic 04 (Sex chromosome PARs):** The PAR system (Topics 04) and the acrocentric system (this topic) are the two most extreme examples of inter-chromosomal pseudohomologous regions in the human genome, and they appear as the two clearly distinct poles in the allele-vs.-paralog reversal analysis. PARs show extreme sharing driven by obligate meiotic crossover; acrocentrics show extreme sharing driven by ectopic recombination via nucleolar proximity. Both are recovered as distinct communities by the Leiden algorithm (C14/C15 for PARs; C7 for acrocentrics) and both appear as named clades in C5 of the abstract. The mechanistic distinction—obligate crossover vs. ectopic recombination mediated by sequence homology—is important for interpreting the current manuscript's claim about "unorthodox recombination."

**Intersection with Topic 11 (Pedigree-based recombination detection):** The pedigree data (Topic 11) and the acrocentric literature are deeply intertwined in the present manuscript. The most informative recombination events in the WashU and CEPH1463 pedigrees are the acrocentric-to-acrocentric patches: 229 "acros_like" events in the WashU pedigree alone (≥5 patches in a flank, ≥3 source chromosomes), representing the most extensive NAHR activity in any community. The new pedigree study directly measuring acrocentric short arm recombination [@Porubsky2025de] is the most recent empirical anchor for this intersection, reporting 107 intergenerational transmissions from phased acrocentric short arms and characterizing both ectopic (Robertsonian-like breakpoint) and allelic recombination events.

## Open questions and recent developments

**Complete acrocentric short arm sequences at pangenome scale.** While Nurk et al. 2022 and Altemose et al. 2022 resolved the T2T-CHM13 acrocentric short arms, pangenome-scale haplotype diversity remained undercharacterized until very recently. The HPRC v2 analysis (current manuscript and Guarracino 2023 predecessor) provides the first population-scale view of acrocentric p-arm diversity, but several questions remain: how much variation exists in rDNA copy number across haplotypes, how does DJ sequence vary among the five chromosome pairs across a population, and whether the SST1 macrosatellite (the Robertsonian breakpoint identified by de Lima/Guarracino 2025) is itself polymorphic in copy number in ways that predict translocation risk. The 2026 biobank-scale Robertsonian genotyping preprint [@Cechova2026] addresses the last question by developing a DJ copy-number method applicable to short-read sequencing, providing the first sequencing-based diagnostic for Robertsonian carrier status and opening the door to population-scale association studies between acrocentric structural variation and disease.

**Mechanism of SST1-mediated fusion.** The de Lima/Guarracino 2025 paper identified SST1 as the common breakpoint in Robertsonian chromosomes involving chromosomes 13, 14, and 21, but several mechanistic details remain unresolved. What makes chromosome 15 and 22 Robertsonian events rarer than 13/14/21 events? The human-specific inversion on chromosome 14 was identified as enabling the crossover geometry, but the 13/21 breakpoints do not require an inversion—their frequency suggests either that SST1 arrays on 13 and 21 can recombine with each other directly, or that the nucleolar colocalization geometry places 13/21 in an orientation where SST1-to-SST1 alignment occurs spontaneously. Whether SST1 is also the breakpoint for the rarer der(14;15), der(14;22), der(13;22), and der(15;21) Robertsonian types is not yet clear from the available assemblies.

**Concerted evolution rate and mechanism.** Stults et al. showed rDNA array length changes in >10% of haplotypes per meiosis, and the pedigree data from the current manuscript show gene-conversion-like patches at high rate among acrocentric p-arms. But the molecular machinery driving inter-chromosomal gene conversion at rDNA and at the flanking DJ/PJ is not well defined. The elevated acrocentric p-arm mutation rate (10× higher than euchromatic autosomes, as measured in the 2025 de novo mutation preprint [@Porubsky2025de]) and the unusual mutational spectrum (elevated C>G, C>A; reduced C>T) suggest that the same unusual chromatin and replication environment that promotes recombination also promotes error-prone repair. Whether these signatures reflect mismatch repair deficiency, oxidative damage, or replication-transcription conflicts in the rDNA-active region is unresolved.

**rDNA dosage and gene expression.** The rDNA copy number on acrocentric chromosomes is among the most variable quantitative traits in the human genome. Stults et al. documented cluster lengths from 50 kb to >6 Mb in normal individuals. Recent work has linked rDNA dosage variation to cell growth rates, stress responses, and potentially disease risk. A 2026 preprint reports population-scale analysis of rDNA copy number variation and its association with human physiology and disease risk [@rDNAdosage2026]. The mechanistic connection between rDNA copy number homeostasis (driven by unequal crossover and gene conversion within and between acrocentric short arms) and the PHR community structure documented in the current manuscript has not been explicitly investigated: does having a larger rDNA array on one chromosome increase the probability of nucleolar association and therefore PHR exchange with other acrocentrics? If so, rDNA dosage would be a co-regulator of the concerted-evolution rate across the whole community.

**Origin and evolution of acrocentric chromosomes.** A December 2025 preprint analyzes the origin and evolution of acrocentric chromosomes across human and great apes [@Guarracino2025ape], extending the T2T and pangenome approaches to chimpanzee and bonobo genomes. The de Lima/Guarracino 2025 paper already noted that the chromosome 14 inversion is human-specific (not present in chimpanzee or bonobo), making the SST1-mediated 13;14 and 14;21 Robertsonian fusion geometry also human-specific. This comparative analysis opens the question of whether the acrocentric pseudohomology community is more or less coherent in non-human great apes, and whether the rate of Robertsonian translocation correlates with species-specific structural differences in the DJ/PJ regions.

**From clinical to population genomics.** Robertsonian translocations have been studied predominantly in clinical genetics for their association with uniparental disomy, Angelman syndrome, Prader-Willi syndrome (chromosomes 15), and Downs syndrome risk from rob(14;21) carriers. Population genetics of Robertsonian carriers has been limited by the inability to genotype carriers from routine sequencing data. The 2026 biobank preprint [@Cechova2026] provides a method to type carriers at scale, enabling the first population-level studies of selection on Robertsonian chromosomes, their geographic distribution, and their co-occurrence with specific acrocentric haplotypes. The current manuscript contributes the sequence-similarity substrate (DJ copy number, SST1 polymorphism) that this diagnostic approach quantifies, and the pedigree data show directly how frequently the relevant ectopic recombination events occur per meiosis.
