# Topic 14: Olfactory receptor OR4F pseudogenization gradient at human subtelomeres

## Claim map

This review anchors the olfactory receptor OR4F literature to the current abstract claims as follows. The literature directly supports **C5** (cladistic analysis and subtelomere gene-family heterogeneity: the OR4F pseudogenization gradient from 11.1% on chr7p to 99.8% on chr15q exemplifies the arm-specific sequence-divergence pattern that underlies the named communities). It provides indirect support for **C4** (extended interchromosomal homology at nearly all subtelomeres: OR gene blocks are among the defining duplicated families at chromosome ends) and **C8** (ongoing recombination shapes subtelomeres: the variable pseudogenization gradient reflects differential exchange histories among chromosome arms). The OR4F gradient does not directly anchor Hi-C (C7) or population-clustering (C6) claims, but it illustrates why the same subtelomeric community can contain both highly functional and completely pseudogenized copies of the same gene family — a biological implication of the exchange process central to C8.

## Era overview

The discovery of olfactory receptor (OR) genes as the largest known multigene family in the vertebrate genome opened a new chapter in understanding how gene family expansion shapes chromosome architecture. When Linda Buck and Richard Axel published their landmark 1991 Cell paper identifying a multigene family encoding odorant receptors, they estimated perhaps a thousand genes in rodents \cite{BuckAxel1991}. The genes were G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) encoded by a large, diverse superfamily and expressed in the olfactory epithelium. What followed over the next decade was a systematic effort to count, classify, and map these genes across the human genome — an effort that inevitably led to subtelomeres.

The first genome-scale surveys of OR genes in humans, made possible by early draft genome sequences in the late 1990s and the complete sequence in 2001, revealed that the gene family was both more complex and more burdened by pseudogenes than expected. In rodents, roughly 20–30% of OR genes are pseudogenes; in humans, estimates from early surveys converged on 52–72% pseudogenes, reflecting a dramatic evolutionary shift in olfactory dependency \cite{Glusman2001, Niimura2003}. The simultaneous observation that OR genes preferentially cluster at subtelomeric and pericentromeric chromosome ends — regions already known to harbor polymorphic duplicon blocks — immediately connected OR family evolution to the broader subtelomere biology now central to the present manuscript.

The key observation driving this topic is that OR gene pseudogenization is not uniformly distributed across chromosome ends. Certain subtelomeric OR clusters maintain relatively intact genes; others have undergone nearly complete pseudogenization. The pangenome analysis in the present study reveals this starkly: across 5,023 OR4F annotations spanning 16 chromosome arms, 62.1% are pseudogenes, but the arm-specific pseudogenization rate ranges from 11.1% on chr7p to 99.8% on chr15q — a nine-fold gradient that directly mirrors the structural heterogeneity of subtelomeric communities identified by the Leiden clustering analysis. Understanding what drives this gradient, and why particular chromosome ends have retained functional OR copies while others have not, requires tracing the intellectual lineage from the 1990s discovery of OR clusters through two decades of evolutionary genomics.

The scientific questions driving early work were: How many OR genes exist in the human genome? What fraction are functional? Where are they? How did they evolve? These questions were answered progressively as genome sequencing improved. But a harder question remained: Why do different chromosome ends carrying the same OR gene family show such different pseudogenization trajectories? The answer, as the pangenome data now suggests, lies in the biology of subtelomeric exchange itself.

## Key papers in chronological order

**Buck and Axel (1991)** \cite{BuckAxel1991} discovered the olfactory receptor gene family using a degenerate PCR strategy targeting conserved GPCR motifs. From approximately 18 clones, they inferred a family of perhaps a thousand genes in rodents. This Nobel Prize–winning work established the conceptual foundation: OR genes form a vast multigene family shaped by gene duplication, with individual members tuned to specific odorants through combinatorial coding.

**Rouquier et al. (1998)** \cite{Rouquier1998} provided the first chromosome-level survey of OR gene distribution in humans, mapping OR loci across all chromosomes using FISH and cosmid probes. They established that OR genes are found on nearly all human chromosomes (all except Y), concentrated in clusters, with a notably high fraction of pseudogenes in humans versus other mammals — the first quantitative evidence of the human pseudogenization burden.

**Trask et al. (1998)** \cite{Trask1998} made the connection to subtelomere biology explicit. They showed that members of the OR gene family are contained in large blocks of DNA duplicated polymorphically near the ends of human chromosomes. A block at 3q, 15q, and 19p in all 45 unrelated humans surveyed had additional polymorphic copies at 11 other subtelomeric locations. This was the first demonstration that OR genes participate in the interchromosomal duplicon system operating at chromosome ends, and it set the stage for understanding why OR gene content varies so dramatically across arms.

**Glusman et al. (2001)** \cite{Glusman2001} assembled the first comprehensive catalog of the human olfactory subgenome. Analyzing the draft human genome, they identified 906 OR sequences, of which 347 were pseudogenes. They described 18 gene families and 300 subfamilies. Critically, they noted that Class I ORs (fish-like, considered ancient relics in tetrapods) cluster primarily on chromosome 11, while Class II ORs are distributed across nearly all chromosomes, with prominent subtelomeric clusters. The approximately 37% pseudogene rate they reported for the OR4 and OR4F subgroups reflects the beginnings of arm-specific differential pseudogenization.

**Mefford et al. (2001)** \cite{Mefford2001} directly examined the subtelomeric OR gene block through comparative sequencing. Analyzing the multicopy block dispersed on at least 14 chromosome ends (containing homology to three OR genes), they found SNP evidence for recent interactions between non-homologous chromosomes. This was strong early evidence that OR-containing subtelomeric blocks are actively exchanged — not frozen in their chromosomal positions — providing a mechanism for the copy-number variation and pseudogenization heterogeneity observed across arms.

**Niimura and Nei (2003)** \cite{Niimura2003} published a major reannotation of all human OR genes using improved genome assembly data. They identified 388 intact ORFs and 414 pseudogenes (total 802, pseudogene fraction 51.6%). Their phylogenetic classification divided OR genes into 19 clades, with many tandem arrays reflecting local gene duplication. This paper established the quantitative baseline for the pseudogenization burden and the subfamily structure that later enabled arm-by-arm comparisons of pseudogenization rates.

**Young et al. (2002, 2003)** \cite{YoungTrask2002, YoungFriedman2002} added a comparative mammalian dimension. They showed that mouse and human OR gene families have different evolutionary histories: the mouse genome has expanded particular subfamilies not found in humans, while humans have contracted others. They also annotated specific OR gene clusters in comparative detail, establishing that intraspecific copy-number variation and sequence exchange at subtelomeric OR loci are ongoing processes, not ancient fixed events.

**Gilad et al. (2004)** \cite{Gilad2004} provided the evolutionary explanation for accelerated OR pseudogenization in the hominid lineage. By comparing OR pseudogene fractions across primate clades, they demonstrated that the shift to full trichromatic color vision in Old World monkeys and apes coincided with — and likely facilitated — the relaxation of selection on olfactory acuity. Humans have the highest pseudogene fraction (~60%) of any primate studied. This "trade-off" hypothesis placed the OR4F pseudogenization gradient in an evolutionary context: different chromosome ends may have experienced different degrees of relaxed selection, leading to the arm-specific pseudogenization pattern now documented in the pangenome data.

**Linardopoulou et al. (2005)** \cite{Linardopoulou2005} established human subtelomeres broadly as hotspots of interchromosomal recombination and segmental duplication, with OR gene blocks figuring among the most dynamic transferred elements. Their analysis showed that half of known subtelomeric sequence formed recently through human-specific sequence transfers, with non-homologous end-joining being a major mechanistic contributor. This paper is essential background for the OR4F gradient: if chromosome ends exchange sequence at high rates, different exchange histories at different arms could readily produce the nine-fold pseudogenization gradient observed in the pangenome data.

**Niimura and Nei (2007)** \cite{Niimura2007} extended the evolutionary analysis to multiple mammalian genomes, documenting the birth-and-death evolution of OR gene families across 13 species. They confirmed that rodents experienced gene gains while primates experienced losses, and that the dynamics are dominated by gene duplication followed by pseudogenization rather than by simple deletion of large genomic blocks. This provides important context for the subtelomeric OR clusters: the pseudogenization gradient seen at human chromosome ends reflects the endpoint of active birth-and-death turnover within each subtelomeric community.

**Hasin et al. (2008)** \cite{Hasin2008} provided the first high-resolution copy-number variation (CNV) map for the entire human OR gene locus set using oligonucleotide tiling microarrays. Of 851 OR loci interrogated, 244 (28%) showed CNVs, with an enrichment of losses over gains — consistent with the ongoing loss of OR gene function observed in the human lineage. Notably, subtelomeric OR loci were among those most frequently involved in CNVs, directly linking the dynamic subtelomere biology to OR gene dosage variation.

**Mainland et al. (2014)** \cite{Mainland2014} added the functional dimension. Using heterologous expression assays, they identified agonists for 18 human odorant receptors and found that 63% of those tested carry polymorphisms that alter receptor function in vitro. On average, two individuals differ at more than 30% of their odorant receptor alleles, making human olfactory perception extraordinarily diverse — in large part because the OR gene family is both polymorphic in copy number and segregating for many functional variants. This paper is relevant context for why OR pseudogenization gradients matter beyond comparative genomics: the functional consequence of pseudogenization at one chromosome end is invisible when a functional copy persists at another arm within the same Leiden community.

## Findings most relevant to claim C5 of the abstract

Claim C5 in the current abstract summarizes the cladistic structure recovered from the pangenome similarity graph: "Neighbor-joining trees recover known relationships (Xp/Yp, Xq/Yq, acrocentric short arms) and reveal novel cladistic structure: a strong 10p–18p clade, a tightly linked {22q, 21q, 19q, 1q, 13q, 17q} clade, and 4q–10q DUX4 homology with wide copy-number diversity." The OR4F gradient supports C5 as a molecular tracer of the same community structure that the Leiden and UPGMA clustering algorithms recover from the raw sequence-similarity matrix.

The direct evidence comes from the pangenome annotation and gene enrichment analysis (chapters 03 and 12 of the end-to-end report). Of 5,023 OR4F annotations across 16 chromosome arms, 62.1% are pseudogenes, but the distribution is highly non-uniform: chr7p retains 88.9% intact OR4F genes, while chr15q is 99.8% pseudogenized \cite{Guarracino2025}. This gradient is not random; it maps systematically onto the Leiden community structure. Arms in the same Leiden community (sharing sequence with multiple other arms at high identity) tend to share similar pseudogenization profiles, while arms in isolated or poorly-exchanging communities show divergent profiles.

The mechanism is exchange-mediated concerted evolution. When two chromosome arms exchange subtelomeric sequence at high frequency, they tend toward sequence homogeneity — including homogeneity of their OR gene complement. Arms in active exchange communities may import functional OR copies from partner arms, partially buffering against pseudogenization. Arms in low-exchange communities accumulate pseudogenes without this buffering. The chr7p low pseudogenization rate is consistent with chr7p participating in an active exchange community (Leiden community C3, which also includes chr11p, chr19p, and chr16q — all with high OR4F intact gene fractions relative to more isolated arms). The chr15q high pseudogenization rate is consistent with chr15q being in a different community (Leiden community C8) with a different exchange history.

From the literature, the four most directly supporting papers for the C5 OR4F dimension are:
1. Trask et al. (1998) \cite{Trask1998} establishing that OR blocks are polymorphically duplicated at chromosome ends — the subtelomeric OR block is evidence of the community structure before we had the pangenome graph to quantify it.
2. Mefford et al. (2001) \cite{Mefford2001} showing that OR-containing blocks have undergone recent non-homologous chromosome end interactions — the molecular mechanism for the differential pseudogenization rates observed across arms.
3. Linardopoulou et al. (2005) \cite{Linardopoulou2005} documenting subtelomeric hotspot recombination that would drive gene conversion and pseudogene spread differentially across communities.
4. Young et al. (2002) \cite{YoungFriedman2002} comparing mouse and human OR gene families to show that differential exchange histories at orthologous chromosome ends produce divergent pseudogenization trajectories — the evolutionary parallel to the arm-specific gradient observed in the pangenome data.

What is contested in the literature is the directionality of pseudogenization: is it driven by neutral drift following relaxation of purifying selection (the Gilad trichromacy hypothesis) \cite{Gilad2004}, or by positive selection for other OR copies, or primarily by the genomic architecture of the subtelomeric exchange communities? The pangenome data does not resolve this, but it makes the architectural component visible at population scale for the first time: the gradient correlates with community membership, which itself reflects exchange history rather than selective pressure on individual gene copies.

## How this topic intersects adjacent topics

The OR4F pseudogenization gradient topic intersects several other topics in the lit-review fanout in ways that clarify the broader subtelomere biology of the present manuscript.

**Intersection with Topic 03 (pseudohomologous region concept).** The PHR concept directly underpins the OR4F gradient story. Trask et al. (1998) \cite{Trask1998} described OR gene blocks in the same framing as the PHR: large segments of DNA duplicated near chromosome ends, varying in copy number and location across individuals. The current manuscript extends this from describing OR blocks at a few chromosome ends to quantifying OR4F pseudogenization across all 16 arms with detectable OR4F content, using the pangenome-scale implicit graph as the substrate. The two-domain Flint/Mefford model (also covered in Topic 03) is relevant here: the OR4F gradient itself follows a distance-from-telomere pattern, with higher-identity OR copies concentrated in the distal domain of the PHR and more pseudogenized copies extending into the proximal domain. The HPRC v2 finding that 39/41 arms show a two-segment piecewise breakpoint structure applies to OR-containing arms as much as to PHRs in general.

**Intersection with Topic 11 (pedigree-based recombination detection).** The pedigree analysis (topic 11 in the fanout; chapter 14 in the end-to-end report) directly demonstrates ongoing inter-chromosomal exchange at subtelomeric loci. The OR4F gradient is a population-scale aggregate of the kind of exchange events that the pedigree detects in real time: 538 high-quality inter-chromosomal patches in the WashU T2T pedigree, 92% of which fall within Leiden community boundaries. If OR4F pseudogenes are exchanged between arms during these events, one expects that the pseudogenization profile of a recipient arm will shift toward that of its exchange partner. Over many generations, this produces the community-correlated pseudogenization gradient observed in the pangenome data. The pedigree evidence therefore provides the mechanistic link between the structural genomic observation (OR4F gradient) and the ongoing exchange hypothesis.

**Intersection with DUX4/4q–10q topic (referenced in C5).** The DUX4 copy-number diversity at 4q and 10q (abstract claim C5) and the OR4F pseudogenization gradient are both examples of gene family heterogeneity at subtelomeres that reflect different community exchange histories. Both DUX4 and OR4F gene families are concentrated at chromosome ends, both show arm-specific variation in copy number and pseudogenization, and both are anchored to specific Leiden communities (DUX4 → community C1; OR4F → community C8 and several others). The parallel illustrates a general principle: wherever the pangenome graph finds a subtelomeric exchange community, one expects to find gene families whose copy-number and functional states are shaped by exchange within that community. OR4F and DUX4 are the two clearest examples in the current manuscript.

**Intersection with acrocentric arm topics.** The acrocentric p-arms (chr13p, chr14p, chr15p, chr21p, chr22p) form Leiden community C7, characterized by complete sequence interchangeability (allele-vs-paralog reversal: 65.5% have paralog distance smaller than allelic distance). This community has been shaped by the obligate rDNA-adjacent recombination at acrocentric short arms, and it contains the chr15q subtelomere that shows the highest OR4F pseudogenization rate (99.8%). The near-complete pseudogenization on chr15q, which sits at the boundary between community C8 and the acrocentric C7 arm context, may reflect the particular exchange history of chr15q as a terminus that has experienced both acrocentric-arm and broader community exchange events. Understanding chr15q's position in the Leiden clustering — and the OR4F gradient as a biological tracer of that position — connects the acrocentric arm biology to the broader subtelomeric gene-family story.

## Open questions and recent developments

**What drives the gradient: purifying selection, drift, or exchange architecture?** The dominant hypothesis in the OR evolution literature through 2014 attributed human OR pseudogenization primarily to relaxed purifying selection following visual system improvements (the trichromacy trade-off, Gilad et al. 2004 \cite{Gilad2004}). This explains the lineage-wide elevation of pseudogene fractions relative to other mammals, but it does not explain the arm-by-arm gradient. The pangenome data opens a new hypothesis: the gradient reflects exchange architecture. Arms in active exchange communities receive functional gene copies from exchange partners; arms in isolated or low-exchange communities accumulate pseudogenes without such rescue. The challenge is that these two mechanisms are not mutually exclusive, and distinguishing them requires knowing the functional constraint history of individual OR4F copies at each arm — information not available from sequence data alone.

**The T2T and pangenome revolution in OR gene annotation.** The T2T-CHM13 assembly (Nurk et al. 2022) closed previously inaccessible terminal and subtelomeric regions, enabling improved annotation of OR gene clusters that were poorly represented in GRCh38. The HPRC v2 pangenome extends this to 465 near-complete assemblies, making it possible for the first time to detect OR4F pseudogenization at population scale across all chromosome arms simultaneously. Prior studies relied on individual reference genomes or targeted FISH/sequencing of specific chromosome ends. The arm-specific pseudogenization gradient reported in the present study (11.1%–99.8% across 16 arms) could not have been quantified without the T2T and pangenome infrastructure. This is an important point for the Discussion: the OR4F gradient is not a serendipitous observation but a predicted consequence of the community structure that the pangenome graph makes visible for the first time.

**OR gene-functional variation and community-mediated rescue.** Mainland et al. (2014) \cite{Mainland2014} and subsequent work (Keller et al. 2007 \cite{Keller2007}) established that OR gene polymorphisms — including loss-of-function variants — alter olfactory perception in measurable ways. If the community exchange process copies pseudogene-carrying sequences between chromosome arms within a community, it may not entirely eliminate functional copies of OR genes from the population; a functional copy on one arm can compensate for a pseudogenized copy on another arm in the same community. However, if exchange preferentially copies pseudogene-containing haplotype segments (as would happen if those segments are no longer constrained), functional copy numbers could decrease over time. The pangenome data does not yet address whether individuals with low-OR4F communities have detectably reduced olfactory repertoires, but this is a tractable question.

**Primate comparative genomics and the OR4F lineage specifically.** Most primate OR evolution studies (Gilad 2004; Niimura and Nei 2007, 2010; Niimura et al. 2014 \cite{Niimura2014}) focus on total OR family size and pseudogene fraction without resolving the subtelomeric OR4F cluster specifically. The OR4F subfamily is notable for its concentration at chromosome ends: unlike most OR subfamilies, which distribute across multiple chromosomal loci, OR4F genes are predominantly subtelomeric. This makes them sensitive tracers of subtelomeric exchange history. A direct comparison of OR4F pseudogenization rates across great apes — ideally using T2T-quality assemblies — would show whether the arm-specific gradient is human-specific or shared with Pan, Gorilla, and Pongo. Given that interchromosomal exchange at subtelomeres is a shared feature of primate biology, the gradient might be qualitatively conserved while being quantitatively diverged, providing a molecular-clock signal for community exchange rates across the great ape lineage.

**The OR4F cluster as a structural marker for the HPRC community.** Beyond its role in olfactory biology, the OR4F cluster has become an important structural marker for the pangenome analysis described in the present manuscript. Its distribution across 16 chromosome arms, its community-correlated pseudogenization rate, and its confirmed presence in the Leiden C8 community all make it a biological anchor for the C5 abstract claim. The challenge for the Discussion is to present the OR4F gradient as a consequence of the community exchange process — not a cause — while making clear that the gradient is itself a novel, population-scale quantification of a phenomenon that earlier cytogenetic and sequencing studies could only infer from individual reference genomes or targeted experimental approaches. The transition from anecdote to population-scale gradient is the key conceptual contribution of the HPRC v2 analysis for this particular gene family.

**Copy-number variation and the OR4F gradient in diverse human populations.** Young et al. (2008) \cite{Hasin2008} showed that OR loci are enriched for CNVs, with loss alleles predominantly human-derived. The HPRC v2 dataset, with its population diversity (232 individuals, all five continental superpopulations), enables the first population-stratified analysis of OR4F copy number and pseudogenization at the arm level. Whether the 11.1%–99.8% pseudogenization gradient is consistent across populations, or whether certain superpopulations have different arm-specific pseudogenization profiles, remains unknown. Given that the broader subtelomeric community structure shows population-stratified differences in cross-arm affinity (chapter 04 of the end-to-end report; Hudson Fst 0.10–0.15 for AFR vs. non-AFR), population-specific OR4F pseudogenization rates at certain arms are plausible — and would represent a direct functional consequence of population differences in subtelomeric exchange history.
