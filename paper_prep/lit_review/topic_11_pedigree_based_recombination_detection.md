# Topic 11: Pedigree-based variant and recombination detection

This review anchors the abstract's pedigree claim: "A four-generation pedigree provides direct evidence of ongoing exchange: 538 patches of recent recombination, 92% confined within sequence-similarity communities." It is not a manuscript section. It is a chronological field map for explaining why pedigree-resolved T2T assemblies turn the C8 thesis from inference into observed transmission evidence, and how the methods distinguish inherited haplotypes, germline de novo events, postzygotic or somatic mutations, and assembly artefacts.

## 2010-2020: pedigree maps defined the language, but not the substrate

The predecessor era established the logic that still governs the present subtelomere analysis: recombination and mutation are most cleanly interpreted when alleles can be tracked through real families. deCODE's fine-scale pedigree maps showed that recombination rates differ by sex, population and individual, and that male recombination is comparatively enriched near telomeres [@Kong2010; @Bherer2017]. Halldorsson and colleagues then linked crossovers and de novo mutations with a sequence-level genetic map, establishing a template for asking whether recombination itself leaves local mutational signatures [@Halldorsson2019]. Large Utah three-generation families extended this into de novo mutation timing: variants observed in children can be assigned to parental germlines, postzygotic events, or mosaic ancestors when grandchildren are available [@Sasani2019].

These studies are the methodological ancestors of the current pedigree analysis, but they depended mostly on short-read SNV or SNP-array information. They could map crossovers and phase small variants, yet the substrates most relevant to human subtelomeres were either absent or unreliable: acrocentric short arms, duplicated subtelomeric blocks, large tandem arrays, and many interchromosomal paralogs. That matters for claims C4-C5 because the present paper's signal is not a conventional allelic crossover map. It is a map of large, recent interchromosomal patches in sequences that are precisely where short-read alignment and reference bias are worst.

The same period also made clear why structural variation needed assembly-level methods. Segmental duplications and complex SVs are frequent sources of human variation, and long-read or multi-platform studies found many variants that short-read reference mapping missed [@Chaisson2019; @Audano2019; @Collins2020]. HGSVC haplotype-resolved assemblies showed that long reads plus strand-specific or linked long-range information could recover diverse structural alleles without depending on a trio [@Ebert2021]. In claim terms, these papers are not direct evidence for C8, but they justify the C2-C4 premise: if the field wants to study subtelomeric exchange rather than only infer its historical residue, it needs chromosome-scale, haplotype-resolved sequence.

## 2020-2023: the T2T and pangenome transition made subtelomeric pedigrees plausible

The T2T-CHM13 reference changed the attainable search space by adding previously missing centromeric, acrocentric, and subtelomeric sequence to a contiguous human assembly [@Nurk2022]. HPRC then moved the field from one complete reference toward many high-quality haplotypes; the first draft pangenome assembled 47 diverse individuals and showed that pangenome-aware analysis reduced small-variant errors and increased SV detection compared with GRCh38-centered workflows [@Liao2023]. The HPRC v2 substrate used in the abstract scales this logic to 466 near-complete haplotype assemblies, which is the foundation for C1-C4.

Assembly methods are part of the claim, not just implementation detail. hifiasm made PacBio HiFi haplotype-resolved assembly routine enough to support population-scale work [@Cheng2021]. Verkko added a diploid T2T assembly strategy that explicitly integrates HiFi and ultra-long ONT reads [@Rautiainen2023]. Diploid assembly and trio binning methods established how parental k-mers, Hi-C, Strand-seq or related signals can assign contigs to haplotypes and expose switch errors [@Cheng2021; @Rautiainen2023]. For a subtelomere paper, this is the bridge between C2's implicit pangenome graph and C8's direct evidence: an interchromosomal patch is only persuasive if the surrounding haplotype is phased, inherited from the expected parent, and not a collapsed paralog or assembly switch.

GIAB and benchmarking work sharpened the same point from another angle. Challenging small-variant benchmarks that incorporated linked and long reads expanded confidence into difficult regions, while SV benchmarks emphasized that truth sets depend on orthogonal technologies and manual or assembly-supported curation [@Wagner2022; @Zook2020]. These resources trained the field to ask whether a call is robust across sequencing technologies, assembly methods, and inheritance. The present analysis applies that standard explicitly: WashU T2T is treated as primary; CEPH1463 hifiasm/verkko is useful only where a parent-by-chromosome-pair feature is reproduced by both assemblers.

Recent long-read SV benchmarking adds a practical warning for C8. Reviews and head-to-head comparisons agree that no single long-read caller or assembly pipeline dominates across variant classes: assembly-based approaches are strong for large insertions and resolving difficult alleles, while alignment-based approaches can retain advantages for genotyping and some complex rearrangements [@Ahsan2023; @Liu2024]. For pedigree-based subtelomere claims, this means the evidentiary unit cannot be just "one caller found a foreign segment." The stronger unit is a transmitted haplotype segment that survives orthogonal checks: expected Mendelian phase in the surrounding background, plausible allele balance or assembly support, community-consistent donor and recipient arms, and, when possible, reproduction across independent assemblers or generations.

## 2024-2025: multigenerational T2T turns de novo and recombination calls into transmission objects

The CEPH1463 work by Porubsky and colleagues is the clearest recent predecessor and companion to this topic [@Porubsky2025]. The published Nature study reports a 28-member, four-generation family sequenced with PacBio HiFi, ultra-long ONT, Strand-seq, Illumina, and Element data, assembled with both Verkko and hifiasm pipelines [@Porubsky2025]. This created a multigenerational framework in which variants can be tracked backward to parental haplotypes and forward to offspring. The study identified Mendelian-consistent SNVs, indels and SVs, built a high-resolution recombination map, and explicitly separated germline de novo SNVs from postzygotic mutations by haplotype linkage, allele balance and transmission.

For the present subtelomere work, two methodological lessons are crucial. First, "de novo" is not just "not in the parents." A true germline event should sit on a transmitted parental haplotype with near-heterozygous balance and, where possible, transmit to the next generation. A postzygotic or somatic event can have lower allele balance, incomplete linkage to a parental haplotype, or inconsistent forward transmission. This distinction is central to Q&A around the abstract's "ongoing exchange" wording. The WashU and CEPH analyses detect recent interchromosomal patches in inherited assemblies; they are direct evidence of exchange in the broad C8 sense, but each patch class should be discussed carefully: gene-conversion-like and crossover-like patterns are mechanistic hypotheses from the local untangle context, whereas "acros_like" marks dense NAHR-like traffic in acrocentric communities.

Second, multigenerational assembly exposes reference bias in recombination mapping itself. Porubsky et al. report that direct parent-child genome comparison refines most breakpoints to kilobase scale and that some breakpoint intervals grow when forced through T2T-CHM13, a reminder that the best reference is still not the family's own transmitted haplotypes [@Porubsky2025]. That point directly supports the present paper's use of `odgi untangle` against parental assemblies rather than only projecting every event through a single reference. It also supports C2's reference-free framing: the implicit pangenome graph is not cosmetic; it avoids chromosomal partitioning and lets subtelomeric homology define the neighborhood in which inheritance should be tested.

The WashU three-generation T2T pedigree from Cechova and colleagues supplies the primary direct evidence used in the abstract [@Cechova2025]. Public descriptions of the work emphasize T2T reference genomes for four admixed African American individuals across three generations and parent-of-origin assigned chromosome-level assemblies spanning regions usually omitted from WGS, including telomeres, acrocentric p-arms and centromeres. In the project analysis, PAN010/PAN011 to PAN027 and PAN027 to PAN028 transmissions yield 538 high-quality interchromosomal subtelomeric patches, of which 494, or 92%, fall within the Leiden arm communities derived independently from unrelated HPRC v2 haplotypes. That cross-generation result is the direct C8 anchor because it validates the C5 community structure as a predictor of observed family-level exchange rather than only a population-clustering artifact.

The internal classification also matters for interpreting mechanism. The WashU set contains 133 gene-conversion-like patches, 16 crossover-like patches, 229 acrocentric-like patches, 115 same-haplotype sandwich patterns, and one complex event. The 133 gene-conversion-like events are especially useful for explaining "ongoing exchange" because they have the local signature of a foreign interchromosomal tract embedded within the expected inherited background. The 16 crossover-like events are rarer but valuable because their flanking parental-haplotype switch resembles meiotic exchange at a breakpoint. The acrocentric-like class is different: it signals extensive traffic among highly homologous short arms, not a single clean crossover. The rhetorical discipline is to present these as tiers of evidence, not as one homogeneous event type.

CEPH1463 is best used as replication and robustness control. Because those assemblies are fragmented relative to WashU T2T, raw within-community rates are low in both hifiasm and verkko outputs. The project therefore reports only 11 parent features that replicate across both assemblers. These include the chr10/chr18 Linardopoulou pair in both NA12877 and NA12878, chr19/chr22 and chr21/chr22 features, and chr6/chr9 or chr12/chr9 features in C5/C6/C7 communities. This is not as clean as the WashU evidence, but it is important for C5-C8: the same named subtelomeric communities reappear under an independent family design and independent assembly pipelines.

## How the field's methods distinguish inherited, de novo, meiotic and somatic signals

Pedigree-resolved subtelomere analysis sits at the intersection of four separations. The first is inherited versus de novo. In standard trio calling, absence from both parents defines a candidate de novo variant, but high-confidence calls require sequencing support, phasing, genotyping in all family members, and Mendelian consistency checks [@Ahsan2023; @Liu2024]. In assembly-based pedigree work, the cleaner question is often whether a child's segment is best explained by an expected parental haplotype or by a tract copied from a different parental chromosome within the same similarity community. That is the logic behind the WashU patch calls.

The second separation is meiotic versus postzygotic or somatic. Porubsky et al. show how allele balance, haplotype linkage and forward transmission can classify small DNMs and postzygotic mutations [@Porubsky2025]. The present subtelomere work does not claim to have sampled meiocytes directly in humans. Instead, it combines inherited patch structure with 3D and mouse meiotic evidence: bulk and single-cell 3D contacts support C7, mouse zygotene Hi-C supplies meiotic timing support, and the pedigree proves recent transmission. The conservative statement is that pedigree patches are inherited products consistent with meiotic exchange; somatic LCL artefact is controlled indirectly by T2T assembly quality, inheritance structure and community validation, but not eliminated by a human meiotic assay.

The third separation is allelic recombination versus ectopic or interchromosomal exchange. Classical pedigree maps count crossovers between homologues. The abstract's unorthodox recombination claim concerns patches whose source is another chromosome arm. That makes existing recombination maps useful background but insufficient tests: short-read T2T-CHM13 recombination rates at acrocentric and PAR arms can be confounded by low callability. The project already notes the honest null that recombination-rate correlations vanish after excluding low-callability arms [@Lalli2025]. The pedigree patch analysis is stronger because it observes the transmitted sequence structure directly.

The fourth separation is biology versus assembly artefact. Recent assembly literature teaches three safeguards: use orthogonal data, compare independent assembly methods, and validate inheritance across generations [@Ebert2021; @Rautiainen2023; @Porubsky2025]. The present analysis follows those principles in a subtelomere-specific way. WashU T2T provides the primary high-contiguity evidence; CEPH1463 contributes only cross-assembler-validated features; every reported patch is tested against HPRC-derived communities rather than accepted solely because an aligner placed it on a different chromosome.

## Claim-anchored synthesis

For C1-C3, pedigree work is the independent validation layer on top of HPRC v2: the pangenome survey creates the community model, and families test whether that model predicts inheritance. For C4-C6, pedigree patches show that extended subtelomeric homology and population communities are not merely historical duplications; they are channels along which recent sequence tracts move. For C7, pedigree evidence complements the 3D proximity analyses by adding the transmitted outcome that physical proximity alone cannot prove. For C8, the strongest wording is: recent interchromosomal subtelomeric exchange is directly observed in multigenerational T2T pedigrees, and 92% of high-quality WashU patches fall inside independently defined sequence-similarity communities. That is the methodological reason the abstract can say "direct evidence of ongoing exchange" rather than only "consistent with ongoing exchange."

The remaining limitation is also clear. Current data distinguish inherited subtelomeric patch structure far better than they distinguish the exact cellular moment at which every patch arose. A human meiotic long-read or single-cell experiment, or more T2T multigenerational families with matched source-material metadata, would sharpen the meiotic-versus-somatic boundary. Until then, the defensible field-level conclusion is that recent, transmitted interchromosomal subtelomeric patches are observable in pedigrees; their distribution is predicted by HPRC-derived sequence communities; and this closes the evidence chain from sequence homology to 3D proximity to ongoing exchange.
