# Concerted evolution and unorthodox recombination of human subtelomeres

**Andrea Guarracino, Angela Gyamfi, Erik Garrison**

Human subtelomeric regions are among the most dynamic and structurally complex parts of the genome, yet their interchromosomal relationships have remained difficult to characterize due to the limitations of both assembly completeness and alignment methodology. Here we present the most comprehensive survey of subtelomeric sequence relationships to date, leveraging 465 near-complete assemblies (232 HPRC v2 individuals and CHM13). To analyze these regions, we introduce the implicit pangenome graph, a reference-free alignment approach that performs all-to-all pairwise comparisons across haplotypes, sampling approximately 12% of all possible combinations, without imposing chromosomal partitioning or positional bias. This yields an unbiased view of interchromosomal homology across the pangenome, where every haplotype serves as its own point of reference, allowing a systematic view of human subtelomeric evolution.

A genome-wide survey of alignment identity reveals extended regions of interchromosomal homology spanning tens to hundreds of kilobases at nearly all subtelomeres, comparable in scale to canonical pseudo-homologous systems such as PAR2 on the sex chromosomes. This expands the scope of known pseudo-homolog regions (PHRs) in the human genome to include almost all subtelomeric regions. Cladistic and community analyses of subtelomeric similarity recover expected relationships, including Xp/Yp and Xq/Yq via the pseudoautosomal regions and the acrocentric short arms, and reveal additional associations, including strong 10p-18p homology, a tightly linked clade involving 22q, 21q, 19q, 1q, 13q and 17q, and extended DUX4-containing homology between 4q and 10q with wide copy-number diversity. We hypothesize that these patterns are maintained by recombination facilitated by the physical proximity of subtelomeres at the nuclear envelope. Human Pore-C and Hi-C support this model by showing that sequence-similar PHRs contact one another more often, and mouse meiotic Hi-C shows that this relationship peaks at zygotene, when telomeres cluster in the bouquet. A telomere-to-telomere human pedigree further suggests recent interchromosomal exchange preferentially within sequence-similarity communities. Our work exposes the extent to which ongoing recombination shapes these highly dynamic and poorly understood regions of the genome.
