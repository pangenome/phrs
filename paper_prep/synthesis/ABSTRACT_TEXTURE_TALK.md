# Talk-faithful abstract texture probe

## 1. Final candidate abstract

Human subtelomeres are dynamic chromosome ends where duplicated sequence, disease-associated rearrangements, and known pseudoautosomal or acrocentric exchanges point to ongoing interchromosomal sharing, but the full scale of that sharing has been hard to see because chromosome ends were incomplete and graph analyses usually kept chromosomes apart. Here we ask how far these relationships extend across the human pangenome by treating every haplotype as its own reference: across 466 near-complete assemblies, an implicit pangenome graph lets us query all-to-all subtelomeric alignments without imposing chromosome or position labels. We find pseudo-homolog regions (PHRs) on 41 of 48 chromosome arms, spanning tens to hundreds of kilobases and totaling 3.51 Mb outside the acrocentric short arms and pseudoautosomal regions. When we compare chromosome ends by the sequence they share, the PHR graph resolves into 15 sequence-similarity communities that recover the expected Xp/Yp, Xq/Yq, acrocentric, 10p-18p, and 4q-10q DUX4 systems, and also reveal a linked q-arm group involving 22q, 21q, 19q, 1q, 13q, and 17q. We then test whether this sequence structure is mirrored in three dimensions, and observe that sequence-similar PHRs contact one another more often in human Pore-C and Hi-C, while mouse meiotic Hi-C shows the relationship peaking at zygotene, when telomeres cluster in the bouquet. Finally, in a telomere-to-telomere human pedigree, high-quality interchromosomal patches fall preferentially within the same sequence communities, providing positive but not definitive evidence for recent exchange between non-homologous chromosome ends. Together, these results show that subtelomeric PHRs are a near-ubiquitous feature of human chromosome ends and support a model in which nuclear proximity and ongoing recombination help maintain their concerted evolution.

Single paragraph retained because the active abstract and Nature target are single-paragraph abstracts, and the talk arc can be followed without breaking the venue-style compression.

## 2. Sentence-to-talk-arc map

1. Biological motivation first: starts from subtelomere dynamism, known exchange systems, and the technical reason the full pattern was previously hard to see, matching the talk's move from acrocentric graph behavior to the need to put all genomes together.
2. Method as enabling move: introduces the question and the implicit pangenome graph only after the biological problem, using "ask" and "lets us query" rather than making the algorithm the subject.
3. Scale: gives the PHR extent result in the talk order, with the 41 of 48 arms, tens-to-hundreds-of-kilobases scale, and 3.51 Mb outside acrocentrics and PARs.
4. Structure: moves from scale to graph/community organization, pairing known positives with new community structure and avoiding a phylogenetic claim.
5. 3D proximity: follows the bouquet setup by asking whether sequence structure is mirrored in 3D, then separates human somatic evidence from mouse meiotic timing.
6. Pedigree evidence: closes the evidence chain with the pedigree, using "preferentially," "positive but not definitive," and "recent exchange" rather than claiming direct proof of human germline recombination.
7. Conclusion: keeps the compact talk conclusion: near ubiquity, model support, nuclear proximity, ongoing recombination, and concerted evolution, while retaining model-level caution.

## 3. Texture rules implied for manuscript fan-out

1. Start each major result from the biological question it answers, then name the technical move that made the answer possible.
2. Use "we ask," "we find," "we compare," "we test," "we observe," and "suggests" as the default evidence verbs; reserve stronger causal verbs for directly measured events.
3. Preserve the talk order at paragraph scale: motivation, method, PHR scale, community structure, 3D proximity, pedigree evidence, then qualified model.
4. Pair expected systems with new findings whenever possible, so validation and discovery sit in the same sentence or paragraph rather than in separate narrative tracks.
5. Let caveats sit beside the claims they qualify, then restate the defensible take-home in plain language.
