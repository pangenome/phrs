# Concerted evolution and unorthodox recombination of human subtelomeres

**Andrea Guarracino, Angela Gyamfi, Erik Garrison**

**Target:** Nature (companion to HPRC v2 main paper)

---

Human subtelomeric regions are among the most dynamic and structurally complex parts of the genome, but their interchromosomal relationships have remained difficult to characterize because chromosome ends were incompletely assembled and alignment methods usually partition homologous chromosomes. Here we survey subtelomeric sequence relationships across 466 near-complete assemblies (465 HPRC v2 haplotypes together with CHM13) using an implicit pangenome graph, a reference-free all-to-all alignment framework that samples about 12% of possible haplotype-pair comparisons and queries their transitive closure without imposing chromosomal or positional priors. We find extended interchromosomal homology at 41 of 48 chromosome arms, spanning tens to hundreds of kilobases and totalling 3.51 Mb outside the acrocentric short arms and pseudoautosomal regions. These pseudo-homolog regions form 15 sequence-similarity communities that recover known subtelomeric systems, including Xp/Yp, Xq/Yq, the acrocentric short arms, 10p-18p and the 4q-10q DUX4 locus, and reveal a tightly linked q-arm group involving 22q, 21q, 19q, 1q, 13q and 17q. Human Pore-C and Hi-C show that sequence-similar pseudo-homolog regions contact one another more often in three dimensions, and a mouse meiotic Hi-C reanalysis shows that this relationship peaks at zygotene, when telomeres cluster in the bouquet. A telomere-to-telomere human pedigree further suggests recent interchromosomal exchange preferentially within these communities. These results show that pseudo-homologous subtelomeric regions are a near-ubiquitous feature of human chromosome ends and support a model in which nuclear architecture and ongoing recombination maintain their concerted evolution.
