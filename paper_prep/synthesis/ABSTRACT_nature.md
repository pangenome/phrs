# Concerted evolution and unorthodox recombination of human subtelomeres

**Andrea Guarracino and Erik Garrison**

**Target:** Nature (companion to HPRC v2 main paper)

---

Human subtelomeres are among the most dynamic and structurally complex regions of the genome, yet their interchromosomal relationships have resisted systematic analysis. Using 466 near-complete haplotype assemblies from the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium (HPRC) v2, we construct an implicit pangenome graph — a reference-free, all-to-all alignment sampling roughly 12% of pairwise haplotype combinations without chromosomal partitioning. We find extended interchromosomal homology spanning tens to hundreds of kilobases at nearly all subtelomeres, comparable in scale to the canonical PAR2. Neighbor-joining trees recover known relationships (Xp/Yp, Xq/Yq, acrocentric short arms) and reveal novel cladistic structure: a strong 10p–18p clade, a tightly linked {22q, 21q, 19q, 1q, 13q, 17q} clade, and 4q–10q DUX4 homology with wide copy-number diversity. Population-genetic analyses resolve subtelomeric clustering across human ancestries. Bulk and single-cell Hi-C in GM12878 and sperm, together with mouse meiotic Hi-C peaking at zygotene (Mantel ρ=0.715), tie sequence homology to nuclear-envelope proximity through the meiotic bouquet. A four-generation pedigree provides direct evidence of ongoing exchange: 538 patches of recent recombination, 92% confined within sequence-similarity communities. These data establish that human subtelomeres are unified by ongoing recombination and concerted evolution.
