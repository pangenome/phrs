# Biology-first abstract texture probe

## Final candidate abstract

Human subtelomeres bring repeated, rapidly changing DNA to the physical ends of nearly every chromosome, creating an unusual setting in which sequence homology, nuclear position and recombination opportunity can reinforce one another across non-homologous chromosomes. How far this sharing extends, and whether it remains an active force in human chromosome-end evolution, has been difficult to measure because subtelomeres were incompletely assembled and chromosome-by-chromosome alignments obscure relationships between arms. Here we use 465 near-complete assemblies (232 HPRC v2 individuals and CHM13) and an implicit pangenome graph, a reference-free all-to-all alignment framework that samples about 12% of possible haplotype-pair comparisons and queries their transitive closure without chromosomal or positional priors, to survey subtelomeric sequence relationships genome-wide. We find extended interchromosomal homology at 41 of 48 chromosome arms, spanning tens to hundreds of kilobases and totaling 3.51 Mb outside the acrocentric short arms and pseudoautosomal regions. These pseudo-homolog regions form 15 sequence-similarity communities that recover known subtelomeric systems, including Xp/Yp, Xq/Yq, the acrocentric short arms, 10p/18p and the 4q/10q DUX4 locus, and reveal a linked q-arm group involving 22q, 21q, 19q, 1q, 13q and 17q. Human Pore-C and Hi-C show that sequence-similar pseudo-homolog regions contact one another more often in three dimensions, while mouse meiotic Hi-C shows that this relationship is strongest at zygotene, when telomeres cluster in the bouquet. A telomere-to-telomere human pedigree provides direct evidence consistent with recent exchange preferentially within these sequence communities, while remaining short of definitive proof for individual germline events. Together, these results identify pseudo-homologous subtelomeric regions as a near-ubiquitous feature of human chromosome ends and support a model in which nuclear architecture and recurrent recombination opportunity contribute to their concerted evolution.

## Rationale for the biology-first shift

- Opens with the biological setting of subtelomeres as chromosome-end DNA where homology, nuclear position and recombination opportunity can interact, rather than opening with assembly or graph methodology.
- Keeps the implicit pangenome graph intelligible and central enough to explain how the measurement was possible, but places it after the unresolved biological question.
- Preserves the committed baseline facts: 465 near-complete assemblies; 232 HPRC v2 individuals plus CHM13; approximately 12% sampled comparisons; 41 of 48 arms; tens to hundreds of kilobases; 3.51 Mb outside acrocentric short arms and PARs; 15 sequence-similarity communities; known X/Y, acrocentric, 10p/18p and 4q/10q systems; the 22q/21q/19q/1q/13q/17q q-arm group; human Pore-C/Hi-C; mouse zygotene bouquet; and pedigree evidence.
- Shifts the emotional center from "we can build/query this graph" to "chromosome ends may share a population-scale substrate for concerted evolution," without adding functional claims about gene regulation, phenotype or adaptive consequence.
- Softens the baseline's risky pedigree phrasing by saying the pedigree gives direct evidence consistent with recent exchange and is not definitive proof for individual germline events, matching the body-language caution in the voice plan and talk.

## Language choices to propagate into the Introduction and final synthesis

- "recombination opportunity" for the biological opening created by telomere clustering and shared homology, reserving mechanistic causality for the model.
- "sequence-similarity communities" as the default term for arm groupings, with "linked q-arm group" rather than unqualified clade or phylogeny.
- "sequence homology, nuclear position and recombination opportunity can reinforce one another" as a model-level synthesis, not a proven causal loop.
- "contact one another more often in three dimensions" for the human Pore-C/Hi-C result, avoiding stronger language that implies a mechanism has been directly observed.
- "direct evidence consistent with recent exchange" and "short of definitive proof for individual germline events" for the pedigree result.

## Claim-strength audit

- Pedigree: The candidate treats the telomere-to-telomere pedigree as direct evidence consistent with recent exchange within sequence communities, but explicitly avoids claiming definitive direct human germline recombination for each patch. This preserves the "positive but not definitive" caution from the manuscript body and talk.
- 3D evidence: The candidate separates human somatic 3D contact evidence from mouse meiotic-stage evidence. Human Pore-C and Hi-C show increased contact among sequence-similar PHRs; mouse meiotic Hi-C shows the association peaks at zygotene. It does not claim that human meiotic contacts were measured directly.
- Mechanism: The candidate frames nuclear architecture plus recurrent recombination opportunity as a supported model for concerted evolution, not as a demonstrated causal mechanism. It avoids saying proximity causes homology, homology causes proximity or ongoing recombination definitively maintains every PHR.
