### C1 — HPRC v2 companion-paper framing

### C2 — Implicit pangenome graph (~12% pairwise sampling, no chromosomal partitioning)

Andrea's chapter 01 §"All-vs-all subtelomeric alignment" describes the pipeline:

> "wfmash v0.23.0-41-gb5f0ff1c (`-p 95 -t 48 --quiet`): each of the 18,827 sequences serves as target in turn, with all 18,827 sequences as queries."

And §"Pangenome graph construction and pairwise similarity":

> "`pggb -p 95 -D /scratch` constructs the pangenome graph from the 15,668 PHR sequences. `odgi similarity --all -P` then computes the Jaccard similarity for every pair of sequences."

**Framing difference**. Andrea's prose treats this as a pggb-pangenome-graph + odgi-similarity pipeline. Per the lead-author clarification and the abstract, the canonical framing should be:

- The all-vs-all PAF set IS the implicit pangenome graph (each PAF edge is a pairwise mapping).
- IMPG (https://github.com/pangenome/impg, ~/impg) is the canonical tool. Transitive closure via `impg query -x` is the operation on the implicit graph.
- Methods should cite IMPG; it does not need to invent the "implicit pangenome graph" concept.

The "**~12% pairwise sampling**" wording in the abstract is **not** in Andrea's chapter — and Andrea's pipeline is described as all-to-all rather than 12%-sampled. This is a real framing gap. Per Erik's clarification, the 12% figure is the wfmash k-mer-sparsified evaluation rate (fraction of pairs that pass k-mer prefiltering), and the Erdős-Rényi connectivity argument (n≈18,827 → p* = log(n)/n ≈ 5.2e-4; 12% is ~230× above that, so the random graph is densely connected and transitive closure reaches everywhere) belongs in Methods. **The Methods writer must derive the 12% number from the on-disk PAFs and supply the ER paragraph; Andrea's report does not.**

## 7. Methodological clarifications (for Methods writers)

Six short paragraphs synthesising what Methods should claim about the topics the brief lists.

### 7a. The implicit pangenome graph (cite IMPG)

The implicit pangenome graph is the all-vs-all PAF set produced by wfmash (`-p 95`) over the 18,827 telomere-anchored 500 kb flanks. Each PAF edge is a pairwise mapping at ≥95% identity; the *implicit* graph is the union of these edges over flanks. IMPG (https://github.com/pangenome/impg, Garrison et al.) provides the canonical query interface: `impg query -x` performs transitive closure on the implicit graph, returning every region reachable from a query interval through chains of overlapping pairwise mappings. The Methods section should cite IMPG and treat the all-vs-all PAFs as the graph; it does not need to invent the "implicit pangenome graph" concept. The constructed pggb GFA (chapter 01 §"Pangenome graph construction and pairwise similarity") and its odgi-derived pairwise Jaccard matrix are *one downstream product* of the implicit graph — used for the similarity / community-detection analyses — but they are not themselves the implicit graph.

### 7b. The ~12% sampling figure and Erdős-Rényi connectivity

For n = 18,827 telomere-anchored flanks, the all-vs-all comparison space has C(n, 2) ≈ 1.77 × 10⁸ pairs. wfmash's k-mer prefiltering evaluates a sparse subset of these pairs at full alignment cost — the realised evaluation rate from the on-disk PAFs is ~12%. The Erdős-Rényi connectivity threshold for a random graph G(n, p) on n nodes to be w.h.p. connected is p* = log(n) / n. For n = 18,827, p* = log(18,827) / 18,827 ≈ 9.84 / 18,827 ≈ 5.23 × 10⁻⁴. The realised 12% sampling rate is therefore ~230× above the connectivity threshold, so the resulting random sub-graph is densely connected w.h.p., and IMPG's transitive closure from any subtelomere reaches virtually every other subtelomere through chains of overlapping pairwise mappings. This justifies "no chromosomal partitioning" as a methodological choice — the sampling does not create a reachability gap. One paragraph in Methods. The Methods writer must derive the 12% number from the on-disk PAF set rather than asserting it.

### 7e. Community detection: Leiden k=15 (arm) and k=50 (sequence)

**Arm-level**: Leiden community detection on the 41×41 arm-level Jaccard distance matrix (computed by averaging pairwise sequence-level Jaccard distances per arm pair). Edge weights via exponential decay w_ij = exp(-d_ij / median(d)). Resolution scanned 0.1–3.0 in steps of 0.01; selected resolution = 1.16 maximises mean silhouette (0.347). Output: 15 communities (chapter 01 §"Arm-level community detection"). UPGMA comparison cut at k=14 (silhouette 0.342). **Sequence-level**: Leiden on the full 15,668 × 15,668 pairwise Jaccard distance matrix; k-NN graph (joint scan over k ∈ {10, 25, 50, 75, 100, 125} and resolution 0.1–3.0); selected k=75, resolution=0.8 (modularity 0.97); 50 communities (silhouette 0.602). ARI (arm-level vs sequence-level partitions when arm-level is propagated to sequences) = 0.35; NMI = 0.76.

The audit's flagged "k=15 vs k=50" question is resolved as: **k=15 is the arm-level partition (15 communities of 41 arms); k=50 is the sequence-level partition (50 communities of 15,668 sequences)**. ### 7f. Hi-C analysis pipeline

**Inputs**. 6 Hi-C samples (5 diploid HPRC: HG002, HG02559, HG00658, HG02148, NA19036; 1 haploid: CHM13) at 5 mcool resolutions (5/10/20/50/100 kb), plus HG002 Pore-C and HG002 CiFi for cross-platform validation. PHR-specific coordinates from `all-vs-all.1Mb.p95.id95.len.tsv` (chapter 01). Per-haplotype mode keeps maternal and paternal as separate arms (75 arms for diploid samples). **Multi-mapping**. All four technologies disable MAPQ filters (`MIN_MAPQ = 0`, `RM_MULTI = 0` for Hi-C; no `--min-mapq` for Pore-C / CiFi); each multimapped read keeps exactly one randomly-chosen alignment, adding symmetric noise (chapter 05 §"Multi-mapping handling"). **Three statistical tests** (chapter 05 §"How"): (1) within/between (W/B) ratio with bootstrap permutation (10,000 perms); (2) global Mann-Whitney U test on within-vs-between contact distributions; (3) Mantel test (Spearman rank correlation between arm-level Jaccard similarity and arm-level Hi-C contact, 10,000 row/column permutations). **Headline numbers**: HG002 Hi-C B/W = 0.027 at 50 kb (p=4.0e-66); CHM13 B/W = 0.071 (p=6.0e-18); Mantel rho = 0.66 for both; per-arm-pair Spearman rho = 0.66 (chapter 05). **Exclusion controls** (chapter 05 §"Acrocentric exclusion confound control"): 5 exclusion sets (no acrocentric p / no sex / no acrocentric p+sex / no all-acrocentric+sex / no strongest = C1+C7+C14+C15) at all 5 resolutions; the Mantel rho **strengthens** for ALL 8 datasets at ALL resolutions when exclusions are applied (HG002 0.66→0.79; HG02148 0.15→0.72; CHM13 0.66→0.85). This is the cleanest available defence against confound-driven Hi-C signal. **O/E inter-chromosomal normalization** (chapter 05 §"O/E inter-chromosomal normalization"): after correcting for arm-marginal contact frequencies, within-community contacts are 8.6×–34.4× higher than between, ruling out chromosome-size effects. **Flanking control**: 100 kb regions immediately centromere-ward of PHR boundaries are unique sequence (no multi-mapping); their B/W is **stronger** than PHR B/W (HG002 0.002 vs 0.027 — 13× stronger). This rules out multi-mapping as the driver of the 3D signal and is the basis for the "flanking paradox" framing (chapter 07 §"Flanking region paradox", chapter 11 finding 9). **Open methodological gap**: the Methods does not yet include a direct LAD / Lamin B1 ChIP overlay supporting the abstract's "nuclear envelope" wording — chapter 05 uses Dip-C radial position as a proxy and chapter 07 cites the Masny 2004 + Ottaviani 2009 mechanism.
