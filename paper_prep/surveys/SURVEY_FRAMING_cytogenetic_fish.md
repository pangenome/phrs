### 2.3 Apparent contradictions, resolved

- **chr4_q ↔ chr10_q discordance: 43.4 % (this work) vs ~20 % (Mefford citing van Deutekom 1996 Southern blot).** Not a contradiction — the FISH/Southern probe targets a narrow segment; pangenome alignment captures the broader PHR (median 105 kb), and the wider catchment legitimately picks up more events.
- **f7501 at chr4_q and chr19_q: present by FISH (Mefford), absent by sequence alignment.** The L78442.1 cosmid produces zero ≥30 kb alignments to chr19_q (457 flanks tested) and only ~954 bp partial matches to chr4_q. **FISH cross-hybridisation extends below the asm20 sequence-identity floor**; these arms host older, more-divergent f7501-like copies that are real biology but invisible to alignment-based detection. The asymmetry is methodological and bidirectional — sequence reveals novel low-frequency loci that FISH missed (chr1_p, chr20_p, chrX_q); FISH retains older diverged paralogues sequence misses.
- **No tested FISH-era claim is overturned.** The 43.4 % vs 20 % is sensitivity, not biology; the f7501 chr4_q / chr19_q "absences" are sensitivity in the other direction. Both vindicate the FISH conclusions while showing why pangenome data are now the primary substrate.

## Part 3 — Framing draft (1–2 pages of introduction-flavoured prose)

### From cytogenetic banding to pangenome graphs: a continuous arc at the chromosome ends

The architecture of human subtelomeres has been investigated since the earliest days of molecular cytogenetics, and the present work is best understood as the latest layer in a four-decade investigation that has repeatedly seen *the same patterns* at progressively higher resolution.

**(a) What cytogenetic-era studies could see.** From the late 1970s onward, banding and *in situ* hybridisation gave the first hint that the most distal portion of every chromosome arm is special: G-banding renders chromosome tips as light bands of variable intensity, and chromosome-painting probes generated from flow-sorted chromosome libraries showed that single-chromosome paint signals "leak" onto the tips of non-homologous chromosomes. The Trask laboratory, building on Bing-Lan Trask's flow-sorted human chromosome library at LLNL, isolated a series of subtelomeric cosmids — most famously the 36 kb f7501 (GenBank L78442) anchored at chr15_q — and probed their distribution by FISH across panels of 50–100 individuals (Mefford & Trask 2002 reviewed the entire programme). The cytogenetic toolbox at that scale could ask three things: (i) *is the probe present on a given chromosome end?* (binary call from FISH cross-hybridisation), (ii) *is its prevalence different between populations?* (chr7p / chr16q "African pygmy" enrichment was the headline finding), and (iii) *is the chromosome-end heterozygous within an individual?* (Stout et al. 1999 used FISH to show interphase chr4q/chr10q pairing). These methods established, qualitatively, the picture summarised in Mefford & Trask's 2002 review: subtelomeres are a "patchwork" of duplicon modules, each module shared across several non-homologous chromosomes, with arm-specific frequencies that sometimes differ between populations.

**(b) What flow + FISH on a few samples revealed.** Three complementary single-genome programmes converted the qualitative picture into a structural model. Flint et al. 1997, by sequence-comparing the chr4p, chr16p, and chr22q tips, proposed a **two-domain model** — a distal domain whose blocks are shared across many chromosome ends, and a proximal domain whose blocks are shared with very few — separated by a degenerate (TTAGGG)n tract. Riethman, Ambrosini, and colleagues (Riethman et al. 2004; Ambrosini et al. 2007) sequenced and assembled the subtelomeric ends of the reference genome to produce a paralogue map of 11 *subtelomere-specific* duplicon blocks plus 6 *subterminal* duplicon families, organised the duplicons by sequence-identity into a bimodal distribution (peaks at 91 % and 98 %), and identified 6 chromosome ends whose distal-most sequence is unique in the genome. Linardopoulou et al. 2005 extended the map to 41 blocks across 33 subtelomeres and, in Stong et al. 2014, a haplotype-resolved subtelomeric assembly showed that "the 19p alleles … differ grossly in subtelomeric content". Across all of this work, the limiting factor was the same: **a single reference genome, augmented by FISH on tens of individuals.** The patterns were sharp; the population-scale frequencies were estimates from FISH; the long, near-identical exchanges seen in any one individual were genuinely difficult to *count*.

**(c) The HPRC pangenome shows the same patterns at scale.** The present work analyses 18,827 subtelomeric flanks (500 kb, telomere-trimmed) from 465 haplotype-resolved assemblies across 233 individuals from the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium. The 95 % identity threshold was deliberately set to capture **the high-identity peak of Ambrosini et al. 2007's bimodal duplicon distribution**, accepting that older exchanges (≤91 %) are missed but ensuring the captured sample is unambiguously orthologous. Across this dataset every cytogenetic-era pattern reappears, quantified: (i) Flint's two-domain model holds on 39 of 48 arms by Spearman gradient and on 39 of 41 testable arms by piecewise breakpoint analysis, with arm-specific transitions between 15 kb (chr22q) and 445 kb; (ii) Mefford & Trask's f7501 distribution is reproduced by direct alignment of the original L78442 cosmid to 465 haplotypes — chr3_q, chr15_q, chr19_p essentially fixed (86–92 %), chr7_p and chr16_q AFR-enriched at OR=3.7 and OR=17.4, plus three additional AFR-enriched arms (chr8_p, chr16_p, chr9_q) that fell below FISH detection; (iii) the Mefford "20 % chr4q/chr10q heterozygosity" estimate from Southern blot becomes a 43.4 % discordance rate in the broader PHR; (iv) Ambrosini's 11 subtelomere-specific duplicon block entries map cleanly onto 15 unbiased Leiden communities, and the 6 one-copy regions are confirmed except that chr7_q and chr12_q are revealed as a previously-undetected private pair (community C4); (v) Linardopoulou's chr19_p polymorphism is quantified — chr19_p sequences distribute across 7 sequence-level communities, second only to chr6_q; (vi) Stout et al. 1999's interphase chr4q/chr10q FISH pairing is mirrored by Hi-C contact enrichment within community C1.

**(d) What is therefore newly addressable.** Three classes of question that were structurally out of reach for FISH and Southern-blot now become tractable:
1. **Population-scale phylogeography of subtelomeric exchanges.** Mefford & Trask 2002 hedged that "measuring the frequency of specific subtelomeric blocks on particular chromosomes might inform phylogenetic studies of modern humans" but cautioned that exchange history could confound demography. With cross-arm affinity frequencies at 9 arms × 5 superpopulations, a population tree built from these frequencies recovers the out-of-Africa topology (AFR deepest split, AMR–EUR closest), and pairwise Fst (mean 0.044) shows the expected AFR/non-AFR gradient — i.e. subtelomeric exchange frequencies *do* carry phylogenetic signal, the hedge is largely resolved on the data side.
2. **Linking sequence communities to 3D nuclear architecture.** Stout et al. 1999 and Linardopoulou et al. 2005 anticipated that interphase clustering should accompany the exchanges. The pangenome makes this testable: across four 3D technologies (Hi-C, Pore-C, Dip-C, sperm 3D) and in mouse meiosis, the same community structure is reproduced as enriched within-community 3D contacts (B/W 0.027–0.074 in Hi-C; 60 % closer in sperm; mouse meiotic per-arm-pair Spearman ρ=0.715 at zygotene).
3. **A unified PHR / PAR view.** PARs were structurally identified by Rouyer et al. 1986 as the *one* exception to homologous chromosome inheritance — non-homologous tips that share sequence and undergo obligate crossover. The present work shows that PAR1 (C15) and PAR2 (C14) are simply two of fifteen exchange communities; the genome-wide system that the cytogeneticists glimpsed is the *general case* and the PAR is the limiting case of a continuum. A consequence: every analytical convention developed for PARs — masking, ploidy switching, explicit boundary annotation — has a genome-wide analogue waiting at the other 13 communities.

The continuity of pattern across four decades — from cosmid FISH on tens of individuals to pangenome alignment on hundreds — is itself the strongest validation that what the cytogeneticists saw was real. The contribution of the present work is not the discovery of subtelomeric exchange; it is the *quantification* of the exchange system at population scale, with sequence resolution, a unifying community structure, and convergent 3D evidence.
